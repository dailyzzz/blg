<h2 id="the-horse-grease-problem">The Horse Grease Problem</h2>
<h3 id="jenners-own-admission">Jenner's Own Admission</h3>
<p>In his original writings, Edward Jenner did not claim that cowpox alone provided immunity to smallpox. He wrote extensively about "grease" — a disease of horses characterized by oozing, pustular lesions on the horses' heels. Jenner believed that grease was transferred from horses to cows (via the hands of farmworkers), where it became cowpox, which then provided immunity to smallpox.</p>
<p>This chain — horse grease to cowpox to smallpox immunity — was Jenner's actual theory. It was not the clean "milkmaid had cowpox, cowpox prevents smallpox" narrative taught in schools. The horse was central to Jenner's thinking, and the material he used for vaccination may have come directly from horses rather than from cowpox lesions on milkmaids.</p>
<h3 id="what-is-horse-grease">What Is Horse Grease?</h3>
<p>Horse grease (more properly called "grease heel" or "scratches") is a dermatological condition affecting horses' lower legs. It produces:</p>
<ul>
<li>Oozing, suppurating lesions</li>
<li>Crusty, scabrous tissue</li>
<li>Foul-smelling discharge</li>
<li>Pain and lameness</li>
</ul>
<p>The condition is caused by a combination of bacterial infection, fungal infection, and poor hygiene in the stable environment. It is not caused by a specific virus. The material from grease lesions would have contained a complex mixture of bacteria, fungi, dead tissue, and inflammatory fluid — not a single viral agent.</p>
<p>When Jenner or his followers used this material for vaccination, they were injecting patients with a cocktail of equine pathogens and inflammatory material. Any "immune response" produced by such an injection would have been a response to the foreign material itself — not a targeted response to a specific virus.</p>
