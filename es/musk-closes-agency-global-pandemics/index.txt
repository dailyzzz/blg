<h2 id="what-actually-happened">What Actually Happened</h2>
<h3 id="the-agency-and-its-functions">The Agency and Its Functions</h3>
<p>The agency in question has served as the primary US government vehicle for funding pandemic preparedness — including vaccine development, medical countermeasure stockpiling, and biosurveillance infrastructure. It has channeled billions of dollars to pharmaceutical companies, research institutions, and international organizations under the mandate of protecting Americans from pandemic threats.</p>
<p>This agency also served as a critical node in the infrastructure that produced and distributed mRNA injections during the COVID-19 crisis. It facilitated the Emergency Use Authorization process, funded the accelerated development timeline, and managed the contracts that guaranteed pharmaceutical companies billions in revenue while shielding them from liability. In this sense, the agency was complicit in the largest mass deployment of experimental biotechnology in human history.</p>

<p>However, the efficiency argument obscures the more complex reality. The agency's closure does not eliminate its functions — it redistributes them. Pandemic preparedness does not cease because an agency closes. The question is where those functions go: to other agencies, to private contractors, to military programs, or to international organizations. Each alternative carries its own risks and reduces different forms of accountability.</p>
