<h2 id="the-ohio-pyramids-americas-forbidden-monuments">The Ohio Pyramids: America's Forbidden Monuments</h2>
<h3 id="the-newark-earthworks-and-beyond">The Newark Earthworks and Beyond</h3>
<p>In the rolling hills of central Ohio lies one of the most significant — and most deliberately ignored — archaeological complexes in the Western Hemisphere. The Newark Earthworks, spanning over four square miles, feature geometric precision that rivals the Great Pyramid of Giza. Perfect circles, squares, and octagonal enclosures were constructed with mathematical relationships that modern surveyors struggle to replicate. Yet mainstream academia dismisses these as simple "burial mounds" built by primitive hunter-gatherers.</p>
<p>The Great Circle Earthwork in Newark measures exactly 1,200 feet in diameter with walls 8 to 14 feet high. The Octagon Earthwork connects to a perfect circle and aligns with the 18.6-year lunar cycle with such precision that archaeoastronomer Ray Hively and philosopher Robert Horn of Earlham College confirmed the alignments in the 1980s — only to see their findings buried in obscure academic journals, never reaching public consciousness.</p>
<p>But the earthworks at Newark are only the beginning. Throughout Ohio, pyramidal mounds dot the landscape in patterns that suggest a unified civilization of enormous sophistication. The Miamisburg Mound, standing 65 feet tall, is the largest conical mound in Ohio. The Serpent Mound in Adams County stretches over 1,300 feet and aligns with astronomical events that presuppose advanced knowledge of celestial mechanics. These are not the works of scattered tribes; they are the remnants of an organized civilization that the establishment has worked tirelessly to erase.</p>
