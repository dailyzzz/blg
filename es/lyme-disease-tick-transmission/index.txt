<h2 id="the-official-lyme-disease-story">The Official Lyme Disease Story</h2>
<h3 id="lyme-connecticut-1975">Lyme, Connecticut, 1975</h3>
<p>The official story begins in 1975, when a cluster of children in Lyme, Connecticut, developed what appeared to be juvenile rheumatoid arthritis. The mothers of these children, suspecting something unusual, contacted the Connecticut State Department of Health. Researcher Allen Steere from Yale University investigated and eventually, in 1982, Willy Burgdorfer identified the spirochete bacterium in deer ticks, which was named Borrelia burgdorferi in his honor.</p>
<p>This narrative is clean, linear, and satisfying. It is also deeply suspicious.</p>

<h2 id="the-transmission-studies-that-dont-add-up">The Transmission Studies That Don't Add Up</h2>
<h3 id="the-tick-bite-problem">The Tick Bite Problem</h3>
<p>The official narrative claims that Borrelia burgdorferi is transmitted through the bite of an infected Ixodes tick (deer tick). But the transmission studies are riddled with problems:</p>
<p><strong>Attachment Duration</strong>: The CDC claims that a tick must be attached for 36-48 hours to transmit the spirochete. But multiple studies have documented Lyme disease in patients who removed ticks within hours of attachment, or who have no memory of a tick bite at all. Some studies estimate that 25-50% of Lyme patients never recall a tick bite.</p>
<p><strong>The Bull's-Eye Rash Myth</strong>: The erythema migrans rash — the characteristic "bull's-eye" rash — is considered the hallmark sign of Lyme disease. But studies show that only 70-80% of Lyme patients develop this rash, and its appearance varies so widely that it is an unreliable diagnostic criterion. Many patients with confirmed Borrelia infections never develop the rash, while some patients develop similar rashes from other causes.</p>
<p><strong>Geographic Anomalies</strong>: If Lyme disease is truly transmitted by deer ticks, one would expect the disease to be concentrated in areas with the highest tick populations. But the geographic distribution of Lyme disease cases does not perfectly correlate with tick distribution. Some areas with heavy tick populations have low Lyme disease rates, and vice versa.</p>
<p><strong>Animal Reservoir Questions</strong>: Deer are blamed as the primary reservoir for infected ticks, leading to campaigns to cull deer populations. But studies have shown that deer are actually <strong>not</strong> competent reservoirs for Borrelia burgdorferi — the spirochete does not survive well in deer blood. White-footed mice are considered the actual reservoir, yet the public narrative continues to focus on deer. Why? Because the deer narrative is simpler and keeps attention away from more uncomfortable questions.</p>
