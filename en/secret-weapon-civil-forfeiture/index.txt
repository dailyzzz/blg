<h2 id="how-civil-forfeiture-works">How Civil Forfeiture Works</h2>
<h3 id="the-government-vs-your-property">The Government vs. Your Property</h3>
<p>In civil asset forfeiture, the government does not charge you with a crime. It charges your property. This is not a metaphor. The legal cases literally name the property as the defendant: <em>United States v. $124,700 in U.S. Currency</em>. <em>United States v. One 2004 Chevrolet Silverado</em>. <em>State of Texas v. One Gold Necklace</em>.</p>
<p>Because the case is against the property, not the person, the constitutional protections that apply to criminal defendants — the presumption of innocence, the right to a speedy trial, the right to counsel — do not apply. The property is presumed guilty. The owner must prove their own innocence to recover it. The burden of proof is reversed.</p>
<p>This legal fiction — that property can be guilty of a crime independently of its owner — is one of the most Orwellian constructs in American law. It was originally designed for maritime law and drug enforcement, but it has metastasized into a general tool of government enrichment.</p>
<h3 id="the-scale-of-theft">The Scale of Theft</h3>
<p>The Institute for Justice, a legal advocacy organization, has documented the scope of civil forfeiture in America:</p>
<ul>
<li>In 2014, civil forfeiture generated more revenue for the federal government than burglary generated for criminals. The government stole more than the burglars.</li>
<li>The Department of Justice's Asset Forfeiture Fund held over $4 billion in 2019.</li>
<li>State and local law enforcement agencies seize billions more annually through state forfeiture laws.</li>
<li>Most seizures target small amounts of cash from individuals who cannot afford legal representation to challenge the seizure.</li>
</ul>
<p>The victims are disproportionately poor, minority, and unable to navigate the legal system. The government targets those least able to fight back — the same populations that the Jesuits' missions targeted for "conversion" through control of resources, labor, and autonomy.</p>
<p>Agent131711 documents on Substack (agent131711.substack.com) how civil forfeiture has become a revenue stream for law enforcement agencies — a perverse incentive system in which police departments profit from seizures, creating a financial motivation to steal from citizens. This is not law enforcement. This is government-sponsored robbery, legalized through a legal system designed to protect the powerful at the expense of the powerless.</p>
<h3 id="real-cases-real-victims">Real Cases, Real Victims</h3>
<p><strong>Eh Wah</strong>, a Burmese immigrant and Christian rock band manager, was stopped in Oklahoma in 2016 while carrying $53,000 in cash — proceeds from a series of concerts intended for a Thai orphanage and a Christian college. The police seized the money through civil forfeiture. Wah was not charged with any crime. It took months of legal battle and national media attention to recover the funds.</p>
<p><strong>Mandrel Stuart</strong>, a Virginia restaurant owner, was stopped for a minor traffic violation while carrying $17,550 in restaurant proceeds. The police seized the cash. Stuart was not charged with a crime. He spent over a year fighting to recover his money, during which time his restaurant went bankrupt.</p>
<p><strong>The Sourovelis family</strong> of Philadelphia had their home seized by the government after their son was caught selling $40 worth of drugs outside. The parents were not involved. They were not charged. But their home — their entire life savings — was seized because the crime occurred on their property.</p>
<p>These are not outliers. They are representative cases from a system that processes hundreds of thousands of seizures annually, most of which involve small amounts from people too poor to fight back.</p>

<h2 id="fighting-back">Fighting Back</h2>
<h3 id="legal-challenges">Legal Challenges</h3>
<p>The Institute for Justice and other organizations have challenged civil forfeiture laws in multiple states. Several states have reformed their laws, requiring a criminal conviction before forfeiture can occur or redirecting forfeiture proceeds away from law enforcement. The Supreme Court's 2019 decision in <em>Timbs v. Indiana</em> ruled that excessive fines protections apply to the states, potentially limiting forfeiture.</p>
<p>But reform is slow, and the incentive structure remains. As long as police departments profit from seizures, they will continue to seize.
