<h2 id="the-ancient-pattern">The Ancient Pattern</h2>

<p><em>"And have no fellowship with the unfruitful works of darkness, but rather reprove them."</em> — Ephesians 5:11</p>

Abraham Lincoln, the sixteenth president of the United States, and Napoleon Bonaparte, the Emperor of the French, both identified the Society of Jesus as the hidden force behind the wars, revolutions, and assassinations that shaped their eras. They came from different nations, different centuries, and different political traditions. They agreed on almost nothing. But on the identity of the enemy operating in the shadows, they spoke with one voice.</p>
<p>The documentary <em>Catholic Confidential</em> compiles their testimony alongside the testimony of other witnesses who reached the same conclusion independently. The convergence of these independent witnesses — men who had no communication with each other, no shared agenda, no common prejudice — constitutes evidence of extraordinary weight. When a French emperor and an American president, separated by half a century, name the same enemy using nearly identical language, the rational response is not skepticism but attention.</p>

<h2 id="lincolns-war-the-jesuits-and-the-american-civil-war">Lincoln's War: The Jesuits and the American Civil War</h2>
<h3 id="the-presidents-accusation">The President's Accusation</h3>
<p>Abraham Lincoln did not merely suggest Jesuit involvement in the Civil War. He stated it as a matter of fact, with the authority of a president who had access to the most comprehensive intelligence available to any person in America:</p>
<p><em>"This war, the American Civil War, would never have been possible without the sinister influence of the Jesuits. We owe it to Popery that we now see our land reddened with the blood of her noblest sons."</em></p>
<p>The Civil War killed over 600,000 Americans — more than all other American wars combined until the twentieth century. Lincoln attributed this catastrophe not to slavery alone, not to political disagreement, not to economic competition between North and South, but to "the sinister influence of the Jesuits." He held "Popery" — the papal system and its Jesuit instrument — responsible for the blood that reddened American soil.</p>
<p>This is not the language of casual suspicion. This is the formal accusation of the president of the United States, made during the most devastating war in American history, identifying a specific organization as the force that made the war possible. Lincoln had access to military intelligence, diplomatic correspondence, and counterintelligence reports. His accusation was not based on prejudice. It was based on evidence.</p>
<h3 id="the-confederate-connection">The Confederate Connection</h3>
<p>Lincoln was specific about the mechanism of Jesuit influence on the Confederacy:</p>
<p><em>"Neither Jeff Davis nor any one of the leading men of the Confederacy would have dared to attack the North had they not relied on the promise of the Jesuits."</em></p>
<p>Jefferson Davis and the Confederate leadership, according to Lincoln, were not acting independently. They were relying on promises from the Jesuits — promises of support, promises of European intervention, promises of resources and assistance. The Confederacy did not wage war on its own initiative. It waged war because the Jesuits encouraged it, supported it, and promised it the backing it needed to succeed.</p>
<p>The historical evidence supports Lincoln's assessment. The Confederacy desperately sought European recognition and intervention. The Catholic monarchies of Europe — France under Napoleon III and Austria under the Habsburgs, both deeply influenced by Jesuit advisors — were the most sympathetic to the Confederate cause. Napoleon III actually installed a Catholic emperor, Maximilian, in Mexico during the Civil War, creating a Catholic monarchy on America's southern border. This was not coincidence. It was coordination.</p>
<h3 id="the-immigration-of-agents">The Immigration of Agents</h3>
<p>Lincoln understood the broader context of the Jesuit strategy in America:</p>
<p><em>"The Protestants of both the North and South would surely unite to exterminate the priests and the Jesuits if they could learn how the priests, nuns, and monks which daily land on our shores, under the pretext of preaching their religion, are nothing else but the emissaries of the Pope, of Napoleon III, and the other despots of Europe, to undermine our institutions, alienate the hearts of our people from our Constitution and our laws, destroy our schools, and prepare a reign of anarchy."</em></p>
<p>Lincoln is describing a covert invasion. The "priests, nuns, and monks" arriving in America were not primarily missionaries. They were "emissaries" — agents of the Pope, Napoleon III, and European despotism. Their mission was not to preach the gospel but to "undermine our institutions," "alienate the hearts of our people from our Constitution," "destroy our schools," and "prepare a reign of anarchy."</p>
<p>Every element of this description has been fulfilled. American institutions have been undermined — systematically, from within, over generations. The Constitution has been progressively reinterpreted to mean something its framers would not recognize. The public school system, which was designed to produce informed citizens capable of self-governance, has been transformed into a system that produces compliant consumers incapable of critical thought. And the anarchy that Lincoln predicted — the social disintegration, the cultural collapse, the loss of shared values and common purpose — is the defining reality of the contemporary American experience.</p>
<h3 id="the-assassination-foretold">The Assassination Foretold</h3>
<p>Lincoln's awareness of the personal danger he faced was constant and specific:</p>
<p><em>"New projects of assassination are detected almost every day, accompanied with such savage circumstances that they bring to my memory the massacre of St. Bartholomew and the Gunpowder Plot."</em></p>
<p>The reference to St. Bartholomew — the 1572 massacre of French Protestants orchestrated by Catholic forces — and the Gunpowder Plot — the 1605 Jesuit conspiracy to blow up the English Parliament — reveals Lincoln's understanding that the threats against him were not random but part of a historical pattern. The Jesuits had a centuries-long history of assassinating leaders who opposed them. Lincoln saw himself as the next target in that pattern.</p>
<p>His assessment of the Jesuit character confirmed this understanding:</p>
<p><em>"I know that Jesuits never forget nor forsake."</em></p>
<p>Seven words that serve as his own epitaph. The Jesuits never forget a target. They never abandon a mission. Lincoln had identified them publicly as the force behind the Civil War. He had named their strategy. He had warned the American people. And he knew — with the certainty of a man who understood his enemies — that they would never stop trying to silence him.</p>
<p>On April 14, 1865, they succeeded. Abraham Lincoln was shot at Ford's Theatre by John Wilkes Booth. The official narrative treats the assassination as the act of a Confederate sympathizer. Lincoln's own testimony — the testimony of the man who was killed — identifies a different sponsor.</p>
<h2 id="napoleons-analysis-the-most-absolute-of-despotisms">Napoleon's Analysis: "The Most Absolute of Despotisms"</h2>
<h3 id="the-emperors-military-assessment">The Emperor's Military Assessment</h3>
<p>Napoleon Bonaparte analyzed the Jesuits not as a theologian but as a military strategist — a man who understood power, organization, and the mechanics of conquest better than almost anyone in history:</p>
<p><em>"Jesuitism is the most absolute of despotisms and at the same time the greatest and most enormous of abuses."</em></p>
<p>Napoleon had encountered despotism in every form. He had fought against monarchies, republics, and empires. He had overthrown the French ancien regime and created his own empire. He had seen every form of political power that Europe could produce. And he concluded that Jesuitism was "the most absolute of despotisms" — more absolute than any monarchy, more complete than any dictatorship, more total than any political system he had encountered.</p>
<p>The word "abuse" is equally significant. Jesuitism is not merely a despotism. It is "the greatest and most enormous of abuses" — an abuse of religion, an abuse of trust, an abuse of the human need for God. The Jesuits exploit the most sacred dimension of human experience — the desire for meaning, for salvation, for connection with the divine — as an instrument of earthly power. This is not merely political tyranny. It is spiritual tyranny — the worst form of abuse because it corrupts the deepest part of the human soul.</p>
<h3 id="the-general-above-the-sovereign">The General Above the Sovereign</h3>
<p>Napoleon identified the specific mechanism of Jesuit political control:</p>
<p><em>"The general of the Jesuits insists on being master sovereign over the sovereign."</em></p>
<p>This single sentence explains the Jesuit relationship to every government in history. The Jesuits do not seek to replace the sovereign. They seek to master the sovereign. The king remains on his throne. The president remains in his office. The prime minister remains in his chamber. But above each of them stands the Jesuit General — unseen, unnamed, unelected, and unaccountable — directing the decisions that the sovereign believes are his own.</p>
<p>Napoleon understood this because he had experienced it. Despite his enormous personal power, despite his military genius, despite his ability to reshape the map of Europe at will, Napoleon found the Jesuit influence to be a force he could not entirely control or eliminate. The Jesuits had been suppressed by the pope in 1773 — before Napoleon's rise — but they had survived in Russia and continued to operate through front organizations and allied networks throughout Napoleon's empire.</p>
<h3 id="the-doctrine-of-meritorious-crime">The Doctrine of Meritorious Crime</h3>
<p>Napoleon identified the moral framework that makes the Jesuit system so dangerous:</p>
<p><em>"Every act, every crime, however atrocious, is a meritorious work if committed for the interest of the Society of the Jesuits or by the order of the General."</em></p>
<p>This is the doctrine that transforms a religious order into a criminal organization of unlimited scope. When the General commands murder, murder becomes a "meritorious work." When the General commands treason, treason becomes an act of obedience to God. When the General commands the destruction of a nation, the destruction becomes a holy offering. There is no crime so atrocious that it cannot be sanctified by the General's order.</p>
<p>This doctrine explains the Jesuit involvement in assassinations, wars, revolutions, and destabilization campaigns throughout their history. The agents who carry out these operations are not troubled by conscience because their conscience has been replaced by obedience. They do not experience guilt because guilt requires an independent moral judgment, and independent judgment has been abolished. They commit atrocities with the serene confidence of men performing religious duty — because that is exactly what they believe they are doing.</p>
<h2 id="paolo-sarpi-the-plague-of-the-world">Paolo Sarpi: "The Plague of the World"</h2>
<h3 id="the-venetian-scholars-indictment">The Venetian Scholar's Indictment</h3>
<p>Paolo Sarpi, the great Venetian scholar and theologian of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, provided one of the most comprehensive indictments of the Jesuit system:</p>
<p><em>"They are a public plague and the plague of the world. From the Jesuit colleges there never is sent a pupil obedient to his father, devoted to his country, loyal to his prince. Every species of vice finds its patronage in them."</em></p>
<p>Sarpi's assessment strikes at the heart of the Jesuit educational project. The Jesuit colleges — which were among the finest educational institutions in Europe — did not produce good citizens. They produced Jesuit agents. A pupil who entered a Jesuit college obedient to his father left obedient to the Society. A pupil who entered devoted to his country left devoted to the Jesuit mission. A pupil who entered loyal to his prince left loyal to the General.</p>
<p>The phrase "every species of vice finds its patronage in them" is a damning summary of the moral consequences of the Jesuit system. When obedience replaces conscience, when the General's command replaces God's law, when any crime becomes meritorious on the order of the Superior — then there is no vice that is not patronized, no sin that is not sanctioned, no evil that is not blessed by the institution.</p>
<h2 id="general-harris-on-the-assassination-strategy">General Harris on the Assassination Strategy</h2>
<h3 id="the-assassination-as-jesuit-policy">The Assassination as Jesuit Policy</h3>
<p>General Harris provided a specific assessment of the Jesuit role in Lincoln's assassination:</p>
<p><em>"It would seem that the Jesuits had had it in mind from the beginning of the war to find an occasion for the taking off [assassination] of Mr. Abraham Lincoln. The favorite policy of the Jesuits is that of assassination."</em></p>
<p>"The favorite policy" — not an occasional resort, not a last-resort measure, but the favored method. Assassination is the Jesuit's preferred instrument of political change. When a leader opposes Jesuit interests, the first option is not negotiation, not compromise, not political pressure — it is assassination. The Jesuits kill their enemies. This is not speculation. <p>Harris places the decision to assassinate Lincoln at "the beginning of the war" — not as a response to Confederate defeat but as part of the original war plan. Lincoln was targeted not because the South was losing but because Lincoln was winning — and because his victory would expose the Jesuit role in starting the war. The assassination was not revenge. It was damage control.</p>
<h2 id="charles-chiniquy-the-prophecy-fulfilled">Charles Chiniquy: The Prophecy Fulfilled</h2>
<h3 id="the-priest-who-warned-lincoln">The Priest Who Warned Lincoln</h3>
<p>Charles Chiniquy was a Catholic priest who left the Church and became one of the most vocal critics of Jesuit power in America. His personal relationship with Abraham Lincoln — whom he had known before Lincoln became president — gave him unique access to Lincoln's thinking about the Jesuit threat. Chiniquy recorded Lincoln's words and added his own prophetic assessment:</p>
<p><em>"The day is very near when the Jesuits will rule their country, from the magnificent White House at Washington to the humblest civil and military department of this vast republic."</em></p>
<p>This prophecy, made in the nineteenth century, describes the twenty-first century with uncomfortable precision. Jesuit-educated individuals occupy positions at every level of American government — from the White House (multiple presidents have been Jesuit-educated or Jesuit-influenced) to "the humblest civil and military department." The Jesuit educational network has produced a governing class that, whether consciously or unconsciously, serves the Jesuit agenda.</p>
<p>Chiniquy's warning was not about distant future. He said "the day is very near." And he was right. Within a generation of his warning, Jesuit institutions were established in every major American city. Within two generations, Jesuit-educated individuals were occupying positions of significant power. Within three generations, the Jesuit influence in American government, education, media, and culture was so pervasive that it was invisible — part of the background noise of American life, unnoticed because it was everywhere.</p>
<h2 id="pope-clement-xiv-the-pope-who-paid-the-price">Pope Clement XIV: The Pope Who Paid the Price</h2>
<h3 id="the-suppression-of-1773">The Suppression of 1773</h3>
<p>The suppression of the Jesuit order by Pope Clement XIV in 1773 is one of the most significant events in modern history. The pope — the supreme authority of the Catholic Church, the man the Jesuits were theoretically sworn to obey — concluded that the Society of Jesus was so dangerous that it had to be dissolved entirely.</p>
<p>The bull <em>Dominus ac Redemptor</em> was the product of years of pressure from the Catholic monarchs of Europe. Portugal had expelled the Jesuits in 1759. France had expelled them in 1764. Spain had expelled them in 1767. Every major Catholic kingdom had concluded, independently, that the Jesuits were a threat to national sovereignty and public order. Clement XIV, under enormous diplomatic pressure, finally agreed.</p>
<p>His assessment was prescient: <em>"The suppression is accomplished. I do not repent of it... But this suppression will be my death."</em></p>
<p>On September 22, 1774, Clement XIV was dead. The rapid decomposition of his body — a hallmark of poisoning in that era — pointed to foul play. The pope who suppressed the Jesuits was murdered by the organization he suppressed.</p>
<h3 id="the-restoration-and-the-holy-alliance">The Restoration and the Holy Alliance</h3>
<p>The Jesuit order was restored by Pope Pius VII in 1814 — forty-one years after its suppression. The restoration came in the aftermath of the Napoleonic Wars, during a period of political realignment that the Jesuits exploited masterfully.</p>
<p>The Holy Alliance of 1815, formed by Russia, Austria, and Prussia after Napoleon's defeat, was ostensibly a pact to maintain Christian principles in European governance. In reality, it was a mechanism for suppressing democratic movements and maintaining the conservative order that served Jesuit interests. The Alliance committed its signatories to oppose revolution, democracy, and popular sovereignty — the very principles that threatened Jesuit control.</p>
<p>The suppression had lasted only forty-one years. During that time, the Jesuits had survived in Russia under Catherine the Great's protection, maintained their networks through front organizations, and waited patiently for the political moment that would allow their restoration. When that moment came, they returned — stronger, wiser, and more determined than ever.</p>
