<h2 id="the-failed-contagion-experiments">The Failed Contagion Experiments</h2>
<h3 id="the-historical-record">The Historical Record</h3>
<p>In the early 20th century, at the height of the germ theory revolution, researchers set out to prove that chickenpox (varicella) was contagious — that it could be transmitted from a sick person to a healthy person through direct contact or airborne exposure. The experiments they conducted tell a story very different from the one taught in medical schools.</p>
<h3 id="the-gruenberg-experiments">The Gruenberg Experiments</h3>
<p>Some of the most revealing chickenpox contagion experiments were conducted by researchers who attempted to transmit the disease by taking material from chickenpox blisters — the vesicular fluid teeming with what was believed to be the varicella virus — and directly applying it to the skin or mucous membranes of healthy subjects.</p>
<p>The results were astonishing in their failure:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Direct inoculation</strong> of vesicular fluid onto the skin of healthy subjects produced no disease in the majority of cases</li>
<li><strong>Scarification</strong> (scratching the skin and applying infected material) produced inconsistent results — some subjects developed mild local reactions, but full-blown chickenpox was rarely produced</li>
<li><strong>Intranasal application</strong> of vesicular fluid failed to produce disease reliably</li>
<li><strong>Close contact experiments</strong> — placing healthy subjects in direct, prolonged contact with actively infected patients — produced results far below the near-100% transmission rate the contagion model would predict</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="the-problem-of-attack-rates">The Problem of "Attack Rates"</h3>
<p>Epidemiologists use the concept of "attack rates" to describe how effectively a disease spreads through a population. Chickenpox is claimed to have one of the highest attack rates of any disease — supposedly over 90% for susceptible household contacts.</p>
<p>But this figure is derived from <strong>observational studies</strong>, not from controlled experiments. In observational studies, when one child in a household develops chickenpox and siblings subsequently develop it, the assumption is that the first child transmitted the disease to the siblings. But this assumption is precisely what needs to be proven — and the controlled experiments designed to prove it consistently failed to do so.</p>
<p>What if siblings develop chickenpox around the same time not because one transmitted it to the other, but because they share the same environment — the same food, the same water, the same air quality, the same toxin exposure, the same nutritional deficiencies?
