<h2 id="the-real-edward-jenner">The Real Edward Jenner</h2>
<h3 id="not-a-doctor-not-a-scientist">Not a Doctor, Not a Scientist</h3>
<p>Edward Jenner was not a trained physician in the modern sense. He obtained his medical degree from the University of St Andrews in Scotland — a degree purchased for the sum of 15 guineas, without examination, without attending lectures, and without clinical experience. It was, by the standards of his own time, a bought credential.</p>
<p>Jenner was a naturalist and a country surgeon. His interest in cowpox and smallpox was based on local folklore — the folk belief that milkmaids who had cowpox were immune to smallpox. He did not conduct rigorous scientific investigation. He collected anecdotes, performed a handful of crude experiments, and published his findings in a self-published pamphlet after the Royal Society rejected his paper.</p>
<h3 id="the-experiment-on-james-phipps">The Experiment on James Phipps</h3>
<p>On May 14, 1796, Jenner took material from a cowpox lesion on the hand of a milkmaid named Sarah Nelmes and scratched it into the arm of an eight-year-old boy named James Phipps — the son of Jenner's gardener. He later inoculated Phipps with smallpox material to test whether the boy was immune.</p>
<p>This experiment was conducted:</p>
<ul>
<li>On a <strong>child</strong> who could not give informed consent</li>
<li>By a man with a <strong>purchased medical degree</strong></li>

<li>Without any understanding of <strong>germ theory</strong>, virology, or immunology</li>
<li>With <strong>no ethical oversight</strong>, no peer review, and no safety protocols</li>
</ul>
<p>By modern medical ethics standards, this experiment would be considered criminal. By the standards of its own time, it was considered reckless by many of Jenner's contemporaries. But the narrative it produced — the heroic doctor saving humanity from plague — was too useful to be questioned.</p>
