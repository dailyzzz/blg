<h2 id="john-adams-eternal-damnation-on-earth-and-in-hell">John Adams: "Eternal Damnation on Earth and in Hell"</h2>
<h3 id="the-founding-fathers-warning">The Founding Father's Warning</h3>
<p>John Adams, the second President of the United States, was one of the most learned men in American history. A lawyer, diplomat, and political philosopher, Adams devoted his life to the study of government, power, and the forces that threaten liberty. His private correspondence reveals a man who understood the Jesuit threat with extraordinary clarity.</p>
<p>In an 1816 letter to Thomas Jefferson — a letter between two former presidents, two men who had led the American Revolution and shaped the new republic — Adams wrote with an urgency that is still startling two centuries later:</p>
<p><em>"My history of the Jesuits is not eloquently written, but it is supported by unquestionable authorities, is very particular and very horrible. Their restoration is indeed a step toward darkness, cruelty, despotism, death."</em></p>
<p>Adams was referring to the restoration of the Jesuit order by Pope Pius VII in 1814, just two years before this letter was written. The order had been suppressed by Pope Clement XIV in 1773 — suppressed by the pope himself, the man who was supposed to be their supreme authority. And now it had been restored. Adams saw this restoration not as a minor ecclesiastical event but as a civilizational catastrophe — "a step toward darkness, cruelty, despotism, death."</p>
<p>His assessment of the Jesuits themselves was even more devastating:</p>
<p><em>"I do not like the appearance of the Jesuits. If ever there were a body of men who merited eternal damnation on earth and in hell, it is this Society of Loyolas."</em></p>
<p>This is not the language of sectarian prejudice. This is the considered judgment of one of the most careful minds in American history, a man who weighed his words with lawyerly precision. Adams did not say the Jesuits were misguided. He did not say they were overenthusiastic. He said they merited eternal damnation — on earth and in hell. He used the strongest possible language because he believed the threat justified it.</p>
<h3 id="the-warning-of-disguise">The Warning of Disguise</h3>
<p>Adams went further, predicting the method by which the Jesuits would infiltrate America:</p>
<p><em>"Shall we not have regular swarms of them here in as many disguises as only a king of the gypsies can assume? Dressed as painters, publishers, writers, and school masters."</em></p>
<p>Two centuries later, this prediction has been fulfilled with astonishing precision. The Jesuits did not come to America in clerical robes demanding submission. They came as educators — founding Georgetown University in 1789, the same year the Constitution was ratified. They came as publishers, writers, and intellectuals. They came in every disguise that Adams predicted, and they established themselves so thoroughly that today, Jesuit institutions are among the most prestigious and influential in the nation.</p>
<p>Adams saw all of this coming. He warned Jefferson. He warned the nation. And the nation did not listen.</p>
<h2 id="napoleon-bonaparte-power-to-control-the-world">Napoleon Bonaparte: "Power to Control the World"</h2>
<h3 id="the-emperors-assessment">The Emperor's Assessment</h3>
<p>Napoleon Bonaparte was not a theologian. He was a military genius, a political strategist, and the master of an empire that stretched from Spain to Russia. His assessment of the Jesuits was not based on religious prejudice but on military analysis — the analysis of a man who understood power better than almost anyone who has ever lived:</p>
<p><em>"The Jesuits are a military organization, not a religious order. Their chief is a general of an army, not the mere father abbot of a monastery. And the aim of this organization is power — absolute power, universal power, power to control the world by the volition of a single man."</em></p>
<p>Napoleon understood what most people miss: the Society of Jesus is not primarily a religious organization. It is a military organization that uses religion as its cover. Its structure is military — a general commanding officers who command soldiers. Its discipline is military — absolute obedience, unquestioning execution of orders. Its strategy is military — infiltration, intelligence gathering, manipulation of adversaries, and the patient accumulation of decisive advantage.</p>
<p>Napoleon's further observations were equally penetrating:</p>
<p><em>"The general of the Jesuits insists on being master sovereign over the sovereign."</em></p>
<p>This single sentence encapsulates the Jesuit political strategy. They do not seek to replace the king. They seek to control the king. They do not want the throne. They want the ear of the man on the throne. The general of the Jesuits insists on being the master of the master — the sovereign above the sovereign, the power behind every visible power.</p>
<p>And Napoleon concluded with a warning that echoes through the centuries:</p>
<p><em>"Wherever the Jesuits are admitted, they will be masters, cost what it may."</em></p>
<p>The cost, as history would demonstrate, would include the lives of presidents, the freedom of nations, and the souls of millions.</p>
<h3 id="the-pattern-napoleon-recognized">The Pattern Napoleon Recognized</h3>
<p>Napoleon's military mind recognized a pattern that civilian observers often miss. The Jesuits do not operate like a church. They operate like a conquering army. They identify strategic objectives — universities, courts, government offices, media institutions. They deploy personnel to those objectives. They establish positions, build networks, and gradually expand their control until the objective is secured. Then they move to the next objective.</p>
<p>The difference between the Jesuits and a conventional army is that the Jesuits never declare war. There is no battle. There is no conquest that anyone can point to and say, "There — that is the moment they took control." The takeover is invisible, gradual, and total. By the time anyone realizes what has happened, the Jesuits are already in control, and the structures of resistance have been infiltrated and neutralized.</p>
<h2 id="abraham-lincoln-the-sinister-influence-of-the-jesuits">Abraham Lincoln: "The Sinister Influence of the Jesuits"</h2>
<h3 id="the-president-who-named-his-assassins">The President Who Named His Assassins</h3>
<p>Abraham Lincoln is remembered as the president who preserved the Union and freed the slaves. What is not remembered — what has been systematically suppressed — is that Lincoln identified the Jesuits as the force behind the Civil War and predicted his own assassination at their hands.</p>
<p>Lincoln's words, as documented in <em>Catholic Confidential</em> and preserved in historical records, are unambiguous:</p>
<p><em>"This war would never have been possible without the sinister influence of the Jesuits."</em></p>
<p>The American Civil War — the bloodiest conflict in American history, a war that killed over 600,000 men — was not, according to the sitting president of the United States, a natural result of political disagreement. It was engineered. It was made possible by the "sinister influence" of a specific organization. Lincoln did not attribute the war to slavery alone, or to states' rights, or to economic differences between North and South. He attributed it to the deliberate machinations of the Society of Jesus.</p>
<p>Lincoln elaborated on this assessment with extraordinary specificity:</p>
<p><em>"Neither Jeff Davis nor any one of the leading men of the Confederacy would have dared to attack the North had they not relied on the promise of the Jesuits."</em></p>
<p>This is a staggering claim. The president of the United States is stating that the Confederacy was emboldened to wage war by promises from the Jesuit order — promises of support, of European intervention, of resources and assistance. The Civil War was not merely a domestic conflict. It was, according to Lincoln, part of a Jesuit strategy to divide and weaken the United States.</p>
<h3 id="the-assassination-foretold">The Assassination Foretold</h3>
<p>Lincoln was not naive about the personal danger he faced. He spoke of it repeatedly with a clarity that is heartbreaking in hindsight:</p>
<p><em>"So many plots have already been made against my life that it is a real miracle that they have all failed."</em></p>
<p>And then, with prophetic precision:</p>
<p><em>"I know that Jesuits never forget nor forsake."</em></p>
<p>Lincoln knew. He knew who was plotting against him. He knew their nature — an organization that never forgets a target and never abandons a mission. He knew that his identification of the Jesuits as the force behind the Civil War had marked him for death. And he was right. On April 14, 1865, Abraham Lincoln was assassinated at Ford's Theatre.</p>
<p>The official narrative attributes the assassination to John Wilkes Booth, a Confederate sympathizer acting on his own initiative or as part of a small conspiracy. But Lincoln himself — the man with the most complete intelligence available to any person in America — attributed the ongoing threats against his life to the Jesuits. The man who was killed named his killers before they killed him, and the nation has spent 160 years refusing to hear what he said.</p>
<h3 id="the-protestant-warning-suppressed">The Protestant Warning Suppressed</h3>
<p>Lincoln also understood the broader implications of the Jesuit strategy:</p>
<p><em>"The Protestants of both the North and South would surely unite to exterminate the priests and the Jesuits if they could learn how the priests, nuns, and monks which daily land on our shores, under the pretext of preaching their religion, are nothing else but the emissaries of the Pope, of Napoleon III, and the other despots of Europe, to undermine our institutions, alienate the hearts of our people from our Constitution and our laws, destroy our schools, and prepare a reign of anarchy."</em></p>
<p>Lincoln saw the Jesuit immigration to America not as a religious movement but as an invasion force — an army of agents arriving "under the pretext of preaching their religion" while actually working to "undermine our institutions" and "destroy our schools." He believed that if the American people understood the truth, they would unite against the threat. But they did not understand, because the truth was suppressed, and Lincoln — the man trying to tell them — was silenced by a bullet.</p>
<p>He also connected the pattern to historical precedents:</p>
<p><em>"New projects of assassination are detected almost every day, accompanied with such savage circumstances that they bring to my memory the massacre of St. Bartholomew and the Gunpowder Plot."</em></p>
<p>The St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre (1572), in which thousands of French Protestants were slaughtered, and the Gunpowder Plot (1605), in which Jesuits conspired to blow up the English Parliament — Lincoln saw these historical events as part of the same pattern that was now targeting him.</p>
<h2 id="lafayette-the-most-crafty-dangerous-enemies">Lafayette: "The Most Crafty, Dangerous Enemies"</h2>
<h3 id="the-french-heros-warning">The French Hero's Warning</h3>
<p>The Marquis de Lafayette, the French hero of the American Revolution, the man who fought alongside Washington to establish American liberty, issued his own warning about the Jesuit threat to that liberty:</p>
<p><em>"If the liberties of this country are destroyed, it will be by the subtlety of the Roman Catholic Jesuit priests, for they are the most crafty, dangerous enemies to civil and religious liberty."</em></p>
<p>Lafayette did not say the liberties of America <em>might</em> be destroyed by the Jesuits. He said <em>if</em> they are destroyed, it will be by the Jesuits. He identified them as the single most dangerous threat to everything the American Revolution had achieved — more dangerous than any foreign army, more dangerous than any domestic faction, more dangerous than any political movement.</p>
<p>The word "subtlety" is key. Lafayette understood that the Jesuit threat was not overt but covert. It would not come as an invasion but as an infiltration. It would not come as a demand for submission but as an offer of education, counsel, and friendship. The Jesuits would not destroy American liberty by attacking it directly. They would destroy it by subtly, gradually, invisibly undermining the foundations until the structure collapsed — and then stepping in to build something new on the ruins.</p>
<h2 id="pope-clement-xiv-this-suppression-will-be-my-death">Pope Clement XIV: "This Suppression Will Be My Death"</h2>
<h3 id="when-even-the-pope-was-not-safe">When Even the Pope Was Not Safe</h3>
<p>The most extraordinary witness against the Jesuits is the pope who tried to stop them. Pope Clement XIV, after years of pressure from the Catholic monarchs of Europe — all of whom had already expelled the Jesuits from their territories — signed the papal bull <em>Dominus ac Redemptor</em> on July 21, 1773, officially suppressing the Society of Jesus.</p>
<p>It was the most dramatic act of papal authority in modern history. The pope — the supreme head of the Catholic Church — declared that the order founded to serve the papacy had become so dangerous that it must be dissolved entirely. Every Jesuit house was to be closed. Every Jesuit was to be released from his vows. The Society of Jesus was to cease to exist.</p>
<p>Clement knew what this act would cost him. He said:</p>
<p><em>"The suppression is accomplished. I do not repent of it... But this suppression will be my death."</em></p>
<p>He was right. On September 22, 1774, Pope Clement XIV died. The circumstances of his death pointed to poisoning. His body decomposed with extraordinary rapidity — a sign, in that era, of poison. The man who had dared to suppress the Jesuits paid for his courage with his life.</p>
<p>Think about what this means. The pope — the man the Jesuits were theoretically sworn to obey — was killed by the organization he tried to dissolve. The vicar of Christ, the supreme pontiff, the Bishop of Rome — murdered because he dared to challenge the power of the Society of Jesus. If the pope himself was not safe from the Jesuits, who is?</p>
<h3 id="the-restoration-and-its-meaning">The Restoration and Its Meaning</h3>
<p>Forty-one years after Clement XIV suppressed the order, Pope Pius VII restored it in 1814. The restoration was not voluntary. It was coerced — the result of political pressure from forces aligned with the Jesuits during the chaos of the Napoleonic era. The Society of Jesus had never actually ceased to exist. It survived in Russia under the protection of Catherine the Great, who refused to enforce the papal bull. It survived in other territories through various legal fictions. And when the political moment was right, it returned — stronger, wiser, and more determined than ever.</p>
<p>The suppression and restoration of the Jesuits is one of the most important events in modern history, and one of the least understood. It proves that even the highest authority in the Catholic Church — the pope himself — could not control the Society of Jesus. It proves that the Jesuits operate above and beyond any earthly authority. It proves that they regard no power — not even papal power — as superior to their own.</p>

They come from the most credible sources imaginable — presidents, emperors, generals, and popes.
