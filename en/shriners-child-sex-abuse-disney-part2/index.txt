<h2 id="the-shriners-exposed">The Shriners Exposed</h2>
<h3 id="behind-the-fez">Behind the Fez</h3>
<p>The Ancient Arabic Order of the Nobles of the Mystic Shrine was founded in 1870 by Walter M. Fleming, a Mason, and William J. Florence, an actor. The order was created as an appendant body of Freemasonry — membership requires prior initiation as a Master Mason — and it adopted Islamic-themed rituals, costumes, and terminology that drew on Orientalist fantasies of the Arabian Nights. The red fez, the scimitar, the crescent moon and star — all are Islamic symbols adopted by a Western Masonic order for purposes that have nothing to do with Islam and everything to do with the mystery school tradition's appropriation of symbols from every culture.</p>
<p>The Shriners' initiation rituals, as documented by investigators and former members, involve mock Islamic prayers, pledges to "Allah," and ceremonies that combine Masonic ritualism with pseudo-Islamic elements. The use of Islamic symbolism by a Masonic order is theologically significant — it represents the syncretic approach to religion that characterizes the entire Masonic system, in which symbols from every tradition are appropriated and reinterpreted within the Hermetic framework.</p>
<p>The requirement that Shriners be Master Masons means that every Shriner has already passed through the three degrees of Craft Masonry, accepting the Masonic worldview and swearing Masonic oaths. The Shrine adds additional layers of initiation and commitment, binding members to a brotherhood whose obligations supersede those of family, community, and faith. This layered obligation structure — borrowed from the Jesuit model of progressive commitment — ensures that members who advance far enough are thoroughly committed before they encounter the order's deeper operations.</p>

<p>The Shriners' public image as lovable philanthropists has been contradicted by a persistent pattern of criminal behavior documented across decades and jurisdictions. Investigations have revealed patterns of financial mismanagement in which charitable funds collected for children's hospitals were diverted to support members' entertainment, alcohol consumption, and personal expenses. <p>More disturbing than financial corruption are the documented cases of Shriners involved in sexual crimes against children.
