<h2 id="the-tallahassee-incident">The Tallahassee Incident</h2>
<h3 id="two-men-and-six-children">Two Men and Six Children</h3>
<p>On February 4, 1987, two well-dressed men were found in a park in Tallahassee, Florida with six disheveled, dirty children ranging in age from two to seven. The children showed signs of neglect — they were poorly clothed, hungry, and appeared unfamiliar with basic hygiene. Some showed signs of possible sexual abuse. The men, identified as Michael Houlihan and Douglas Ammerman, could not adequately explain their relationship to the children or why the children were in such condition.</p>
<p>Tallahassee police took the men into custody and began an investigation. Initial inquiries revealed that the men were associated with a group called "The Finders," based in Washington, D.C. The group was led by a man named Marion David Pettie, who had extensive connections to the intelligence community. The children's mothers were identified as members of The Finders community, but the circumstances of the children's transfer to Houlihan and Ammerman raised serious concerns about possible trafficking.</p>
<h3 id="the-investigation-begins">The Investigation Begins</h3>
<p>The Tallahassee police and the U.S. Customs Service initiated an investigation that would quickly encounter extraordinary resistance. Customs agent Ramon Martinez filed a detailed report documenting the investigation's findings and, more critically, the obstruction it faced. His report, classified for decades, was eventually released through Freedom of Information Act requests and confirmed what investigators had suspected: The Finders was connected to the Central Intelligence Agency, and the investigation was shut down on those grounds.</p>
<p>Martinez's report described the discovery of detailed instructions for obtaining children, including targeting women at specific locations, and documents relating to international travel with children. The investigation revealed connections to properties in multiple states and international locations. The scope of the operation far exceeded anything that could be attributed to a simple commune or alternative lifestyle group.</p>
<h2 id="the-washington-dc-warehouse">The Washington D.C. Warehouse</h2>
<h3 id="what-investigators-found">What Investigators Found</h3>
<p>When investigators executed search warrants on Finders properties in Washington D.C., they discovered materials that transformed the investigation from a potential neglect case into something far more disturbing. Among the items found were:</p>
<p>Detailed records of international travel involving children to multiple countries. Photographs of adults and children participating in what appeared to be ritualistic activities, including the sacrifice of animals. Computer records containing detailed instructions for the procurement and management of children. Documents indicating connections to schools, daycare centers, and international organizations. Correspondence suggesting coordination with entities in multiple countries.</p>
<p>The warehouse contained filing cabinets full of documents organized by topic, including files labeled with the names of various operations. The level of documentation was inconsistent with an informal commune — it was consistent with an organized intelligence operation maintaining detailed records of its activities.</p>
<h3 id="the-customs-report">The Customs Report</h3>
<p>Ramon Martinez's customs report, filed on February 7, 1987 and subsequently classified, described what investigators found and what happened next. According to the report, the investigation was progressing normally through law enforcement channels until the CIA connection was identified. At that point, the case was transferred from the U.S. Customs Service to the FBI — and effectively terminated.</p>
<p>Martinez wrote that he was told the case was "a CIA internal matter" and that the FBI would handle it from that point forward. The FBI's "handling" consisted of closing the investigation without prosecution. The children were returned to The Finders community. No charges were filed against any adult members of the group. The evidence gathered by Customs investigators was classified and sealed.</p>
<p>The pattern was identical to every other investigation that encountered intelligence-connected child exploitation networks: initial discovery, rapid escalation, identification of intelligence connections, transfer to a compromised agency, and termination. The track <a href="https://zzzdaily.zzzdaily.com/tracks/en/127-Operation_Midnight_Climax.mp4">Operation Midnight Climax</a> documents how the CIA has historically used human exploitation as an operational tool, and The Finders case demonstrated that this methodology extended to the exploitation of children.</p>
<h2 id="marion-david-pettie-the-man-behind-the-curtain">Marion David Pettie: The Man Behind the Curtain</h2>
<h3 id="intelligence-connections">Intelligence Connections</h3>
<p>Marion David Pettie, the founder and leader of The Finders, had an intelligence background that stretched back decades. His connections to the CIA and military intelligence were not disputed — even his wife, who worked for the CIA, acknowledged the family's intelligence connections. Pettie himself described his group in terms that blurred the line between social experiment and intelligence operation.</p>
<p>Pettie's father had intelligence connections, and Pettie himself had been involved with intelligence-adjacent organizations since the 1950s. The Finders' properties included a farm in rural Virginia where members lived communally and children were raised outside conventional structures. The farm's location, its isolation, and its organized security measures were more consistent with an intelligence facility than a hippie commune.</p>
<p>Pettie's leadership style mirrored the Jesuit model of governance. He exercised absolute authority over The Finders community while maintaining a deliberately eccentric public persona. Members were assigned tasks, travels, and activities according to Pettie's directives. The group's resources — multiple properties in Washington D.C. and Virginia, extensive international travel capabilities, sophisticated communications equipment — exceeded what any genuine commune could sustain.</p>
<h3 id="the-cult-structure">The Cult Structure</h3>
<p>The Finders operated as a structured organization with clear hierarchy, assigned roles, and a philosophy that explicitly rejected conventional morality. Children within the group were not raised by their biological parents but were treated as communal property — raised collectively according to principles established by Pettie. This detachment of children from parental bonds is characteristic of cult programming and consistent with the conditioning techniques used in intelligence-related mind control programs.</p>
<p>Former members described a group that controlled every aspect of members' lives — their work, their relationships, their finances, and their children. This total control is the hallmark of both destructive cults and intelligence operations, and in The Finders, the two were indistinguishable. The Jesuit requirement of blind obedience, documented in Catholic Confidential, found its secular expression in The Finders' demand for absolute submission to Pettie's authority.</p>
<h2 id="declassified-documents-what-the-government-knew">Declassified Documents: What the Government Knew</h2>
<h3 id="the-2019-release">The 2019 Release</h3>
<p>In 2019, the FBI released a significant cache of documents related to The Finders through the FBI Vault. These documents, totaling hundreds of pages, confirmed much of what investigators and researchers had alleged for decades: that The Finders was connected to intelligence operations, that children within the group were subjected to abuse, and that the investigation was deliberately suppressed.</p>
<p>The released documents included police reports, customs reports, internal FBI memoranda, and investigative summaries that painted a picture of a group operating with intelligence community protection. Photographs recovered during searches showed children in disturbing situations. Documents described rituals involving animals and children. Correspondence revealed connections to international organizations and foreign governments.</p>
<p>The release of these documents decades after the events they described confirmed a pattern that Howard Pittman's testimony illuminates spiritually: the truth is suppressed not permanently but strategically, released when it can no longer threaten the operational networks it would have exposed. The demons Pittman witnessed operate on timelines that extend far beyond human perception — decades of suppression are merely tactical delays in a war that spans millennia.</p>
<h3 id="what-was-still-redacted">What Was Still Redacted</h3>
<p>Despite the 2019 release, significant portions of The Finders documents remained redacted. The names of CIA personnel connected to the group, specific operational details, and information about international connections were blacked out. <p>The selective nature of the release — enough to confirm the broad outlines of the story while protecting specific identities and operations — is characteristic of intelligence community document management.
