<h2 id="the-ancient-pattern">The Ancient Pattern</h2>
<blockquote>
<p><em>"For God hath not given us the spirit of fear; but of power, and of love, and of a sound mind."</em> — 2 Timothy 1:7</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Pay no attention to the man behind the curtain. This is the central message of L. Frank Baum's 1900 novel and its iconic 1939 film adaptation — a message so subversive, so revelatory, so dangerous to the power structure that it had to be disguised as a children's story. The Wizard of Oz is not a fairy tale. It is a blueprint for understanding how the world actually works: through fear, illusion, and the systematic suppression of the individual's innate power.</p>
<p>Howard Pittman, during his near-death experience, saw behind the curtain of the spiritual realm. Three heavens. Demonic hierarchy in the second. 97.5 percent of Christians rejected — they had "never made Me Lord." Pittman witnessed the man behind the curtain: a vast, organized system of deception that operates through fear, hierarchy, and the illusion of overwhelming power. The Wizard of Oz describes the same system in allegorical terms.</p>
<p>The Jesuits are the wizards of the real world. Loyola's military organization wielding Napoleon's "absolute power." Adams's "eternal damnation." Tamburini governing "all the world" from behind a curtain — literally, from a single room. Clement XIV poisoned when he pulled the curtain aside. <em>Catholic Confidential</em> reveals the method: infiltration "cloaked with cunning," blind obedience, "every species of vice." The Jesuits operate exactly as the Wizard operates: projecting terrifying power while hiding their true nature behind a curtain of institutional authority.</p>

<h2 id="the-yellow-brick-road-the-path-of-gold">The Yellow Brick Road: The Path of Gold</h2>
<h3 id="following-the-money">Following the Money</h3>
<p>Dorothy is told to follow the Yellow Brick Road to reach the Emerald City. Yellow bricks are gold bricks. The road to power, in the real world, is paved with gold — with money, finance, and the control of currency. L. Frank Baum wrote <em>The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</em> during a period of intense debate over the gold standard in America, and many scholars have interpreted the story as a monetary allegory.</p>
<p>The Emerald City is green — the color of money, the color of the Federal Reserve note. To enter, visitors must wear green-tinted glasses, which make everything appear emerald-colored. This is the most powerful metaphor in the story: the illusion of value is maintained by forcing everyone to view the world through the same lens. Remove the glasses, and the city is ordinary. Remove the programming, and the financial system is revealed as the fraud it is.</p>
<p>The Jesuits' connection to global finance runs deep. The banking families that created the Federal Reserve system have documented connections to Jesuit-educated networks. The financial system — which keeps the population in debt bondage through mortgages (death pledges), credit cards, and student loans — is the Yellow Brick Road. It promises liberation. It delivers to the Wizard's city.</p>
<p>Agent131711 documents on Substack (agent131711.substack.com) how the financial system functions as a control mechanism, extracting labor from the population while directing wealth to the institutions that manage the system. The Yellow Brick Road does not lead to freedom. It leads to the Wizard — and the Wizard is a fraud.</p>
<h3 id="the-silver-slippers">The Silver Slippers</h3>
<p>In Baum's original novel, Dorothy's slippers are silver, not ruby. This is significant in the monetary allegory: gold and silver were the two metals at the center of the currency debate. The silver slippers — representing the silver standard, the people's money — had the power to transport Dorothy home at any time. But she did not know it.</p>
<p>This is the central revelation: you already have the power. The system — the Wizard, the Jesuits, the financial architecture, the institutional church — depends on you not knowing this. As long as you follow the Yellow Brick Road, looking for salvation from the Wizard, you remain under control. The moment you look down at your own feet, the spell is broken.</p>
<h2 id="the-four-companions-what-the-system-takes">The Four Companions: What the System Takes</h2>
<h3 id="the-scarecrow-the-mind">The Scarecrow: The Mind</h3>
<p>The Scarecrow believes he has no brain. He seeks intelligence from the Wizard. But throughout the journey, the Scarecrow is the most intelligent member of the group — solving problems, devising plans, thinking creatively. He had a brain all along. He simply did not believe it.</p>
<p>The education system — designed in large part by the Jesuits through their <em>Ratio Studiorum</em> — produces Scarecrows. It trains people to believe they cannot think without credentials, without degrees, without institutional validation. The mortarboard (Saturn's cube, as discussed in Part 4 of the Saturn series) crowns the graduate as an official thinker — implying that before graduation, thinking was not authorized.</p>
<p>Howard Pittman's experience reveals that spiritual discernment — the highest form of intelligence — requires no institutional credential. The 97.5 percent who were rejected had been through the system. They had their diplomas, their doctrines, their denominational affiliations. None of it mattered. What mattered was whether they could think for themselves — whether they could discern truth from the institutional fabrication that surrounded them.</p>
<h3 id="the-tin-man-the-heart">The Tin Man: The Heart</h3>
<p>The Tin Man believes he has no heart. He seeks emotion from the Wizard. But throughout the journey, the Tin Man is the most compassionate member of the group — weeping over crushed beetles, mourning fallen trees, caring deeply about his companions. He had a heart all along.</p>
<p>The industrial system — the cubicle, the factory, the corporate hierarchy — produces Tin Men. It strips people of emotional connection, reducing them to functional units in a productive machine. The Jesuits, with their emphasis on institutional obedience over personal feeling, created the template for this dehumanization. The order demands that members suppress personal emotion in favor of institutional loyalty — blind obedience that overrides the heart.</p>
<h3 id="the-cowardly-lion-the-courage">The Cowardly Lion: The Courage</h3>
<p>The Lion believes he has no courage. He seeks bravery from the Wizard. But throughout the journey, the Lion faces every danger, protects his companions, and perseveres despite his fear. He had courage all along.</p>
<p>The fear system — terrorism, pandemic, economic collapse, climate catastrophe, eternal damnation — produces Cowardly Lions. It convinces the population that they cannot face the world without the protection of the institution. The government, the military, the church, the corporation — all present themselves as necessary shields against a terrifying world. Without them, you are helpless. With them, you are safe. This is the Lion's delusion.</p>
<p>The <em>Catholic Confidential</em> documentary reveals how the Jesuits use fear deliberately: the fear of hell, the fear of excommunication, the fear of social ostracism. These fears are not natural. They are manufactured — tools of control designed to keep the population dependent on the institution.</p>
<h3 id="dorothy-the-soul">Dorothy: The Soul</h3>
<p>Dorothy wants to go home. She believes the Wizard can take her there. But the Wizard cannot. Only Dorothy can take herself home, using the power she already possesses — the silver slippers on her own feet.</p>
<p>Dorothy represents the human soul, trapped in Oz (the material world), seeking liberation from an external authority (the Wizard/institution/Saturn system). Her journey teaches her that the power was always within her — that she never needed the Wizard, the road, or the Emerald City. She needed only to recognize her own capacity and act on it.</p>
<p>This is the message that the Jesuits, the secret societies, and the Saturn system cannot tolerate. If people realized that they already possess everything they need — that no institution, no ritual, no hierarchy is necessary for spiritual liberation — the entire control system would collapse. The Wizard would be revealed as the little man behind the curtain. The Emerald City would lose its green tint. And the population would click their heels and go home.</p>
<h2 id="the-witch-the-fear-machine">The Witch: The Fear Machine</h2>
<h3 id="the-wicked-witch-of-the-west">The Wicked Witch of the West</h3>
<p>The Witch represents fear itself — the weapon that the power structure uses to maintain control. She commands flying monkeys (surveillance agents), controls an army (military force), and terrorizes the land from her castle (the institution). Her power appears overwhelming.</p>
<p>But her weakness is water. She melts when Dorothy throws water on her. Water, in scripture, represents truth, purification, the word of God. The fear machine, no matter how terrifying it appears, dissolves when truth is applied. The Jesuits fear truth more than any other weapon. Their entire operation — the infiltration, the cunning, the blind obedience, the vice — exists because truth would destroy them.</p>
<p>Howard Pittman learned that the demonic hierarchy operates through fear. The demons he witnessed in the second heaven used fear as their primary tool — fear of death, fear of loss, fear of the unknown. But Pittman also learned that these demons have no power over those who stand in truth. The Witch melts. The demons flee. The Wizard is exposed.</p>
<h3 id="the-tornado-the-great-reset">The Tornado: The Great Reset</h3>
<p>Dorothy arrives in Oz via tornado — a violent disruption that tears her from her normal life and deposits her in a strange, controlled environment. This parallels the great upheavals — wars, pandemics, economic collapses — that the power structure engineers to reshape society.</p>
<p>The tornado is not an accident. It is a tool. Every great reset — every revolution, every pandemic response, every economic restructuring — serves to tear the population from its moorings and deposit it in a new environment where the rules are different and the Wizard's power appears necessary. The Jesuits have been engineering these resets for centuries, from the Thirty Years' War to the French Revolution to the events that shape our present moment.</p>
<h2 id="the-man-behind-the-curtain">The Man Behind the Curtain</h2>
<h3 id="exposure-and-its-consequences">Exposure and Its Consequences</h3>
<p>When Toto pulls the curtain aside, the Wizard is revealed as an ordinary man operating an elaborate machine of illusion. His power was never real. It was projected — amplified by technology, sustained by fear, and dependent on no one looking too closely.</p>
<p>This is the reality of every institution that claims supernatural authority: the Vatican, the secret societies, the intelligence agencies, the financial system. Behind the curtain, there are men — ordinary, fallible, mortal men — operating machines of illusion. The Jesuits' power is not supernatural. It is organizational, financial, and psychological. It is impressive, but it is not divine. And like the Wizard's power, it evaporates the moment enough people see through it.</p>
<p>Agent131711's work on Substack is dedicated to pulling the curtain aside — to exposing the ordinary men behind the extraordinary illusions. Every article, every investigation, every documented connection between the visible and the hidden is a tug on the curtain. And every person who reads, understands, and shares this information is another Toto, pulling the curtain back a little further.</p>
<h2 id="the-power-you-already-have">The Power You Already Have</h2>
<h3 id="clicking-your-heels">Clicking Your Heels</h3>
<p>The Wizard of Oz ends with a revelation: Dorothy always had the power to go home. She needed only to click her heels three times and say the words. The power was in her all along.</p>
<p>This is not merely a storytelling device. It is a spiritual truth. The power to break free from the Saturn system, the Jesuit matrix, the financial prison, the fear machine — this power resides in every individual. It is the power of truth, discernment, and the refusal to participate in illusions.</p>
<p>Ephesians 6:12 describes the battle: not against flesh and blood, but against principalities and powers. The weapons are spiritual — truth, faith, the word of God. Not the institutional word. Not the Jesuit-curated word. The living word that exists beyond institutions, beyond texts, beyond the curtain of human authority.</p>
<p>Howard Pittman returned from the other side with a simple message: wake up. Stop serving self. Stop trusting the Wizard. Click your heels. Go home.</p>
<p>The 97.5 percent never clicked. They stayed in Oz, following the Yellow Brick Road, wearing the green glasses, believing the Wizard's promises. Do not make their mistake. The power is yours. It always was.</p>
