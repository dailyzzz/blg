<h2 id="debunking-the-medical-claims">Debunking the Medical Claims</h2>
<h3 id="claim-1-circumcision-prevents-penile-cancer">Claim 1: Circumcision Prevents Penile Cancer</h3>
<p>This was one of the earliest medical justifications and is still cited today. The claim is that circumcision reduces the risk of penile cancer. Here are the facts:</p>
<ul>
<li>Penile cancer is <strong>extremely rare</strong> — it affects approximately 1 in 100,000 men annually in the United States</li>
<li>The incidence of penile cancer in <strong>Denmark</strong> (where circumcision is virtually nonexistent) is <strong>lower</strong> than in the United States</li>
<li>The American Cancer Society has stated that circumcision should <strong>not</strong> be recommended as a prevention strategy for penile cancer</li>
<li>Risk factors for penile cancer include <strong>smoking, HPV infection, phimosis</strong> (which can be treated without circumcision), and <strong>poor hygiene</strong> — not the presence of a foreskin</li>
</ul>
<p>Using cancer prevention to justify routine infant surgery on a body part that develops cancer at a rate of 0.001% is like justifying the removal of all breast tissue from infant girls to prevent breast cancer — which has an incidence rate <strong>140 times higher</strong> than penile cancer.</p>
<h3 id="claim-2-circumcision-prevents-utis">Claim 2: Circumcision Prevents UTIs</h3>
<p>Studies have claimed that circumcised males have a lower risk of urinary tract infections in the first year of life. Even if this claim is accepted at face value:</p>
<ul>
<li>The absolute risk reduction is approximately <strong>1%</strong> — from about 1.5% to 0.5%</li>
<li>UTIs in infant males are easily treated with <strong>antibiotics</strong></li>
<li><strong>Girls</strong> have a much higher rate of UTIs than boys, and no one suggests surgically altering female anatomy to prevent them</li>
<li>The studies showing UTI reduction have been criticized for <strong>methodological flaws</strong>, including failure to account for confounding variables like breastfeeding (which independently reduces UTI risk)</li>
</ul>
<p>To prevent one UTI, approximately <strong>100 circumcisions</strong> must be performed. This means 99 boys are subjected to unnecessary surgery — with its attendant risks of bleeding, infection, adhesions, scarring, and worse — to potentially prevent one easily treatable infection.</p>
<h3 id="claim-3-circumcision-prevents-hiv">Claim 3: Circumcision Prevents HIV</h3>
<p>This is the most recent and most heavily promoted claim, based on three randomized controlled trials conducted in Africa between 2005 and 2007. These trials claimed that circumcision reduced female-to-male HIV transmission by approximately 60%.</p>
<p>The problems with these studies are profound:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Early termination</strong>: All three trials were stopped early, which is known to inflate effect sizes in clinical trials</li>
<li><strong>Confounding variables</strong>: Circumcised men in the trials received medical care, condoms, and health education that uncircumcised men did not receive — making it impossible to isolate the effect of circumcision</li>
<li><strong>Lead-time bias</strong>: Circumcised men were told to abstain from sex during their recovery period (6-8 weeks), giving them less time at risk compared to the control group</li>
<li><strong>Absolute vs. relative risk</strong>: The "60% reduction" is a relative risk figure. The absolute risk reduction was approximately <strong>1.3%</strong> — from 2.5% to 1.2% over the study period</li>
<li><strong>No protection for women</strong>: Studies on female-to-male transmission do not apply to male-to-female transmission, and subsequent studies found <strong>no protective effect</strong> for women</li>
<li><strong>Contradicted by population data</strong>: The United States has the highest circumcision rate in the developed world AND the highest HIV rate. If circumcision prevented HIV, the opposite should be true</li>
</ul>
<p>These African trials were funded by organizations with financial ties to the circumcision industry and were promoted by agencies seeking to justify mass circumcision programs in Africa — programs worth billions of dollars in international aid money.</p>
