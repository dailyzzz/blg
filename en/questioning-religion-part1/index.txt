<h2 id="the-ancient-pattern">The Ancient Pattern</h2>
<blockquote>
<p><em>"Prove all things; hold fast that which is good."</em> — 1 Thessalonians 5:21</p>
</blockquote>
<p>There comes a moment in the life of every honest seeker when the questions can no longer be suppressed. The doubts that whisper at 3 AM. The contradictions that nag during the sermon. The gap between what the institution teaches and what the text actually says. This moment is not a crisis of faith. It is the birth of faith — real faith, tested faith, faith that has survived the fire of inquiry rather than the lukewarm bath of tradition.</p>
<p>Howard Pittman, during his near-death experience, was confronted with the most disturbing truth a religious person can face: the institution fails. Of the Christians who died during his experience, 97.5 percent were rejected. They had attended church, celebrated holidays, spoken the name of Jesus. But they had "never made Me Lord." Pittman learned that 95 percent of professing Christians serve "self" rather than God. Their words, which carry spiritual weight according to Matthew 12:36-37, were the words the institution taught them — but those words did not save them.</p>
<p>The Jesuits built the institution. Ignatius of Loyola's military organization, wielding Napoleon's "absolute power," created the educational, liturgical, and theological framework within which most Christians worship. Adams warned of their "eternal damnation." Tamburini governed "all the world." Clement XIV was poisoned for opposing them. The religion you practice was shaped by men who, according to <em>Catholic Confidential</em>, infiltrate "cloaked with cunning" and practice "every species of vice."</p>
<p>This is where the journey begins.</p>

<h2 id="the-first-question-who-taught-you">The First Question: Who Taught You?</h2>
<h3 id="the-chain-of-transmission">The Chain of Transmission</h3>
<p>Every belief you hold about God, Jesus, salvation, scripture, morality, and the afterlife was transmitted to you by someone. Your parents, your pastor, your Sunday school teacher, your denomination — each link in the chain shaped your understanding of spiritual reality. But who shaped theirs?</p>
<p>Trace any denomination back far enough and you arrive at an institution. The Catholic Church claims apostolic succession — an unbroken chain from Peter to the current Pope. Protestant denominations trace to the Reformation, which was a reaction against the Catholic Church but retained much of its theological framework. The creeds, the canon, the calendar, the concepts of heaven and hell, the structure of worship — all were established by institutions, specifically by councils of men who made decisions about what you would believe centuries before you were born.</p>
<p>The Jesuits influenced these institutions more than any other single organization. Their educational system trained the clergy. Their theologians shaped the doctrines. Their missionaries established the churches. Their Ratio Studiorum became the template for seminary education. When you sit in a pew and hear a sermon, you are hearing the output of a system the Jesuits designed.</p>
<h3 id="the-uncomfortable-implications">The Uncomfortable Implications</h3>
<p>This does not mean everything you were taught is false. It means everything you were taught should be examined. Paul instructed the Thessalonians to "prove all things" — to test, to examine, to verify. The Bereans were praised in Acts 17:11 because "they received the word with all readiness of mind, and searched the scriptures daily, whether those things were so."</p>
<p>The institutional church discourages this behavior. It calls questioning "doubt" and treats doubt as sin. It demands faith — but faith in the institution, not faith in God. There is a vast difference between trusting God and trusting the men who claim to speak for God, especially when those men were trained by an organization that <em>Catholic Confidential</em> reveals operates through deception, blind obedience, and "every species of vice."</p>
<p>Agent131711 documents on Substack (agent131711.substack.com) how the institutional church has systematically discouraged independent scripture study throughout its history. For centuries, the Catholic Church kept the Bible in Latin, inaccessible to common people. When Wycliffe and Tyndale translated it into English, they were persecuted. The Jesuits burned Tyndale at the stake. The institution does not want you to read the text yourself, because the text contradicts the institution.</p>
<h2 id="what-the-institution-added">What the Institution Added</h2>
<h3 id="traditions-not-found-in-scripture">Traditions Not Found in Scripture</h3>
<p>The list of institutional traditions that have no scriptural basis is longer than most Christians realize:</p>
<p><strong>Christmas</strong>: No scripture records the date of Jesus' birth. The December 25 date was adopted from pagan solar festivals. The Christmas tree, gift exchange, and Santa Claus are pagan additions.</p>
<p><strong>Easter</strong>: The name derives from Ishtar/Eostre, pagan fertility goddesses. The eggs and rabbits are fertility symbols. The timing is based on the pagan lunar calendar.</p>
<p><strong>Sunday Worship</strong>: The early church met on the Sabbath (Saturday). The shift to Sunday was a Catholic Church decision, not a scriptural command.</p>
<p><strong>The Trinity</strong>: The word "Trinity" does not appear in scripture. The doctrine was formalized at the Council of Nicaea in 325 AD, under the direction of Emperor Constantine — a political leader, not a prophet.</p>
<p><strong>Infant Baptism</strong>: No scripture records the baptism of an infant. Baptism in scripture is consistently presented as a conscious, voluntary act.</p>
<p><strong>The Papacy</strong>: No scripture establishes a single human leader of the church. The interpretation of Matthew 16:18 ("upon this rock I will build my church") as establishing papal authority is a Catholic tradition, not a textual certainty.</p>
<p>Each of these additions serves the institution. Christmas and Easter create mandatory participation events. Sunday worship differentiates Christianity from Judaism. The Trinity provides a theological framework for institutional authority. Infant baptism captures members before they can choose. The Papacy centralizes power.</p>
<h3 id="traditions-that-contradict-scripture">Traditions That Contradict Scripture</h3>
<p>Some institutional traditions do not merely add to scripture — they directly contradict it.</p>
<p><strong>Calling clergy "Father"</strong>: Jesus explicitly prohibited this in Matthew 23:9: <em>"And call no man your father upon the earth: for one is your Father, which is in heaven."</em> The Catholic Church ignores this command entirely.</p>
<p><strong>Repetitive prayer</strong>: Jesus warned against "vain repetitions" in Matthew 6:7. The Rosary, which repeats the same prayers dozens of times, is the definition of vain repetition.</p>
<p><strong>Confession to priests</strong>: Scripture says there is "one mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus" (1 Timothy 2:5). The confessional, where priests claim authority to absolve sins, contradicts this directly.</p>
<p><strong>Praying to saints</strong>: The second commandment prohibits the creation and veneration of images. The Catholic practice of praying to saints and venerating their images violates this commandment.</p>
<p>The Jesuits, who serve as the intellectual elite of the Catholic Church, are fully aware of these contradictions. Their strategy, as revealed in <em>Catholic Confidential</em>, is to maintain traditions that serve institutional power regardless of their scriptural basis. The blind obedience demanded by the Jesuit oath ensures that members enforce these traditions without question.</p>
<h2 id="what-the-institution-removed">What the Institution Removed</h2>
<h3 id="the-lost-teachings">The Lost Teachings</h3>
<p>More disturbing than what was added is what was removed. The institutional church edited the canon — the list of books considered "scripture" — through a series of councils that excluded texts the institution found inconvenient.</p>
<p>The Book of Enoch, quoted in the epistle of Jude (Jude 1:14-15) and clearly known to the apostles, was excluded from the Western canon. Its detailed descriptions of fallen angels, the Nephilim, and the structure of the heavens did not serve the institution's theological framework.</p>
<p>The Gospel of Thomas, a collection of sayings attributed to Jesus, was excluded. Many of its sayings emphasize direct, personal knowledge of God — gnosis — which undermines the institution's role as necessary intermediary.</p>
<p>Numerous other texts — the Shepherd of Hermas, the Didache, the Apocalypse of Peter — were included in early Christian canons and later removed. The institution decided what you could read and what you could not, what was "inspired" and what was not, based on criteria that served institutional power.</p>
<p>Howard Pittman was shown that the spiritual realm operates on truth, not tradition. The texts that were removed may or may not be inspired, but the decision to remove them was made by men — men influenced by the same Jesuit-type institutional logic that has governed the church since Constantine's co-option of Christianity in the 4th century.</p>
<h2 id="the-fear-of-questioning">The Fear of Questioning</h2>
<h3 id="why-most-people-stop">Why Most People Stop</h3>
<p>Most people who begin questioning their religion stop before they get very far. The reasons are emotional, not intellectual:</p>
<p><strong>Fear of hell</strong>: The institution has taught that questioning leads to damnation. This is the ultimate control mechanism — weaponized fear of eternal punishment.</p>
<p><strong>Fear of social loss</strong>: Church communities provide friendship, support, and identity. Questioning risks losing all of that.</p>
<p><strong>Fear of meaninglessness</strong>: If the institution is wrong, what is true? The void is terrifying.</p>
<p><strong>Fear of being wrong</strong>: What if the institution is right and you are wrong? The cost of error seems infinite.</p>
<p>These fears are by design. The Jesuit system depends on fear to maintain compliance. The <em>Catholic Confidential</em> documentary shows how the order uses "every species of vice" — including psychological manipulation — to achieve its goals. The fear of questioning is the most powerful tool in the institution's arsenal, more effective than any Inquisition, any excommunication, any burning at the stake.</p>
<p>But consider Howard Pittman's testimony. The 97.5 percent who were rejected did not question. They trusted the institution. They followed the traditions. They did not examine, test, or prove. And they were turned away. The fear of questioning did not save them. It damned them.</p>
<h3 id="the-courage-to-begin">The Courage to Begin</h3>
<p>Questioning does not mean rejecting. It means examining. It means taking Paul's instruction seriously: prove all things. It means reading the text yourself, without the filter of denominational interpretation. It means asking hard questions and accepting uncomfortable answers. It means being willing to discover that some of what you believed is true, some is false, and some is a mixture designed by men with agendas that are not God's agendas.</p>
<p>This is Part 1 — the beginning of the journey. In the parts that follow, we will examine the specific claims of institutional Christianity: the crucifixion, the resurrection, the authorship of the Bible, the construction of the New Testament, and the mass deletions that created the version of Christianity most people practice today.</p>
<p>The journey will not be comfortable. But as Pittman learned, comfort is not the path to truth. Self-service is not the path to God. And the institution that promised you salvation may be the very thing standing between you and the real thing.</p>
