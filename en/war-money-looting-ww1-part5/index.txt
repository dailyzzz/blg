<h2 id="the-war-profiteers">The War Profiteers</h2>
<h3 id="the-arms-manufacturers">The Arms Manufacturers</h3>
<p>World War I was the first fully industrialized war, and the industrial corporations that supplied the combatants reaped profits on a scale never before seen:</p>
<p><strong>DuPont</strong> — The Delaware-based chemical company supplied 40% of the explosives used by the Allied forces. DuPont's profits increased from $6 million in 1914 to $82 million in 1916 — a 1,300% increase in two years. The DuPont family, already one of the wealthiest in America, emerged from the war as an industrial dynasty whose wealth rivaled that of the Rockefellers.</p>
<p><strong>Bethlehem Steel</strong> — Under the leadership of Charles Schwab, Bethlehem Steel became the primary supplier of armor plate, guns, and munitions to the Allied powers. Schwab personally traveled to London to negotiate contracts worth hundreds of millions of dollars — contracts financed by Liberty Bond proceeds. Bethlehem Steel's stock price increased by over 500% during the war years.</p>
<p><strong>U.S. Steel</strong> — J.P. Morgan's creation, U.S. Steel, supplied steel for ships, tanks, artillery, and infrastructure. The company's profits tripled during the war years, and its executives were rewarded with bonuses and stock options that made them among the wealthiest men in America.</p>
<p><strong>Standard Oil</strong> — John D. Rockefeller's oil empire supplied petroleum products to both sides of the conflict. Standard Oil's subsidiaries in Germany continued to supply the German military throughout the war, even as the parent company's American operations supplied the Allies. This dual-supply arrangement, which violated the spirit (if not the letter) of neutrality laws, was possible because the Rockefeller network operated above national loyalties — serving the interests of the global financial elite rather than any single nation.</p>

<h3 id="the-income-tax-during-wartime">The Income Tax During Wartime</h3>
<p>The income tax, established in 1913, was initially a modest levy on the wealthy: 1% on incomes over $3,000, with a maximum rate of 7% on incomes over $500,000. But the war provided the pretext for dramatic increases:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>1916</strong>: Maximum rate raised to 15%</li>
<li><strong>1917</strong>: Maximum rate raised to 67%</li>
<li><strong>1918</strong>: Maximum rate raised to 77%</li>
</ul>
<p>These rates applied to the highest income brackets, but the war also broadened the tax base dramatically. By 1918, millions of Americans who had never paid income tax were filing returns and paying taxes to fund the war.</p>
<p>The income tax revenue went directly to debt service — paying interest on the bonds and loans that financed the war.
