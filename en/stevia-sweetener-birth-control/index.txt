<h2 id="the-contraceptive-history">The Contraceptive History</h2>
<h3 id="indigenous-knowledge">Indigenous Knowledge</h3>
<p>Stevia rebaudiana is a plant native to Paraguay and Brazil. The Guarani people, who have used it for centuries, called it <em>ka'a he'e</em> — "sweet herb." But the Guarani did not use stevia only as a sweetener. They used it as a medicinal plant — and one of its documented traditional uses was as a contraceptive.</p>
<p>This is not fringe history. It is documented in ethnobotanical literature. The Guarani women used stevia preparations to prevent pregnancy and to regulate menstruation. The plant was recognized by indigenous peoples as having anti-fertility properties long before Western science examined it.</p>
<p>The Jesuit missionaries who established missions (reducciones) among the Guarani in the 17th and 18th centuries would have known about stevia's traditional uses. The Jesuits were meticulous documentarians of indigenous knowledge — their reports to Rome included detailed botanical and medical information. The question of what the Jesuits did with this knowledge is one that institutional history has carefully avoided.</p>

It entered the institutional network and has resurfaced in forms that serve institutional agendas.</p>
<h3 id="the-scientific-evidence">The Scientific Evidence</h3>
<p>Multiple studies have examined stevia's effects on fertility:</p>
<p>A 1968 study published in the journal <em>Economic Botany</em> documented the Guarani's use of stevia as a contraceptive and noted that prolonged consumption reduced fertility in laboratory animals.</p>
<p>A 1999 study published in <em>Phytomedicine</em> found that stevioside — stevia's active compound — reduced testosterone levels and sperm production in male rats at high doses.</p>
<p>A 2012 study found that stevia extract affected reproductive hormones in female rats, potentially disrupting ovulation.</p>
<p>The industry response is predictable: the doses in these studies are higher than normal dietary consumption. The dose argument — the same argument used to defend arsenic in rice — ignores cumulative exposure, synergistic effects, and the reality that stevia is now present in thousands of products consumed daily by millions of people.</p>
<h2 id="the-modern-stevia-explosion">The Modern Stevia Explosion</h2>
<h3 id="from-niche-to-ubiquitous">From Niche to Ubiquitous</h3>
<p>Two decades ago, stevia was a niche product found in health food stores. Today, it is everywhere. Coca-Cola uses it (as Truvia). PepsiCo uses it (as PureVia). It appears in yogurt, protein bars, flavored water, chewing gum, toothpaste, children's vitamins, and thousands of other products.</p>
<p>The speed of stevia's adoption is remarkable. In 2008, the FDA granted stevia GRAS (Generally Recognized As Safe) status. Within a decade, it had penetrated virtually every category of the food industry. This rapid adoption was not driven by consumer demand — it was driven by food manufacturers seeking a "natural" alternative to artificial sweeteners that were facing increasing public skepticism.</p>
