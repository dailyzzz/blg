<h2 id="the-bootlegging-fortune">The Bootlegging Fortune</h2>
<h3 id="prohibition-and-the-catholic-connection">Prohibition and the Catholic Connection</h3>
<p>Prohibition (1920-1933) was one of the most profitable periods in American criminal history — and Joseph Kennedy was at the center of it. While the exact nature and extent of Kennedy's bootlegging operations remain debated (the family has spent decades suppressing the evidence), multiple credible sources confirm that Kennedy imported and distributed illegal liquor on a massive scale during the 1920s.</p>
<p>Kennedy's liquor connections were established well before Prohibition. His father, P.J. Kennedy, was a Boston saloon owner and political boss with deep connections to the Irish Catholic underworld. When Prohibition began, Joseph Kennedy was uniquely positioned to exploit the situation: he had the family connections to the liquor trade, the financial resources to organize large-scale smuggling operations, and the political protection to operate with impunity.</p>
<p>The connection between Prohibition and the Catholic Church is rarely discussed but critical. The Temperance movement that led to Prohibition was overwhelmingly Protestant, and it was explicitly anti-Catholic in many of its manifestations. Catholic immigrants — Irish, Italian, German — were disproportionately targeted by Prohibition enforcement. Yet the Catholic establishment profited enormously from Prohibition through the bootlegging networks that it controlled.</p>
<p>This is the classic Jesuit strategy: create a conflict, position yourself on both sides, and profit regardless of the outcome. The Jesuits supported Prohibition through their influence on the Progressive movement (which overlapped significantly with the Temperance movement), while simultaneously profiting from the illegal liquor trade through their Catholic network. Joseph Kennedy was the point man for this operation in the Northeast.</p>
<h3 id="the-liquor-licensing-windfall">The Liquor Licensing Windfall</h3>
<p>When Prohibition ended in 1933, Kennedy was positioned to profit again. He had already secured exclusive import licenses for Scotch whisky and British gin — licenses that he obtained through his connections to the British establishment (including, reportedly, Winston Churchill). These licenses, granted before Prohibition was officially repealed, ensured that Kennedy would dominate the legal liquor market from day one.</p>
<p>The company Kennedy established, Somerset Importers, became one of the largest liquor distributors in the United States. The fortune Kennedy made from legal liquor sales dwarfed whatever he had earned from bootlegging — and it was all made possible by advance knowledge of Prohibition's repeal, knowledge that was available only to the politically connected elite.</p>
<h2 id="the-wall-street-manipulation">The Wall Street Manipulation</h2>
<h3 id="insider-trading-and-pool-operations">Insider Trading and Pool Operations</h3>
<p>In the 1920s, Joseph Kennedy became one of the most successful stock market operators in America — through methods that would be considered criminal today. Kennedy participated in "stock pools" — coordinated operations in which a group of insiders would artificially inflate the price of a stock, attract outside investors, and then sell at the peak, leaving the outside investors holding worthless shares.</p>
<p>Kennedy's most famous pool operation involved Yellow Cab Company stock. Working with a group of insiders, Kennedy manipulated the stock price through coordinated buying, planted news stories, and false tips to outside investors. When the price reached the target level, the pool sold out, pocketing millions while the outside investors were wiped out.</p>
<p>These operations were perfectly legal in the 1920s — there were no laws against insider trading or market manipulation. But they were morally indefensible, and they contributed directly to the speculative bubble that led to the 1929 crash. Kennedy, who had exited the market before the crash (reportedly after a shoeshine boy offered him stock tips, which Kennedy took as a sign that the market was overheated), emerged from the Depression wealthier than ever while millions of Americans lost everything.</p>
<h3 id="the-sec-the-fox-guarding-the-henhouse">The SEC: The Fox Guarding the Henhouse</h3>
<p>In 1934, President Franklin Roosevelt appointed Joseph Kennedy as the first chairman of the Securities and Exchange Commission — the agency created to regulate the very practices that had made Kennedy rich. Roosevelt reportedly justified the appointment by saying: "Set a thief to catch a thief."</p>
<p>Kennedy's appointment to the SEC was the ultimate expression of the Jesuit strategy of infiltration. By placing an insider trader in charge of regulating insider trading, the financial elite ensured that the new regulations would be designed to eliminate their competition while preserving their own ability to profit. Kennedy served as SEC chairman for just over a year, during which he established the framework of securities regulation that remains largely intact today — a framework that regulates small operators while leaving the biggest players untouched.</p>
