<h2 id="the-new-food-pyramid-decoded">The New Food Pyramid Decoded</h2>
<h3 id="what-changed-and-why">What Changed and Why</h3>
<p>The new food pyramid shifts dietary emphasis toward "healthy proteins" — a category that sounds nutritious until you examine what the category includes. Alongside traditional proteins (meat, eggs, dairy), the new pyramid prominently features plant-based protein alternatives, insect-derived proteins, and — critically — creates space for lab-grown and cell-cultured proteins that are moving rapidly toward market authorization.</p>
<p>The inclusion of these alternatives is presented as nutritional science. In reality, it is policy preparation: normalizing synthetic and alternative protein sources before they replace traditional protein in the food supply. The food pyramid has always been a policy tool as much as a nutritional guide, and the "healthy protein" emphasis prepares the American public for a protein transition that serves corporate interests, not nutritional needs.</p>
<p>The USDA — which produces dietary guidelines — is structurally conflicted. It simultaneously promotes agricultural products and regulates the food industry, creating a built-in conflict between consumer health and industry revenue. The new food pyramid reflects industry influence: promoting protein categories that align with corporate investment in lab-grown meat, insect farming, and plant-based alternatives.</p>
<h3 id="the-protein-redefinition">The Protein Redefinition</h3>
<p>Traditional protein sources — grass-fed beef, pasture-raised poultry, wild-caught fish, eggs from free-range hens — are being systematically devalued through climate change narratives (livestock produce emissions), animal welfare campaigns (farming is cruel), and health scares (red meat causes cancer). Each narrative serves to push consumers away from natural protein and toward synthetic alternatives.</p>
<p>The replacement proteins — lab-grown meat, insect protein, plant-based formulations, and mycoprotein (fungus-based) — are presented as healthier, more sustainable, and more ethical. Karen Kingston has documented how these replacement proteins serve the broader agenda of centralizing food production in the hands of corporations that can control the food supply. When your protein comes from a laboratory rather than a farm, the laboratory controls your nutrition.</p>

Leviticus 11 specified which animals are clean and suitable for consumption. Throughout Scripture, food is treated not merely as fuel but as divine provision — something God designed with care and purpose.</p>

This is the transhumanist message applied to nutrition: technology improves upon creation.</p>
