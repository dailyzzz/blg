<h2 id="the-three-captures-of-1913">The Three Captures of 1913</h2>
<h3 id="the-16th-amendment-the-income-tax">The 16th Amendment: The Income Tax</h3>
<p>The 16th Amendment to the United States Constitution, ratified on February 3, 1913, gave Congress the power to levy an income tax without apportioning it among the states. This seemingly technical change had revolutionary implications: for the first time, the federal government could reach directly into the pockets of every American citizen and extract a portion of their labor.</p>
<p>Before the 16th Amendment, the federal government was funded primarily by tariffs and excise taxes — taxes on goods, not on people. The income tax transformed the relationship between the citizen and the state from one of limited obligation to one of unlimited extraction. There is no cap on the income tax; the rate can be set at any level Congress chooses. In theory (and in practice during wartime), the government can take 90% or more of a citizen's income.</p>
<p>The income tax was necessary to service the debt that the Federal Reserve would create. Without a mechanism for taxing citizens directly, the government could not guarantee repayment of the bonds and notes issued by the Federal Reserve system. The income tax was not a revenue measure — it was a debt service mechanism, designed to ensure that the banking elite would always be repaid.</p>
<p>The ratification of the 16th Amendment is itself shrouded in controversy. Legal researcher William Benson's research, documented in <em>The Law That Never Was</em>, presents evidence that the amendment was never properly ratified by the required number of states. If Benson's research is correct, the entire federal income tax system rests on a fraudulent foundation — which would be entirely consistent with the Jesuit modus operandi of building power on deception.</p>
<h3 id="the-17th-amendment-the-senate-capture">The 17th Amendment: The Senate Capture</h3>
<p>The 17th Amendment, ratified on April 8, 1913, changed the method of electing United States Senators from appointment by state legislatures to direct popular election. This change was presented as a democratic reform — giving "the people" a direct voice in choosing their senators.</p>
<p>In reality, the 17th Amendment destroyed the last structural safeguard against federal overreach. Under the original Constitution, senators were chosen by state legislatures and served as representatives of state governments in the federal system. This gave states a direct check on federal power: if the federal government overstepped its bounds, state legislatures could recall or replace their senators.</p>
<p>Direct election severed this connection. Senators no longer answered to state governments; they answered to the electorate — which meant, in practice, they answered to whoever controlled the media, the campaign financing, and the party machinery. And who controlled these? The same banking and corporate interests that controlled the Federal Reserve.</p>
<p>The 17th Amendment thus completed the centralization of power in Washington by removing the states' only direct representation in the federal government. Combined with the income tax (which gave the federal government unlimited revenue) and the Federal Reserve (which gave the banking elite control over the money supply), the 17th Amendment created a system of governance in which the people believed they were free while being, in fact, subjects of a financial oligarchy.</p>
