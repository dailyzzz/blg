<h2 id="what-gwen-was--officially">What GWEN Was — Officially</h2>
<p>The Ground Wave Emergency Network (GWEN) was a command-and-control communications system built by the United States Air Force during the Cold War. Its stated purpose was simple and, on the surface, reasonable: to ensure that military communications could survive a nuclear attack.</p>
<p>The problem GWEN was designed to solve was real. A nuclear detonation in the upper atmosphere creates an electromagnetic pulse (EMP) that fries conventional electronics and disrupts high-frequency radio communications. The entire U.S. military command structure — the ability to communicate launch orders, coordinate defense, and maintain chain of command — depended on communications infrastructure that a single high-altitude nuclear burst could destroy.</p>
<p>GWEN's solution was to operate on <strong>extremely low frequencies (ELF)</strong> — specifically in the 150-175 kHz range — using ground-hugging radio waves that travel along the surface of the Earth rather than bouncing off the ionosphere. These ground waves are far more resistant to EMP disruption. Each GWEN tower was designed to broadcast in a 200-300 mile radius, and the network of approximately 58 relay nodes would blanket the continental United States with a survivable, low-frequency communication grid.</p>

<p>According to declassified Air Force documentation:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Tower height:</strong> Approximately 299 feet (just under the 300-foot threshold that would require aircraft obstruction lighting, making them less visible)</li>

<li><strong>Frequency range:</strong> 150-175 kHz (low frequency), with some systems operating in the UHF range for relay purposes</li>
<li><strong>Power output:</strong> 1,000-2,000 watts</li>
<li><strong>Coverage:</strong> Each tower covered a 200-300 mile radius; the full network of 58+ towers covered the entire continental United States</li>
<li><strong>Construction period:</strong> 1982-1992</li>
</ul>
<p>The buried copper wire system is critical to understand. Each GWEN tower was not merely a tall antenna — it was the visible tip of an underground electromagnetic array that extended hundreds of feet in every direction. The tower was the transmitter. The ground itself became the antenna.</p>
