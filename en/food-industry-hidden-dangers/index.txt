<h2 id="the-hidden-ingredients">The Hidden Ingredients</h2>
<h3 id="seed-oils-the-silent-killer">Seed Oils: The Silent Killer</h3>
<p>The most dangerous ingredient in the modern food supply is not a preservative or a colorant. It is seed oil — soybean oil, canola oil, corn oil, sunflower oil, safflower oil, cottonseed oil. These oils, which barely existed in the human diet before the 20th century, now constitute the primary source of fat in the American diet.</p>
<p>Seed oils are rich in omega-6 fatty acids, which promote inflammation when consumed in excess. The optimal omega-6 to omega-3 ratio in the human diet is approximately 1:1 to 4:1. The modern American diet, saturated with seed oils, has a ratio of approximately 20:1 to 50:1. This massive inflammatory load is linked to heart disease, diabetes, obesity, cancer, Alzheimer's disease, and autoimmune disorders.</p>
<p>The transition from traditional fats (butter, lard, tallow, olive oil) to seed oils was not driven by health science. It was driven by industry. Procter &#x26; Gamble introduced Crisco — hydrogenated cottonseed oil — in 1911. The American Heart Association, funded in part by seed oil manufacturers, promoted seed oils as "heart-healthy" in the 1960s. The U.S. dietary guidelines, influenced by industry lobbyists, recommended replacing saturated fat with vegetable oils.</p>
<p>Agent131711 documents on Substack (agent131711.substack.com) how the seed oil industry manufactured scientific consensus through funded research, institutional capture, and media influence. The pattern is identical to the institutional control mechanisms documented throughout this series: the Jesuit-style infiltration of regulatory, academic, and media institutions to serve industrial interests.</p>
<h3 id="excitotoxins-destroying-your-brain">Excitotoxins: Destroying Your Brain</h3>
<p>Monosodium glutamate (MSG), aspartame, and similar excitotoxins are added to processed food to enhance flavor and create addictive consumption patterns. These substances overstimulate neurons, causing cell damage and death in sensitive individuals.</p>
<p>The food industry has responded to public awareness of MSG by hiding it under dozens of alternative names: hydrolyzed protein, yeast extract, natural flavoring, autolyzed yeast, sodium caseinate, and many more. This deliberate obfuscation — naming something to hide what it is — is the linguistic equivalent of the Jesuit strategy of infiltration "cloaked with cunning."</p>
<h3 id="artificial-colors-petroleum-on-your-plate">Artificial Colors: Petroleum on Your Plate</h3>
<p>The most common food colorings — Red 40, Yellow 5, Yellow 6, Blue 1 — are derived from petroleum. They are linked to hyperactivity in children, allergic reactions, and potential carcinogenicity. European countries have banned or restricted many of these dyes. The United States has not.</p>
<p>The FDA's refusal to restrict petroleum-based food dyes is a textbook case of regulatory capture. The food dye industry generates billions in revenue. The FDA officials who evaluate dye safety often have financial connections to the industry. The result is that American children eat petroleum derivatives in their cereal, their candy, their yogurt, and their medications — while the agency tasked with protecting them does nothing.</p>
<h3 id="sugar-the-acceptable-addiction">Sugar: The Acceptable Addiction</h3>
<p>The average American consumes approximately 77 grams of added sugar per day — more than three times the WHO's recommended maximum. Sugar is addictive, inflammatory, and metabolically destructive. It drives insulin resistance, obesity, type 2 diabetes, heart disease, fatty liver disease, and dental decay.</p>
<p>The sugar industry knew about these effects decades ago and suppressed the evidence. In 2016, the JAMA Internal Medicine journal published research revealing that the sugar industry paid Harvard researchers in the 1960s to shift blame for heart disease from sugar to fat — a deception that shaped dietary policy for 50 years.</p>
<p>This is the same pattern: an industry funding research to produce predetermined conclusions, then using those conclusions to influence government policy.
