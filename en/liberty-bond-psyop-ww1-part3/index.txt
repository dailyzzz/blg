<h2 id="the-liberty-bond-machine">The Liberty Bond Machine</h2>
<h3 id="four-loan-drives">Four Loan Drives</h3>
<p>Between 1917 and 1918, the United States government issued four Liberty Bond drives, plus a final "Victory Bond" drive in 1919. The total amount raised was approximately $21.5 billion — equivalent to roughly $450 billion in today's dollars. This money was lent by ordinary American citizens to the federal government, which used it to finance the war effort.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>First Liberty Loan</strong> (June 1917): $2 billion at 3.5% interest</li>
<li><strong>Second Liberty Loan</strong> (October 1917): $3.8 billion at 4% interest</li>
<li><strong>Third Liberty Loan</strong> (April 1918): $4.2 billion at 4.25% interest</li>
<li><strong>Fourth Liberty Loan</strong> (September 1918): $7 billion at 4.25% interest</li>
<li><strong>Victory Liberty Loan</strong> (April 1919): $4.5 billion at 3.75-4.75% interest</li>
</ul>
<p>The interest rates on these bonds were deliberately set below the rate of inflation, meaning that bondholders were guaranteed to lose purchasing power over the life of the bond. A 4% bond in an economy experiencing 15-20% wartime inflation is not an investment; it is a donation disguised as an investment.</p>
<h3 id="the-propaganda-campaign">The Propaganda Campaign</h3>
<p>The Liberty Bond drives were accompanied by the most intensive propaganda campaign in American history. The Creel Committee, working with the Treasury Department, deployed every available medium to pressure Americans into buying bonds:</p>
<p><strong>Posters</strong> — Over 20 million posters were printed, featuring images designed to evoke fear, patriotism, and guilt. The famous "I Want You" Uncle Sam poster, the ominous "Destroy This Mad Brute" gorilla poster, and countless images of women, children, and soldiers were plastered on every available surface.</p>
<p><strong>Four-Minute Men</strong> — A network of 75,000 volunteer speakers delivered short, emotionally charged speeches in movie theaters, churches, lodges, and public gatherings across the country. These speakers were provided with scripted talking points by the Creel Committee and trained in techniques of persuasion that would later be recognized as psychological manipulation.</p>
<p><strong>Celebrity endorsements</strong> — Movie stars including Charlie Chaplin, Mary Pickford, and Douglas Fairbanks appeared at massive bond rallies, using their celebrity to pressure fans into purchasing bonds. These appearances were not voluntary acts of patriotism; they were coordinated by the government's propaganda apparatus.</p>
<p><strong>Social pressure</strong> — Communities organized "loyalty drives" in which citizens who failed to purchase bonds were publicly shamed, ostracized, or worse. In some communities, failure to buy bonds was treated as evidence of disloyalty, leading to harassment, vandalism, and in extreme cases, violence. The German-American community was particularly targeted — a pattern that would be repeated with Japanese-Americans during World War II.</p>
<h3 id="the-coercion-behind-the-voluntary-program">The Coercion Behind the "Voluntary" Program</h3>
<p>The Liberty Bond program was officially voluntary. In practice, it was coercive. Employers were pressured to purchase bonds on behalf of their employees (often through payroll deductions that workers could not refuse). Banks were pressured to purchase bonds with depositors' money. Communities set quotas, and those who failed to meet them faced social and economic consequences.</p>
<p>This coercion was particularly vicious in rural America, where community bonds were strong and the consequences of social ostracism were severe. Farmers who questioned the war or refused to buy bonds found their credit cut off, their insurance canceled, and their businesses boycotted. Some were tarred and feathered. At least one man — Robert Prager, a German-born coal miner in Collinsville, Illinois — was lynched by a mob for allegedly making "disloyal" remarks.</p>
