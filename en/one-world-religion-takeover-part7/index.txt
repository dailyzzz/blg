<h2 id="the-architecture-of-the-merger">The Architecture of the Merger</h2>
<h3 id="the-interfaith-movement">The Interfaith Movement</h3>
<p>The interfaith movement — the organized effort to bring representatives of all world religions into dialogue and cooperation — was not a spontaneous expression of spiritual goodwill. It was engineered by networks connected to the same Masonic-Jesuit-Illuminist structures that have shaped every major institutional development of the modern era.</p>
<p>The 1893 World Parliament of Religions in Chicago was the first major interfaith gathering, and it set the template for all that would follow. The Parliament brought together representatives of Christianity, Judaism, Islam, Hinduism, Buddhism, and other traditions for dialogue aimed at identifying common ground. The Parliament's lasting significance was not any specific agreement reached but the precedent it established: the principle that all religions are equal expressions of a common spiritual reality, and that the future lies in their convergence rather than their distinctiveness.</p>
<p>This principle directly contradicts the exclusive claims of Jesus Christ: "I am the way, the truth, and the life: no man cometh unto the Father, but by me" (John 14:6). The interfaith movement's foundational premise — that all paths lead to the same destination — is a theological assertion that can only be true if Christ's words are false. The movement is thus, at its foundation, an anti-Christian enterprise, regardless of how many Christian leaders participate in it.</p>
<h3 id="the-united-religions-initiative">The United Religions Initiative</h3>
<p>The United Religions Initiative (URI), founded in 2000, represents the institutional embodiment of the one-world religion agenda. Modeled on the United Nations, the URI brings together representatives of the world's religions to work toward common goals — peace, justice, sustainability. These goals are unobjectionable in themselves, which is precisely what makes the URI effective as an instrument of spiritual subversion.</p>
<p>The URI's founder, Episcopal Bishop William Swing, described his vision as creating a "United Nations of Religions" — a permanent body in which religious leaders would cooperate on shared objectives. The organizational structure mirrors the UN's structure, with regional affiliates, a global council, and institutional partnerships with governmental and non-governmental organizations. The URI's funding comes from sources connected to the same financial networks that fund the broader globalist agenda — Rockefeller, Soros, and other foundations aligned with the new world order.</p>
<p>The URI's operational effect is the normalization of religious syncretism — the blending of religious traditions into a unified system that retains the forms of individual religions while dissolving their substance. A Christian who participates in URI activities does not formally renounce Christianity — they simply accept the premise that Christianity is one path among many, which achieves the same result through different means.</p>
<h2 id="the-vaticans-role">The Vatican's Role</h2>
<h3 id="ecumenism-as-strategy">Ecumenism as Strategy</h3>
<p>The Vatican's embrace of ecumenism — the effort to reunite Christian denominations and engage with non-Christian religions — represents the institutional implementation of the one-world religion agenda. The Second Vatican Council (1962-1965), which was heavily influenced by Jesuit theologians, fundamentally redirected the Catholic Church's approach to other religions, moving from a position of exclusivism to one of inclusivism.</p>
<p>The Council's declaration Nostra Aetate stated that the Catholic Church "rejects nothing that is true and holy in these [other] religions" and urged Catholics to enter dialogue with followers of other faiths. This was presented as a breakthrough in interfaith understanding, but its theological implications were revolutionary: the acknowledgment that truth and holiness exist outside the Church opened the door to the logical conclusion that the Church is not the exclusive vehicle of salvation — a conclusion that undermines the Church's own claims and, more importantly, the exclusive claims of Christ.</p>
<p>Pope Francis has accelerated this ecumenical trajectory with unprecedented speed. His meetings with Islamic leaders, his joint declarations with Protestant leaders, his embrace of indigenous spiritual practices, and his calls for a "new humanism" that transcends religious boundaries all serve the one-world religion agenda. The Document on Human Fraternity, signed by Pope Francis and the Grand Imam of Al-Azhar in 2019, declared that "the pluralism and diversity of religions" is "willed by God" — a statement that directly contradicts the first commandment and the exclusive claims of the Gospel.</p>
<h3 id="the-abrahamic-family-house">The Abrahamic Family House</h3>
<p>The Abrahamic Family House in Abu Dhabi, opened in 2023, is the architectural embodiment of the one-world religion agenda. The complex includes a mosque, a church, and a synagogue, built around a shared garden — a physical representation of the theological claim that Islam, Christianity, and Judaism are equal expressions of the same divine reality.</p>
<p>The project was inspired by the Document on Human Fraternity and funded by the government of the United Arab Emirates. Its design communicates a message that words alone could not: the three Abrahamic faiths are fundamentally compatible, their differences are superficial, and their future lies in convergence. The shared garden at the center represents the common ground that the three faiths supposedly share — a ground that can only exist if the distinctive claims of each faith are abandoned.</p>
<p>For Christians, the Abrahamic Family House requires the abandonment of the most fundamental Christian confession: that Jesus Christ is the only begotten Son of God, that salvation comes through His sacrifice alone, and that no other name under heaven is given among men by which we must be saved. The "family" metaphor conceals the reality that the project demands the renunciation of everything that makes Christianity Christianity.</p>
<h2 id="the-new-age-component">The New Age Component</h2>
<h3 id="theosophy-and-the-master-plan">Theosophy and the Master Plan</h3>
<p>The New Age movement provides the spiritual content for the one-world religion that the institutional infrastructure of the interfaith movement and the Vatican's ecumenism is designed to house. The New Age synthesis — drawing on Theosophy, Eastern mysticism, Western esotericism, and quantum physics — offers a spiritual framework that accommodates every tradition while committing to none.</p>
<p>Theosophy, founded by Helena Blavatsky in 1875, was the original blueprint for the New Age synthesis. Blavatsky's magnum opus, "The Secret Doctrine," taught that all religions derive from a single ancient wisdom tradition and that the apparent differences between them are the result of cultural adaptation rather than genuine theological disagreement. Blavatsky explicitly identified Lucifer as the "light-bearer" who brought wisdom to humanity — a position that reveals the true spiritual orientation of the Theosophical system and its New Age descendants.</p>
<p>Alice Bailey, Blavatsky's successor, established the Lucis Trust (originally the Lucifer Publishing Company) in 1922. Bailey's writings describe a plan for the establishment of a new world religion, guided by a hierarchy of spiritual masters, that would replace all existing religions with a unified system of spiritual practice aligned with the "new age" of human evolution.</p>

<p>The theological absurdity of Chrislam — Islam explicitly denies the divinity of Christ and the Trinity, which are foundational Christian doctrines — is precisely the point. The one-world religion does not require theological coherence; it requires the abandonment of theological distinctives. By persuading Christians to worship alongside Muslims and treat Islamic scripture as spiritually equivalent to the Bible, the Chrislam movement accomplishes the central objective: the dissolution of Christian exclusivism.</p>
