<h2 id="what-is-5g">What Is 5G?</h2>
<p>Fifth-generation wireless technology (5G) is the latest standard for cellular networks, designed to deliver dramatically faster data speeds, lower latency, and the capacity to connect billions of devices simultaneously. 5G operates across three frequency bands:</p>
<p><strong>Low-band 5G</strong> (600 MHz to 1 GHz) — similar to 4G frequencies, offering modest speed improvements with wide coverage area. This is the least controversial band.</p>
<p><strong>Mid-band 5G</strong> (1 GHz to 6 GHz) — the "sweet spot" that most carriers are deploying, offering a balance of speed and coverage. The 3.5 GHz band is particularly important for 5G deployment.</p>
<p><strong>High-band 5G / Millimeter Wave (mmWave)</strong> (24 GHz to 100 GHz) — the most distinctive and controversial aspect of 5G. Millimeter waves deliver enormous data speeds but have very short range, requiring dense networks of small cell antennas — one every 100-300 meters in urban areas.</p>
<p>The mmWave deployment is what distinguishes 5G from previous generations. It requires an unprecedented density of antenna installations — on streetlights, utility poles, buildings, and purpose-built structures. Urban areas will be blanketed with thousands of small cell antennas, each broadcasting millimeter wave radiation continuously.</p>
<p>This is not an incremental change. It is a fundamental transformation of the electromagnetic environment in which human beings live.</p>
