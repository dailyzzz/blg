<h4 id="casimir-funk-and-the-vitamine-myth">Casimir Funk and the "Vitamine" Myth</h4>
<p>Casimir Funk, who coined the term "vitamine" (later shortened to "vitamin") in 1912, was working at the Lister Institute in London. His theory was that diseases like beriberi, scurvy, pellagra, and rickets were caused by deficiencies of specific chemical compounds in the diet. This theory — the "deficiency disease" model — became the foundation of modern nutrition science.</p>
<p>But Funk's model contained a fundamental flaw that was never corrected because correcting it would have destroyed a trillion-dollar industry. The flaw is this: <strong>the diseases attributed to vitamin deficiencies are caused by poor overall nutrition and toxic environmental conditions, not by the absence of specific isolated compounds.</strong></p>
<p>Beriberi, for example, appeared in Asian populations that ate polished white rice — rice stripped of its bran, germ, and most of its nutritional value. The solution was not to take a Vitamin B1 pill. The solution was to eat whole rice, or better yet, a diverse diet of whole foods. But the industrial solution — the profitable solution — was to strip the food, then sell back the missing nutrients as supplements.</p>
<p>This is the Rockefeller model in miniature: create the problem, then sell the solution.</p>

<h2 id="the-deficiency-disease-fraud">The Deficiency Disease Fraud</h2>
<h3 id="scurvy-the-foundation-myth">Scurvy: The Foundation Myth</h3>
<p>The scurvy story is the cornerstone of the vitamin narrative: sailors deprived of fresh fruits and vegetables developed a horrifying disease, and Vitamin C was the cure. But the actual history is more complicated — and more revealing.</p>
<p>James Lind's famous 1747 experiment on HMS Salisbury did show that citrus fruits helped scurvy patients. But Lind never claimed to have discovered a specific chemical compound. He simply observed that certain foods helped and others didn't. The leap from "eat fresh fruits" to "take ascorbic acid pills" was made over 150 years later, and it was made not by doctors seeking to help patients but by chemists seeking to create products.</p>
<p>Moreover, scurvy appeared primarily in populations eating terrible diets overall — sailors eating hardtack and salt pork for months on end, prison populations eating gruel, industrial workers eating the cheapest possible food. These populations were deficient not just in "Vitamin C" but in everything. Attributing their illness to a single missing compound was a reductionist fiction that served industrial purposes.</p>
<h3 id="pellagra-the-poverty-disease-rebranded">Pellagra: The Poverty Disease Rebranded</h3>
<p>Pellagra — a disease characterized by dermatitis, diarrhea, dementia, and death — was epidemic in the American South in the early 20th century. It was eventually attributed to niacin (Vitamin B3) deficiency. But the real cause of pellagra was poverty: Southern sharecroppers and mill workers ate a diet consisting almost entirely of cornmeal, molasses, and fatback, because that was all they could afford.</p>
<p>The "discovery" that niacin cured pellagra allowed the establishment to medicalize a social problem. Instead of addressing the crushing poverty and exploitation that caused the disease, they simply fortified flour with synthetic niacin and declared victory. The poverty continued. The exploitation continued. But the embarrassing disease disappeared, and the vitamin industry gained another success story.</p>
<h3 id="rickets-sunlight-not-supplements">Rickets: Sunlight, Not Supplements</h3>
<p>Rickets — a disease of bone deformity in children — was attributed to Vitamin D deficiency. But rickets was overwhelmingly a disease of industrialization: children working in factories and living in sunless urban slums developed it at epidemic rates. Children who lived in rural areas with access to sunlight rarely developed it.</p>
<p>The solution was sunlight and outdoor living. The industrial solution was Vitamin D fortification of milk — a process that allowed the dairy industry to market an otherwise declining product as essential for children's health. Once again, a social problem (child labor, urban poverty, industrial exploitation) was rebranded as a nutritional deficiency and solved with a synthetic additive.</p>
