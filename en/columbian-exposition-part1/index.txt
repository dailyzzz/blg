<h2 id="the-impossible-timeline">The Impossible Timeline</h2>
<h3 id="built-in-two-years">"Built" in Two Years?</h3>
<p>According to the official narrative, the World's Columbian Exposition — a complex of over 200 buildings spread across 690 acres, featuring the largest buildings ever constructed at that time, connected by canals, railways, and an elaborate electrical grid — was designed, engineered, and built from scratch in approximately two years (1891-1893).</p>
<p>Consider what this claim requires you to believe:</p>
<p>The <strong>Manufactures and Liberal Arts Building</strong> was the largest building in the world at the time, covering 44 acres with a single roof. Its main hall measured 1,687 by 787 feet and was topped by a steel-and-glass roof that rose 245 feet above the floor. According to the official story, this building was designed, engineered, and constructed in less than 18 months.</p>
<p>The <strong>Administration Building</strong>, designed by Richard Morris Hunt, featured a dome that rivaled the United States Capitol in size and sophistication. Its ornate facades, classical columns, and elaborate interior decoration would require years of skilled craftsmanship under normal circumstances. Yet supposedly it was completed from foundation to finished dome in about a year.</p>
<p>The <strong>Court of Honor</strong>, the central architectural ensemble, featured monumental neoclassical buildings arranged around a formal basin of water, with the 65-foot-tall "Statue of the Republic" at one end. The level of sculptural detail, the uniformity of design, and the sheer scale of construction would challenge modern contractors with modern equipment. In the 1890s, with hand tools, horse-drawn carts, and manual labor, the claimed timeline is not just ambitious — it is physically impossible.</p>
<h3 id="the-staff-explanation">The "Staff" Explanation</h3>
<p>The official explanation for the impossible timeline is "staff" — a mixture of plaster, cement, and jute fiber that was applied over wooden frames to create the appearance of stone. The White City, we are told, was essentially a movie set: real-looking but temporary, designed to impress but not to last.</p>
<p>This explanation has several problems:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p><strong>Photographs</strong> of the exposition show buildings with architectural details — carved capitals, fluted columns, ornate cornices, sculptural reliefs — that would require months of skilled work to produce even in staff. The sheer volume of decorative work visible in the photographs is inconsistent with a two-year construction timeline.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Engineering</strong> — even temporary buildings of this scale require substantial foundations, structural steel, and sophisticated engineering. The Manufactures Building alone used 12 million pounds of steel. Fabricating, transporting, and erecting that much steel in 1891-1892, while simultaneously constructing dozens of other major buildings, is a logistical feat that stretches credibility.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>The electrical system</strong> — the exposition was one of the first large-scale demonstrations of alternating current electricity (the famous Tesla/Westinghouse vs. Edison battle was fought here). The electrical infrastructure required to illuminate the White City — 100,000 incandescent lamps, plus arc lights, electric motors for exhibits, and power for the elevated railway — was vastly more complex than anything that had existed before. Designing and installing this system while simultaneously constructing the buildings it served, in under two years, is a claim that should be treated with extreme skepticism.</p>
</li>
</ol>
