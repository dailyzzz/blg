15:T36ec,<h1 id="the-insect-diet-great-reset-on-your-plate">The Insect Diet: Great Reset on Your Plate</h1>
<h2 id="the-ancient-pattern">The Ancient Pattern</h2>
<blockquote>
<p>"And God blessed them, and God said unto them, Be fruitful, and multiply, and replenish the earth, and subdue it: and have dominion over the fish of the sea, and over the fowl of the air, and over every living thing that moveth upon the earth." — Genesis 1:28</p>
</blockquote>
<p>God gave humanity dominion over creation — and with it, the authority to eat abundantly from the earth's provision. After the Flood, God explicitly expanded this provision: "Every moving thing that liveth shall be meat for you; even as the green herb have I given you all things" (Genesis 9:3). The diet God prescribed was abundant, varied, and dignified — cattle, fish, fowl, grains, fruits, and vegetables. The Great Reset's proposed diet of insects, lab-grown meat substitutes, and engineered protein slurries is not an upgrade. It is a deliberate degradation — a reversal of the dominion mandate, designed to reduce humanity from the masters of creation to the consumers of its lowest-status organisms.</p>
<p>As the Confliction album track <a href="https://confliction.zzzdaily.com/tracks/en/43-Hunger_and_Thirst.mp4">Hunger and Thirst</a> warns, the corruption of God's provision is one of the serpent's most persistent strategies. The insect diet is the latest iteration: replacing what God gave abundantly with what the technocrats allocate grudgingly.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="the-agenda">The Agenda</h2>
<p>The push to replace traditional protein sources with insects is not a grassroots movement. It is a top-down initiative coordinated by the world's most powerful institutions:</p>
<p><strong>The World Economic Forum</strong> has published numerous articles, reports, and white papers promoting insect protein as a sustainable alternative to conventional animal agriculture. A 2021 WEF article titled "5 Reasons Why Eating Insects Could Reduce Climate Change" epitomizes the messaging: insects are framed as environmentally virtuous, protein-efficient, and necessary for the survival of the planet.</p>
<p><strong>The European Union</strong> approved four insect species for human consumption between 2021 and 2023: dried yellow mealworm, migratory locust, house cricket, and lesser mealworm. These approvals allow insect-derived ingredients to be included in a wide range of food products — bread, pasta, biscuits, sauces, and processed foods — without prominent labeling in some cases.</p>
<p><strong>The United Nations Food and Agriculture Organization</strong> published a 200-page report in 2013 titled "Edible Insects: Future Prospects for Food and Feed Security," which laid the institutional groundwork for promoting insects as a food source in both developing and developed nations.</p>
<p><strong>The Bill &#x26; Melinda Gates Foundation</strong> has invested in insect farming companies and alternative protein startups. Gates himself has publicly stated that "rich countries should move to 100% synthetic beef."</p>
<p><strong>Investment capital</strong> has flooded the insect farming industry. Global market research firms project the edible insect market to grow from approximately $1 billion in 2023 to over $8 billion by 2030. Major companies including Nestle, PepsiCo, and Tyson have invested in insect protein and alternative protein ventures.</p>
<p>The message is coordinated, the funding is enormous, and the regulatory path has been cleared. This is not consumer demand driving innovation. This is institutional engineering of a new food system.</p>
<h2 id="the-health-questions-nobody-asks">The Health Questions Nobody Asks</h2>
<p>The safety of long-term insect consumption by Western populations that have no evolutionary history of eating insects is essentially unstudied:</p>
<p><strong>Chitin.</strong> Insects contain chitin — a structural polysaccharide that forms the exoskeleton of arthropods. Humans produce chitinase, an enzyme that breaks down chitin, but the effectiveness of this enzyme varies significantly between individuals and populations. Chitin has been associated with allergic responses and inflammatory activation in some studies. The long-term effects of regular chitin consumption on the gut lining, immune system, and inflammatory pathways are unknown.</p>
<p><strong>Allergenicity.</strong> Insects are arthropods, closely related to crustaceans (shrimp, crab, lobster) and arachnids (spiders, mites). Cross-reactivity between insect proteins and shellfish allergens has been documented, meaning that individuals with shellfish allergies may be at risk of severe allergic reactions to insect-derived food products. A 2019 study found that 30% of individuals with shellfish allergies also reacted to mealworm protein.</p>
<p><strong>Parasites and pathogens.</strong> A 2019 study published in the journal PLOS ONE examined samples from insect farms and found parasites in 81% of insect farms, including parasites pathogenic to humans. While processing (drying, roasting, grinding) reduces pathogen risk, the insect farming industry's quality control and safety standards are in their infancy compared to conventional food production.</p>
<p><strong>Anti-nutrients.</strong> Insects contain anti-nutrients including phytic acid and oxalic acid that can interfere with mineral absorption. Given that mineral deficiency is already epidemic (as documented in <a href="/soil-depletion-mineral-crisis/?lang=en">Soil Depletion: The Mineral Crisis</a>), adding foods that further impair mineral absorption to the diet is a step in precisely the wrong direction.</p>
<p><strong>Heavy metals and contaminants.</strong> Insects bioaccumulate heavy metals and pesticides from their feed and environment. As documented in <a href="/heavy-metals-food-supply/?lang=en">Heavy Metals in the Food Supply</a>, the food chain concentrates contaminants at every level. Insects raised on agricultural waste or industrial feedstocks can contain significant levels of heavy metals, pesticide residues, and other contaminants.</p>
<h2 id="you-will-own-nothing-and-eat-bugs">You Will Own Nothing and Eat Bugs</h2>
<p>The insect diet agenda does not exist in isolation. It is one component of the World Economic Forum's "Great Reset" vision, as documented in our article on <a href="/great-reset-fourth-industrial-revolution/?lang=en">The Great Reset: The Fourth Industrial Revolution</a>.</p>
<p>The Great Reset envisions a world in which:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Private property is minimized</strong> — "You'll own nothing and you'll be happy" (WEF promotional video, 2016)</li>
<li><strong>Meat consumption is drastically reduced</strong> — replaced by insects, lab-grown alternatives, and plant-based substitutes</li>
<li><strong>Energy use is rationed</strong> — through carbon credits, smart meters, and digital tracking</li>
<li><strong>Movement is restricted</strong> — through 15-minute cities, digital IDs, and geofencing</li>
<li><strong>Financial transactions are controlled</strong> — through CBDCs and social credit systems</li>
</ul>
<p>The food transition is inseparable from the broader agenda of control. When you cannot raise your own cattle, grow your own food, or buy unapproved products with unapproved money, the question of what you eat is no longer a personal choice — it is an institutional allocation.</p>
<p>As documented in our article on <a href="/agenda-2030-un-global-governance/?lang=en">Agenda 2030: UN Global Governance</a>, the UN's Sustainable Development Goals provide the framework for this transition. SDG 2 (Zero Hunger) and SDG 12 (Responsible Consumption) are being interpreted to justify the restructuring of global food systems away from traditional agriculture and toward centralized, technology-dependent production — of which insect farming is the most visible symbol.</p>
<h2 id="lab-grown-meat-the-other-alternative">Lab-Grown Meat: The Other "Alternative"</h2>
<p>Alongside insect protein, the institutional agenda promotes lab-grown (cultured) meat as a sustainable alternative to conventional animal agriculture. As documented in our article on <a href="/lab-grown-meat-food-future/?lang=en">Lab-Grown Meat: The Food Future They're Engineering</a>, cultured meat is produced by harvesting stem cells from animals and growing them in bioreactors using growth media — a process that is energy-intensive, dependent on pharmaceutical-grade inputs, and controlled by a handful of venture capital-backed companies.</p>
<p>The environmental claims made for lab-grown meat are contested. A 2023 study by researchers at UC Davis found that the carbon footprint of lab-grown meat, when energy inputs and growth media production are fully accounted for, could be 4-25 times greater than conventional beef production. The "sustainable" alternative may be less sustainable than what it replaces.</p>
<p>But sustainability was never the point. Control is the point. Lab-grown meat, like insect protein, centralizes food production in facilities controlled by a small number of corporations, removes the farmer from the food system, and makes the population dependent on technologies it cannot replicate, maintain, or opt out of.</p>
<h2 id="the-degradation-of-human-dignity">The Degradation of Human Dignity</h2>
<p>There is a dimension to the insect diet agenda that transcends nutrition, economics, and environmental policy: it is an assault on human dignity.</p>
<p>Throughout history, cultures that consumed insects did so because they lacked alternatives — in desert environments, during famine, or in contexts where insect consumption was integrated into millennia of cultural practice. The promotion of insect consumption to affluent Western populations that have abundant access to high-quality animal protein is not a response to scarcity. It is an imposition of scarcity — a psychological operation designed to condition populations to accept degraded living standards, reduced expectations, and diminished self-worth.</p>
<p>The elites promoting insect consumption do not eat insects. The delegates at Davos dine on steak, lobster, and fine wine. The insects are for you. The reduced expectations are for you. The degradation is for you. They will continue to eat as they always have — because the rules of the Great Reset, like all authoritarian systems, apply to the subjects, not the sovereigns.</p>
<p>As documented in <a href="/food-pyramid-lie-decades/?lang=en">The Food Pyramid Lie</a>, the pattern of dietary degradation has been progressive: first, replace animal fats with seed oils; then, replace real food with processed food; then, replace meat with plant-based substitutes; and finally, replace even the plant-based substitutes with insects and lab-grown slurry. Each step moves further from what God provided and closer to what the technocrats allocate.</p>
