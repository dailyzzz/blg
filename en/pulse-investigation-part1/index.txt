<h2 id="the-official-story">The Official Story</h2>
<h3 id="what-you-were-told">What You Were Told</h3>
<p>The official narrative states that on June 12, 2016, a lone gunman entered Pulse nightclub in Orlando, Florida, during Latin Night and opened fire, killing 49 people and wounding 53 others before being killed by police approximately three hours later. The attacker was identified as Omar Mateen, a 29-year-old security guard who had been investigated by the FBI twice before the attack.</p>
<p>This narrative was established within hours of the event. Within days, it was hardened into accepted fact. Within weeks, it was being used to advance gun control legislation, surveillance expansion, and counter-terrorism policy. Within months, it was memorialized as the deadliest mass shooting in American history at that time.</p>
<p>The speed of narrative establishment is itself suspicious. Genuine investigations take time. The Warren Commission took ten months. The 9/11 Commission took twenty. Yet the Pulse narrative was complete before the bodies were identified.</p>

<ul>
<li>Why was a man investigated twice by the FBI for potential terrorist ties still able to purchase weapons and maintain a security clearance?</li>
<li>Why did it take police three hours to enter the building?</li>
<li>Why were witnesses reporting multiple shooters in the immediate aftermath, before the single-shooter narrative was established?</li>
<li>Why was the building demolished before independent investigators could examine the physical evidence?</li>
<li>How did a single person achieve the reported casualty count in the reported timeframe with the reported weapons?</li>
<li>Why were the 911 call transcripts initially redacted to remove references to the attacker's stated motivations?</li>
</ul>
<p>Each of these questions has an institutional answer. None of the institutional answers are satisfactory. The investigation begins here — with the questions the system does not want asked.</p>
<h2 id="the-fbi-connection">The FBI Connection</h2>
<h3 id="two-prior-investigations">Two Prior Investigations</h3>
<p>The FBI investigated Omar Mateen twice — in 2013 and 2014 — for potential terrorist connections. Both investigations were closed. After the Pulse event, the FBI claimed that the investigations found insufficient evidence to maintain surveillance.</p>
<p>This pattern — the FBI investigating a subject who later commits a major attack — is not unique to Pulse. It has occurred in multiple high-profile events, creating a pattern that either represents catastrophic institutional failure or something more troubling: institutional awareness that failed to prevent (or was not intended to prevent) the event.</p>
<p>The FBI's relationship with subjects of investigation is complicated by its extensive use of informants and undercover operatives. The Bureau has a documented history of facilitating the very plots it claims to disrupt — providing weapons, encouragement, and logistical support to individuals it is ostensibly investigating. Congressional hearings have documented cases where FBI informants provided everything needed for an attack, then arrested the subject just before execution.</p>
<p>Whether this pattern applies to Pulse is a question the investigation must explore. The institutional resistance to exploring it — the media's refusal to ask, the government's refusal to disclose — is itself evidence that the question is dangerous to the official narrative.</p>
<h3 id="the-security-clearance-anomaly">The Security Clearance Anomaly</h3>
<p>Mateen worked for G4S, one of the world's largest private security companies, with contracts across government and military sectors. He held a security clearance despite two FBI investigations. This anomaly has never been adequately explained.</p>
<p>In the security industry, an FBI investigation is typically grounds for clearance revocation. The fact that Mateen retained his clearance through two investigations suggests either catastrophic bureaucratic failure or institutional decision-making that deemed his continued employment and access more valuable than the risk he posed.</p>
<p>Howard Pittman's testimony about institutional deception applies directly. The institutions tasked with protecting the public — the FBI, the security industry, the intelligence community — failed in ways that are either inexplicably incompetent or deliberately planned. The 97.5 percent rejection rate reflects a population that trusts institutions without examining their failures. The Pulse investigation examines those failures.</p>
<h2 id="the-three-hour-gap">The Three-Hour Gap</h2>
<h3 id="why-did-police-wait">Why Did Police Wait?</h3>
<p>The attack began at approximately 2:02 AM. Police first made entry into the building at approximately 5:02 AM — a three-hour gap during which, according to the official timeline, the attacker had access to the victims inside the building.</p>
<p>During those three hours, wounded victims bled. Some bled to death. The three-hour gap is one of the most disturbing aspects of the Pulse event, and the institutional explanation — that police followed "active shooter" protocols that required assessment before entry — contradicts the "active shooter" protocols established after Columbine, which mandate immediate entry to stop the killing.</p>
<p>The three-hour gap raises questions that this investigation will pursue in subsequent parts. What happened inside during those three hours? Why were the established protocols not followed? Who made the decision to wait, and on what authority?</p>
