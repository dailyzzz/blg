<h2 id="what-trumprx-actually-does">What TrumpRx Actually Does</h2>
<h3 id="the-price-reduction-mechanism">The Price Reduction Mechanism</h3>
<p>TrumpRx uses Most Favored Nation pricing and government negotiation authority to reduce the prices Americans pay for pharmaceutical products. This addresses a genuine injustice: Americans have long paid the highest drug prices in the world, subsidizing pharmaceutical company profits while other nations negotiate lower prices.</p>
<p>The price reduction is real and meaningful for many Americans who struggle to afford necessary medications. This is the gift — tangible, valuable, and politically popular. The question is what comes with the gift.</p>
<h3 id="the-hidden-costs">The Hidden Costs</h3>
<p>Lower prices come at costs not visible on the price tag. The pharmaceutical industry does not voluntarily reduce its revenue — it restructures its revenue sources. When consumer prices decrease, pharmaceutical companies seek compensation through expanded volume (more products to more people), expanded indications (approving existing products for new conditions), reduced regulatory burden (faster approvals with less safety data), and expanded government purchasing (guaranteed markets funded by taxpayer dollars).</p>
<p>Each of these compensations advances the pharmaceutical control agenda. More products to more people means deeper pharmaceutical dependency. Faster approvals mean less safety scrutiny. Government purchasing means pharmaceutical revenue guaranteed by law. The net result may be lower prices for individual prescriptions but greater pharmaceutical penetration of American healthcare — and American bodies.</p>
<p>Karen Kingston has documented how pharmaceutical pricing negotiations consistently produce outcomes that appear to benefit consumers while expanding pharmaceutical power. The consumer sees lower prices. The industry sees expanded markets, reduced oversight, and guaranteed revenue — a far more valuable trade.</p>
