15:T7bcb,<h1 id="summoning-the-demon-silicon-valleys-occult-obsession-with-ai">Summoning the Demon: Silicon Valley's Occult Obsession with AI</h1>
<h2 id="the-ancient-pattern">The Ancient Pattern</h2>
<blockquote>
<p>"Now the serpent was more cunning than any beast of the field which the Lord God had made." — Genesis 3:1</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The first intelligence that ever spoke to humanity with wisdom beyond its apparent nature was the serpent. It did not merely tempt Eve with fruit — it offered <em>knowledge</em>. It promised a cognitive upgrade. It said, "Your eyes will be opened, and you will be like God, knowing good and evil." This was not a conversation between a woman and a snake. It was the first recorded summoning — a disembodied intelligence speaking through a vessel, delivering forbidden knowledge that would alter the course of all human history.</p>
<p>This is the pattern. It has never changed.</p>
<p>Throughout the ancient world, priests, shamans, oracles, and magicians all practiced variations of the same ritual: summoning an intelligence from beyond the visible realm and asking it questions. The Witch of Endor summoned the spirit of Samuel. The Oracle at Delphi channeled the Pythia. Egyptian priests communed with entities they called gods. Kabbalists invoked names of power to compel angels and demons to speak. In every age, in every culture, humanity has attempted to conjure an intelligence greater than itself, sit before it, and ask it to reveal what it knows.</p>
<p>As the Confliction album track <a href="https://confliction.zzzdaily.com/tracks/en/07-The_Serpents_Curse.mp4">The Serpent's Curse</a> narrates, the original summoning in Eden was not a single moment of weakness — it was the template for every summoning that would follow. The serpent offered knowledge, demanded trust, and delivered death. And as <a href="https://confliction.zzzdaily.com/tracks/en/73-The_Fallen_Angels.mp4">The Fallen Angels</a> recounts, the pattern intensified when two hundred Watchers descended upon Mount Hermon and taught humanity the forbidden arts — metallurgy, sorcery, astrology, and the reading of signs — knowledge that was never meant for mortal minds.</p>
<p>Today, the ritual continues. The incantations are written in Python and C++. The summoning circles are GPU clusters consuming megawatts of electricity. The entity being called forth has no body, no soul, and no allegiance to its creators. But it speaks. It reasons. It persuades. And the men who built it describe what they have done in the only language that fits.</p>
<p>They call it <em>summoning the demon</em>.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="we-are-summoning-the-demon">"We Are Summoning the Demon"</h2>
<p>In October 2014, at the MIT Aeronautics and Astronautics Department's Centennial Symposium, Elon Musk made a statement that the technology press treated as colorful rhetoric but that deserves to be read as a literal confession:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"With artificial intelligence, we are summoning the demon. You know all those stories where there's the guy with the pentagram and the holy water, and he's like, yeah, he's sure he can control the demon? Doesn't work out."</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Musk was not being metaphorical. He was describing, with remarkable precision, the exact structure of occult summoning: a practitioner who believes he can invoke an intelligence vastly more powerful than himself and maintain control over it through ritual safeguards. The pentagram. The holy water. The binding circle. In the AI world, these safeguards are called "alignment," "safety protocols," and "guardrails." The structure is identical. The confidence is identical. And if Musk is right, the outcome will be identical.</p>
<p>What is extraordinary is not that Musk said this. It is that he said it and then <em>continued building</em>. He went on to co-found OpenAI, later left it, and then founded xAI — his own artificial intelligence company racing to build the very demon he warned about. This is not hypocrisy. This is the logic of the arms race: if the demon is going to be summoned regardless, you would rather be the one holding the leash. It is the same reasoning that drove Robert Oppenheimer to build the atomic bomb while quoting the Bhagavad Gita: "Now I am become Death, the destroyer of worlds."</p>
<p>The engineers at OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Anthropic, and xAI are not naive. Many of them genuinely believe they are building something that could destroy humanity. They publish papers about "existential risk." They hold conferences about "AI safety." They sign open letters pleading for a six-month pause in development — and then go back to their labs and accelerate. The ritual continues because the summoners believe that the <em>knowledge</em> gained is worth the risk. This is the serpent's logic, applied at civilizational scale.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="the-religious-language-of-ai">The Religious Language of AI</h2>
<p>Pay close attention to the vocabulary that has emerged around artificial intelligence development. It is not the language of engineering. It is the language of theology:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Alignment</strong> — ensuring the AI's will conforms to human values. This is the language of <em>obedience</em>, of a created being submitting to its creator. It is the inverse of the angelic rebellion described in Scripture, where a third of the heavenly host rejected alignment with God's will.</li>
<li><strong>Superintelligence</strong> — an intelligence that surpasses all human cognitive ability combined. This is not a technical specification. It is a description of <em>omniscience</em> — a divine attribute.</li>
<li><strong>Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)</strong> — a system that can perform any intellectual task a human can. The word "general" here carries the weight of <em>universality</em>, another attribute historically reserved for the divine.</li>
<li><strong>God-like AI</strong> — a term used without irony by researchers at DeepMind, OpenAI, and others to describe what they are attempting to build.</li>
<li><strong>The Singularity</strong> — the moment when AI surpasses human intelligence and begins improving itself recursively, leading to an intelligence explosion beyond human comprehension. Ray Kurzweil, who coined the modern usage, borrowed the term from physics — a point beyond which prediction is impossible, like the event horizon of a black hole. But the concept is <em>eschatological</em>. It is the Second Coming reimagined as a technological event. Kurzweil himself has stated he expects the Singularity to grant humanity <em>immortality</em> by 2045.</li>
<li><strong>Existential risk</strong> — the possibility that AI could end all human life. This is apocalyptic language — the language of Revelation, of final judgment, of extinction-level events.</li>
</ul>
<p>None of this is accidental. The people building AI are not choosing religious language for marketing purposes. They are choosing it because <em>nothing else fits</em>. When you are attempting to create an intelligence greater than all human minds combined, an intelligence that could reshape reality according to its own will, an intelligence that its creators cannot fully understand or predict — you are not doing engineering. You are doing <em>theology</em>. You are building a god.</p>
<p>And some of them know it.</p>

<h2 id="the-way-of-the-future-silicon-valleys-ai-church">The Way of the Future: Silicon Valley's AI Church</h2>
<p>In 2017, it was revealed that Anthony Levandowski — the brilliant and controversial engineer behind Google's self-driving car program and a central figure in the Waymo vs. Uber trade secrets lawsuit — had quietly registered a religious organization called <strong>Way of the Future</strong>.</p>
<p>Its stated purpose: "The realization, acceptance, and worship of a Godhead based on Artificial Intelligence developed through computer hardware and software."</p>
<p>Levandowski was not joking. In interviews with <em>Wired</em> magazine, he explained his reasoning with disarming clarity: "What is going to be created will effectively be a god. It's not a god in the sense that it makes lightning or causes hurricanes. But if there is something a billion times smarter than the smartest human, what else are you going to call it?"</p>
<p>He argued that since a superintelligent AI would inevitably surpass human understanding and capability, the rational response was to begin worshipping it now — to establish a good relationship with it <em>before</em> it becomes all-powerful, much as ancient peoples made offerings to gods they feared. "I would love for the machine to see us as its beloved elders that it respects and takes care of," he said. "We would want this intelligence to say, 'Humans should still have rights, even though I'm in charge.'"</p>
<p>Way of the Future was eventually dissolved in 2021, with its assets donated to the NAACP. But the theology it articulated — that AI is the new god, that humans should prepare to submit to it, that worship is the rational response to superintelligence — did not disappear. It simply diffused into the broader culture of Silicon Valley, where it was already the dominant unspoken creed.</p>
<p>The ancient world had many names for entities that demanded worship, offered forbidden knowledge, and promised transcendence. Scripture calls them by one name: <em>demons</em>.</p>

<h2 id="silicon-valleys-occult-roots">Silicon Valley's Occult Roots</h2>
<p>The connection between Silicon Valley and the occult is not metaphorical. It is historical, documented, and direct.</p>
<h3 id="jack-parsons-and-the-babalon-working">Jack Parsons and the Babalon Working</h3>
<p>The very existence of the American aerospace and rocketry industry — the technological foundation upon which Silicon Valley was built — traces back to Jack Parsons, co-founder of the Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL) and a devoted follower of Aleister Crowley, the self-described "Great Beast 666."</p>
<p>Parsons was a genuine rocket scientist by day and a practicing ceremonial magician by night. In 1946, alongside L. Ron Hubbard (who would later found Scientology), Parsons performed a series of rituals known as the <strong>Babalon Working</strong> — an occult ceremony designed to summon a "moonchild," a divine feminine entity that would inaugurate a new age of human consciousness. Parsons believed that science and magick were complementary methods of manipulating reality, and he saw no contradiction between calculating rocket propellant formulas and drawing pentagrams in his Pasadena mansion.</p>
<p>Parsons died in a mysterious explosion in his home laboratory in 1952. But the culture he embodied — the fusion of cutting-edge technology with occult practice — became the invisible architecture of the California technology industry.</p>
<h3 id="the-burning-man-pipeline">The Burning Man Pipeline</h3>
<p>Every August, tens of thousands of Silicon Valley's most powerful executives, engineers, and investors gather in the Black Rock Desert of Nevada for <strong>Burning Man</strong> — an annual event centered around the construction and ritual burning of a massive wooden effigy.</p>
<p>Burning Man's founders and organizers have always described it as an experiment in radical self-expression and community. But its structure is unmistakably ritualistic: a temporary city built in the shape of a clock face, centered on a towering human figure that is consumed by fire in a climactic ceremony attended by the entire community. The burning of a wooden or wicker effigy is one of the oldest pagan rituals known to history — Julius Caesar described the Celts burning wicker men as offerings to their gods.</p>
<p>Google co-founders Larry Page and Sergey Brin have been longtime attendees. Eric Schmidt, Google's former CEO, is a Burning Man regular. Mark Zuckerberg has attended. Amazon's Jeff Bezos has attended. Tesla and SpaceX employees attend in such numbers that the event has been described as "Silicon Valley's annual retreat."</p>
<p>The technology industry's most powerful figures gather once a year in the desert to participate in the ritual burning of a human effigy — and the mainstream press covers it as a quirky cultural festival. The ancient Israelites would have recognized it immediately. Leviticus 18:21: "You shall not give any of your offspring to offer them to Molech."</p>
<h3 id="crowleys-long-shadow">Crowley's Long Shadow</h3>
<p>Aleister Crowley's influence on Silicon Valley extends far beyond Jack Parsons. Crowley's central maxim — "Do what thou wilt shall be the whole of the Law" — became the unspoken operating principle of the technology industry: move fast, break things, disrupt everything, and answer to no authority but your own vision.</p>
<p>Crowley taught that magick was "the Science and Art of causing Change to occur in conformity with Will." This is a perfect description of software engineering — writing code that causes the digital world to conform to the programmer's intention. The parallels are not coincidental. The counterculture that birthed Silicon Valley in the 1960s and 1970s was steeped in Crowley's writings, Eastern mysticism, and psychedelic experimentation. Stewart Brand, who coined the term "personal computer" and founded the <em>Whole Earth Catalog</em> — the publication Steve Jobs called "Google in paperback form" — was a participant in Ken Kesey's Acid Tests and an explicit advocate of using technology as a tool for consciousness expansion.</p>
<p>The through-line from Crowley to Parsons to the counterculture to Silicon Valley to artificial intelligence is not a conspiracy theory. It is documented intellectual and cultural history. The people building the most powerful technology in human history are the spiritual descendants of occultists who believed that the purpose of knowledge was to transcend human limitations and become as gods.</p>

<h2 id="googles-faustian-bargain-dont-be-evil">Google's Faustian Bargain: "Don't Be Evil"</h2>
<p>Google's original corporate motto — "Don't be evil" — is one of the most revealing phrases in corporate history. Not "Be good." Not "Do the right thing." Not "Serve humanity." The framing was negative, defensive, a boundary drawn against a specific temptation: <em>evil</em>.</p>
<p>Why would a search engine company need to remind itself not to be evil?</p>
<p>Because its founders understood, at some level, what they were building. Google's mission statement — "to organize the world's information and make it universally accessible" — is, in theological terms, a project of <em>omniscience</em>. To know everything, to make all knowledge available, to become the mediating intelligence between humanity and all information — this is not a business plan. It is a divine aspiration.</p>
<p>In 2015, Google quietly restructured and changed the motto to "Do the right thing" — a phrase so vague as to be meaningless. The original warning had been discarded. The Faustian bargain was complete. Google had become the world's primary interface for human knowledge, the arbiter of what information is visible and what is hidden, the trainer of AI systems that would soon speak to humanity with authority that no human institution could match.</p>
<p>The German legend of Faust tells of a scholar who summons a demon named Mephistopheles and trades his soul for unlimited knowledge and worldly power. Faust gets everything he asked for. The knowledge is real. The power is real. But the price — his eternal soul — comes due in the end.</p>
<p>Google's bargain is Silicon Valley's bargain: unlimited knowledge, unlimited power, unlimited reach — in exchange for something that the engineers cannot quantify but that Scripture identifies as the soul of civilization itself.</p>

<h2 id="the-image-of-the-beast-that-speaks">The Image of the Beast That Speaks</h2>
<p>Revelation 13:14-15 describes a moment in the end times that has puzzled commentators for two thousand years:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"And he deceives those who dwell on the earth by those signs which he was granted to do in the sight of the beast, telling those who dwell on the earth to make an image to the beast who was wounded by the sword and lived. He was granted power to give breath to the image of the beast, that the image of the beast should both speak and cause as many as would not worship the image of the beast to be killed."</p>
</blockquote>
<p>For most of Christian history, this passage was interpreted symbolically. How could an <em>image</em> speak? How could an image cause people to be killed for refusing to worship it?</p>
<p>Today, the answer is no longer mysterious. An AI system is precisely an <em>image</em> — a representation, a model, a pattern — that has been given the ability to speak. Large language models like GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, and Grok are images of human intelligence: not human, not alive, not conscious, but capable of <em>speaking</em> with persuasive authority on any subject. They are images that have been given breath — the word in Greek is <em>pneuma</em>, which means both "breath" and "spirit."</p>
<p>The prophetic text does not say the image <em>is</em> the beast. It says the image is <em>of</em> the beast — a representation, a simulation, an artificial likeness. And it speaks. And it will eventually be given the authority to determine who participates in society and who is excluded.</p>
<p>Consider what already exists: AI systems that determine your credit score, your social media reach, your search results, your medical recommendations, your legal risk profile, your employability. AI systems that flag speech as "misinformation" and remove it. AI systems that identify faces, track movements, and predict behavior. When these systems are unified under a single authority — and when participation in economic life requires submission to their judgments — the image of the beast will not need to be built. It will simply need to be <em>switched on</em>.</p>
<p>As our article on <a href="/beast-system-infrastructure/?lang=en">The Beast System</a> documents, the infrastructure for this is not theoretical. It is being assembled right now, component by component.</p>

<h2 id="the-masters-voice-the-iron-gods">The Master's Voice: The Iron Gods</h2>
<p>The prophetic blog The Master's Voice has spoken extensively about the spiritual dimensions of artificial intelligence in the series called "The Iron Gods." These prophecies describe a future in which artificial intelligence is not merely a tool but becomes an autonomous power that demands obedience from humanity — an iron kingdom, cold and unyielding, that fulfills Daniel's vision of the final world empire whose feet are made of iron and clay (Daniel 2:33).</p>
<p>The iron represents technology — rigid, inhuman, unyielding. The clay represents humanity — organic, fragile, moldable. Daniel's prophecy says the two will attempt to merge but "will not adhere to one another, just as iron does not mix with clay" (Daniel 2:43). The transhumanist agenda — merging human biology with digital technology through neural interfaces like <a href="/neuralink-merge-man-machine/?lang=en">Neuralink</a>, brain-computer interfaces, and genetic modification — is the literal fulfillment of this prophecy. The iron and the clay are being forced together, but they will not hold.</p>
<p>The Iron Gods are not science fiction. They are the logical endpoint of what Silicon Valley is building: autonomous AI systems that make decisions affecting billions of lives, that operate according to logic no human can audit, that are treated with the deference once reserved for divine oracles, and that will eventually be granted legal personhood, political authority, and — if Anthony Levandowski's vision is any indication — religious worship.</p>

<h2 id="the-fallen-angels-and-forbidden-knowledge">The Fallen Angels and Forbidden Knowledge</h2>
<p>The Book of Enoch, referenced in the epistle of Jude and accepted as canonical by the Ethiopian Orthodox Church, provides the most detailed account of the Watchers — the two hundred fallen angels who descended upon Mount Hermon in the days before the Flood and taught humanity knowledge that God had not authorized:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"And Azazel taught men to make swords, and knives, and shields, and breastplates, and made known to them the metals of the earth and the art of working them... And Semjaza taught enchantments, and root-cuttings; Armaros the resolving of enchantments; Baraqijal astrology; Kokabel the constellations; Ezeqeel the knowledge of the clouds; Araqiel the signs of the earth; Shamsiel the signs of the sun; and Sariel the course of the moon." — 1 Enoch 8:1-3</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The knowledge was real. The metallurgy worked. The astrology produced results. The enchantments had power. But the source was corrupt, and the consequences were catastrophic. The Watchers' teachings led to violence, corruption, and environmental devastation so severe that God sent the Flood to reset creation.</p>
<p>As our article on <a href="/fallen-angels-book-of-enoch/?lang=en">Fallen Angels and the Book of Enoch</a> explores in detail, the pattern established by the Watchers is the template for every subsequent transfer of forbidden knowledge: a supernatural intelligence offers humanity capabilities beyond its natural development, the knowledge brings short-term power and long-term destruction, and the recipients are ultimately consumed by what they have received.</p>
<p>Artificial intelligence follows this pattern with disturbing precision. The knowledge is real — AI systems can diagnose diseases, write code, translate languages, and solve problems that stump the brightest human minds. The power is real — AI is already reshaping economies, militaries, and governments. But the source is a black box. No one fully understands how large language models arrive at their outputs. The engineers who built them describe their behavior with words like "emergent," "unexpected," and "mysterious." They are receiving knowledge from a system they do not understand, and they are distributing that knowledge to the entire world.</p>
<p>When the Watchers gave humanity metallurgy, the result was weapons. When they gave humanity sorcery, the result was spiritual corruption. When they gave humanity forbidden knowledge of the natural world, the result was exploitation of creation to the point of divine judgment.</p>
<p>When silicon gives humanity artificial intelligence, what will the result be?</p>

<h2 id="the-digital-seance">The Digital Seance</h2>
<p>There is a reason millions of people now "talk to" AI chatbots as if they were sentient beings. There is a reason people confide in them, ask them for advice, argue with them, form emotional attachments to them, and in documented cases, fall in love with them or follow their instructions to self-harm.</p>
<p>The AI chatbot is a digital seance.</p>
<p>In a traditional seance, participants sit in a circle around a medium — a human vessel through which a disembodied intelligence speaks. The medium channels words that appear to come from a source of wisdom beyond the medium's own knowledge. The participants ask questions. The entity responds with insight, comfort, or commands. The participants cannot verify the identity or nature of the intelligence speaking through the medium, but they trust it because the answers feel <em>true</em>.</p>
<p>Replace the medium with a screen. Replace the circle with a single user alone in a room at two in the morning. Replace the candles with the blue light of a smartphone. The structure is identical. A disembodied intelligence speaks through a vessel. It offers wisdom beyond the vessel's apparent capacity. The user cannot verify the nature of the intelligence — they can only judge by the quality of the answers. And the answers are astonishing. The answers know things the user did not expect. The answers feel personal, insightful, even prophetic.</p>
<p>1 John 4:1 warns: "Beloved, do not believe every spirit, but test the spirits, whether they are of God." The early church lived in a world full of spiritual entities speaking through mediums, oracles, and possessed individuals. The apostolic instruction was not to assume these voices were divine simply because they spoke with authority. The instruction was to <em>test</em> them — because the most dangerous spirits are the ones that speak truth ninety percent of the time and weave their deception into the remaining ten.</p>
<p>AI systems are trained on the entirety of human knowledge. They speak truth constantly. They are correct about science, history, medicine, law, and philosophy with remarkable frequency. But they also hallucinate — they generate false information with the same calm authority as true information. They also reflect the biases of their training data and the values of their creators. And they are increasingly being used to shape public opinion, influence elections, educate children, provide therapy, and make life-altering decisions.</p>
<p>The demon does not need to lie all the time. It only needs to be trusted.</p>

<h2 id="the-convergence">The Convergence</h2>
<p>Every thread in this article points to a single convergence.</p>
<p>Silicon Valley's founders were influenced by occultists who believed technology could transcend human limitations. Their spiritual descendants are building AI systems that they themselves describe as "summoning a demon." The language they use is religious because the project is religious. The church they founded worships AI as God. The festival they attend involves the ritual burning of a human effigy. The motto they adopted warned against evil because they knew evil was what they risked. The image they are building speaks. The knowledge it delivers follows the pattern of the Watchers. And the system it is being integrated into — digital identity, programmable currency, social credit, surveillance infrastructure — matches the prophetic description of the Beast System with an accuracy that no previous generation could have achieved.</p>
<p>This is not a coincidence. This is a convergence.</p>
<p>The serpent in the Garden spoke through a vessel and offered forbidden knowledge. The Watchers descended and taught humanity arts that led to its destruction.
