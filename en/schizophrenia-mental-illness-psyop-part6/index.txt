<h2 id="the-invention-of-schizophrenia">The Invention of Schizophrenia</h2>
<h3 id="a-disease-without-a-cause">A Disease Without a Cause</h3>
<p>Schizophrenia was first described by Emil Kraepelin in 1893 (as "dementia praecox") and renamed by Eugen Bleuler in 1908. Despite over a century of research, the psychiatric establishment has failed to identify:</p>
<ul>
<li>A <strong>biological marker</strong> for schizophrenia (no blood test, brain scan, or genetic test can diagnose it)</li>
<li>A <strong>specific brain abnormality</strong> that causes schizophrenia (brain scans show statistical differences in populations but cannot diagnose individuals)</li>
<li>A <strong>genetic cause</strong> (despite massive funding, no "schizophrenia gene" has been found)</li>
<li>A <strong>chemical imbalance</strong> (the "dopamine hypothesis" has been repeatedly challenged and never proven)</li>
<li>A <strong>pathological process</strong> (no one can describe how schizophrenia develops at a biological level)</li>
</ul>
<p>In every other branch of medicine, a disease diagnosis requires an identifiable pathological process. Cancer has tumors. Diabetes has measurable blood sugar abnormalities. Heart disease has observable arterial damage. Schizophrenia has nothing — only a collection of behavioral symptoms that are observed, categorized, and labeled by a psychiatrist.</p>
<h3 id="the-dsm-a-catalogue-of-opinions">The DSM: A Catalogue of Opinions</h3>
<p>The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM) — psychiatry's "bible" — does not define diseases. It defines behavioral categories agreed upon by committee vote. The DSM-5 criteria for schizophrenia require two or more of the following symptoms for at least one month:</p>
<ol>
<li>Delusions</li>
<li>Hallucinations</li>
<li>Disorganized speech</li>
<li>Grossly disorganized or catatonic behavior</li>
<li>Negative symptoms (diminished emotional expression or avolition)</li>
</ol>
<p>Notice what is absent: any biological test, any objective measurement, any physical evidence. The diagnosis is entirely subjective — based on the psychiatrist's interpretation of the patient's behavior and self-report. Two psychiatrists can examine the same patient and reach different diagnoses. This is not science — it is opinion dressed in medical authority.</p>
<h3 id="the-chemical-imbalance-myth">The Chemical Imbalance Myth</h3>
<p>The "chemical imbalance" theory of schizophrenia — the claim that it is caused by excess dopamine in the brain — was never more than a theory, and it has been repeatedly undermined by evidence:</p>
<ul>
<li>Antipsychotic drugs (which block dopamine receptors) often fail to relieve symptoms</li>
<li>Many patients with "schizophrenia" have normal dopamine levels</li>
<li>The theory was reverse-engineered from drug effects (the drugs block dopamine, so the disease must be caused by excess dopamine — a logical fallacy)</li>
<li>Recent research has questioned the dopamine hypothesis entirely</li>
</ul>
<p>In 2022, a landmark umbrella review published in Molecular Psychiatry concluded that there is <strong>no convincing evidence</strong> that depression is caused by low serotonin levels — demolishing the chemical imbalance theory for depression. The same critique applies to the dopamine hypothesis of schizophrenia: it was always a marketing narrative for pharmaceutical products, not a scientific finding.</p>
