<h2 id="the-quarantine-camps-of-the-19th-century">The Quarantine Camps of the 19th Century</h2>
<h3 id="lazarettos-and-pest-houses">Lazarettos and Pest Houses</h3>
<p>Long before FEMA, long before the CDC, and long before modern public health bureaucracy, the United States operated a network of quarantine facilities that functioned as detention camps for the sick, the suspected sick, and anyone the authorities chose to classify as a health risk.</p>
<p><strong>Lazarettos</strong> — Named after the biblical Lazarus, lazarettos were quarantine stations established at ports of entry to detain immigrants and travelers suspected of carrying infectious diseases. The Philadelphia Lazaretto, established in 1799, was one of the oldest in the country. Detainees were held without trial, without legal representation, and without recourse. Their property was confiscated and often destroyed. Those who died in quarantine were buried in unmarked graves.</p>
<p><strong>Pest houses</strong> — Nearly every American city maintained a "pest house" — a facility for the isolation of people with contagious diseases. These facilities ranged from converted buildings to purpose-built structures, and conditions within them were often appalling. Patients were confined against their will, denied visitors, and treated (or untreated) at the discretion of the attending physician. Death rates in pest houses were staggeringly high — not merely from the diseases themselves but from the conditions of confinement.</p>
<h3 id="the-immigrant-detention-system">The Immigrant Detention System</h3>
<p>Ellis Island, celebrated in American mythology as the "gateway to freedom," was in reality a quarantine and detention facility where immigrants were examined, interrogated, and in many cases confined for weeks or months. Those deemed "unfit" — physically, mentally, or morally — were deported.</p>
<p>The medical examinations at Ellis Island were conducted by Public Health Service physicians who wielded enormous power over the lives of incoming immigrants. A chalk mark on a person's clothing — "H" for heart problems, "L" for lameness, "X" for suspected mental illness — could condemn an individual to detention, further examination, or deportation. These physicians were not independent medical professionals; they were agents of the state, enforcing immigration policies that were shaped by the same eugenics ideology that would later produce forced sterilization programs and, in its most extreme form, the Nazi concentration camps.</p>
<h2 id="the-typhoid-mary-precedent">The Typhoid Mary Precedent</h2>
<h3 id="the-case-of-mary-mallon">The Case of Mary Mallon</h3>
<p>Mary Mallon, known to history as "Typhoid Mary," was an Irish-born cook who was identified as an asymptomatic carrier of typhoid fever in 1907. Mallon was forcibly quarantined — imprisoned — on North Brother Island in New York's East River from 1907 to 1910, then released on the condition that she never work as a cook again.</p>
<p>When Mallon was found cooking again in 1915 (using a false name), she was re-arrested and returned to North Brother Island, where she remained imprisoned for the rest of her life — 23 years — until her death in 1938.</p>
<p>The Typhoid Mary case established a critical legal precedent: the government has the authority to imprison individuals who pose a public health risk, without trial, without time limit, and without the due process protections guaranteed by the Constitution. This precedent has been invoked repeatedly in the century since — and it forms the legal basis for the modern quarantine powers that FEMA and the CDC exercise today.</p>

<h2 id="the-internment-precedent">The Internment Precedent</h2>
<h3 id="japanese-american-internment">Japanese-American Internment</h3>
<p>On February 19, 1942, President Franklin Roosevelt signed Executive Order 9066, authorizing the forced relocation and internment of approximately 120,000 Japanese-Americans. The internees — the majority of whom were American citizens — were stripped of their property, confined in camps surrounded by barbed wire and guard towers, and held for up to four years.</p>
<p>The official justification was "military necessity" — the claim that Japanese-Americans posed a security risk. But public health language was also used to justify the internment: Japanese-Americans were described as a "contamination" and a "disease" that needed to be "quarantined" from the healthy body politic.</p>
<p>The internment camps were, in every meaningful sense, FEMA camps before FEMA. They demonstrated that the United States government was willing and able to round up an entire ethnic group, confine them in camps, and strip them of their constitutional rights — all under the authority of executive order.</p>
