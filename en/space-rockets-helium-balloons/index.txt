<h2 id="the-evidence">The Evidence</h2>
<h3 id="the-balloon-connection">The Balloon Connection</h3>
<p>NASA has a long and documented history of using high-altitude balloons:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>NASA's Scientific Balloon Program</strong> launches approximately 10-15 large scientific balloons per year</li>
<li>These balloons carry payloads of up to <strong>8,000 pounds</strong> to altitudes of <strong>130,000 feet</strong> (approximately 25 miles)</li>
<li>The balloons are <strong>enormous</strong> — when fully inflated at altitude, they can be as large as a football stadium</li>
<li>NASA uses balloons for <strong>astronomical observation, atmospheric research, and technology testing</strong></li>
<li>Some balloon missions carry instruments that are later claimed to have been "in space"</li>

<h3 id="the-physics-questions">The Physics Questions</h3>
<h4 id="rocket-fuel-and-payload">Rocket Fuel and Payload</h4>
<p>The physics of rocketry involve a fundamental problem: the rocket must carry the fuel that will propel it, and carrying fuel requires more fuel to carry the fuel. This is the "tyranny of the rocket equation," and it severely limits what rockets can actually accomplish.</p>
<p>According to NASA's own figures, the Saturn V rocket (which allegedly carried astronauts to the moon) had a gross weight of <strong>6.5 million pounds</strong>, of which approximately <strong>6 million pounds</strong> was fuel. The remaining <strong>500,000 pounds</strong> included the rocket structure and the payload — meaning that over 92% of the rocket's weight was fuel.</p>
<p>Some researchers have questioned whether a vehicle that is 92% fuel can actually achieve the velocities and distances claimed. The mathematics of the rocket equation are well-established, but the assumptions that feed into the equation — the efficiency of the engines, the energy density of the fuel, the trajectory parameters — are provided by NASA itself and cannot be independently verified.</p>
<h4 id="the-van-allen-belts">The Van Allen Belts</h4>
<p>The Van Allen radiation belts — zones of intense radiation surrounding the Earth — present a potentially lethal barrier to human space travel. NASA acknowledges the belts but claims that Apollo astronauts passed through them too quickly to receive dangerous radiation doses.</p>
<p>This claim has been questioned by researchers who note:</p>
<ul>
<li>The radiation levels in the belts are sufficient to damage electronic equipment and harm biological tissue</li>
<li>The Apollo spacecraft's aluminum hull would have provided minimal radiation shielding</li>
<li>NASA's own contemporary documents expressed concern about Van Allen belt radiation</li>
<li>No subsequent manned mission has traveled through the Van Allen belts since Apollo</li>
</ul>
