<h2 id="the-contaminants">The Contaminants</h2>
<h3 id="fluoride-the-mass-medication">Fluoride: The Mass Medication</h3>
<p>Approximately 73 percent of Americans receive fluoridated water. Fluoride is added to public water supplies ostensibly to prevent tooth decay. But fluoride is also a neurotoxin. In 2024, a federal court ruled that water fluoridation at current U.S. levels poses an "unreasonable risk" to the intellectual development of children, citing a comprehensive NTP (National Toxicology Program) review that classified fluoride as a cognitive neurodevelopmental hazard.</p>
<p>The fluoridation of public water is mass medication without individual consent. No other medication is administered through the water supply. No other medical treatment is delivered without a doctor's prescription, individual dosing, and informed consent. Fluoride is the exception — delivered to every person who drinks tap water, at a uniform dose regardless of age, weight, health status, or need.</p>
<p>The history of water fluoridation involves connections to aluminum and nuclear industries that needed a market for their fluoride waste byproduct. Agent131711 documents on Substack (agent131711.substack.com) how these industrial interests manufactured scientific consensus in favor of fluoridation through funded research, institutional capture, and media management — the same methods used by every industry documented in this series.</p>
<h3 id="pfas-the-forever-chemicals">PFAS: The Forever Chemicals</h3>
<p>Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) — known as "forever chemicals" because they do not break down in the environment — have been found in the water supplies of virtually every American community tested. These chemicals, used in non-stick cookware, waterproof clothing, firefighting foam, and food packaging, contaminate groundwater, surface water, and drinking water.</p>
<p>PFAS are linked to cancer, thyroid disease, immune suppression, reproductive problems, and liver damage. They bioaccumulate — meaning they build up in the body over time with each exposure. The average American has measurable levels of multiple PFAS chemicals in their blood.</p>
<p>The EPA has set advisory levels for PFAS in drinking water, but these levels are not legally enforceable in most jurisdictions. The chemical manufacturers — primarily DuPont and 3M — knew about PFAS toxicity for decades and concealed the information. Internal documents revealed in litigation show that these companies understood the health risks and chose profit over public safety.</p>
<h3 id="pharmaceuticals-the-unintended-medication">Pharmaceuticals: The Unintended Medication</h3>
<p>Pharmaceutical residues — antibiotics, hormones, antidepressants, painkillers, chemotherapy drugs — have been found in drinking water supplies across the country. These drugs enter the water through human excretion, improper disposal, and agricultural runoff. Municipal water treatment does not remove them.</p>
<p>The health effects of chronic low-level exposure to pharmaceutical cocktails in drinking water are unknown. No research institution has studied the long-term consequences of consuming trace amounts of dozens of drugs simultaneously, every day, for decades. The institutional silence on this topic is itself evidence of the cover-up: the most basic question about water safety — what are we drinking and what does it do? — remains unanswered.</p>
<h3 id="agricultural-runoff">Agricultural Runoff</h3>
<p>Nitrates from fertilizer, pesticide residues, herbicide compounds (including glyphosate, the active ingredient in Roundup), and livestock waste all contaminate water supplies in agricultural regions. The EPA's limits on these contaminants are widely regarded as inadequate by independent scientists, and enforcement is inconsistent.</p>
<p>The intersection of agricultural contamination and water supply is a direct consequence of the industrial agriculture system documented throughout this series — a system that prioritizes production over environmental and human health, regulated by captured agencies, and profiting entities connected to the same institutional networks that govern finance, media, and religion.</p>

<h3 id="what-you-can-do">What You Can Do</h3>
<ol>
<li><strong>Test your water</strong>: Independent testing services can analyze your water for contaminants.</li>
<li><strong>Filter your water</strong>: Reverse osmosis and activated carbon filters remove most contaminants. Pitcher filters remove some but not all.</li>
<li><strong>Avoid plastic bottles</strong>: Bottled water is often no cleaner than tap water and may contain microplastics and PFAS from the plastic bottle.</li>
<li><strong>Source natural spring water</strong>: If possible, find and test a natural spring. SpringFinder databases can help locate springs near you.</li>
<li><strong>Advocate for change</strong>: Support legislation requiring comprehensive water testing, enforceable limits, and public disclosure of results.</li>
</ol>
