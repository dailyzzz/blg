<h2 id="the-hendershot-story">The Hendershot Story</h2>
<h3 id="the-inventor">The Inventor</h3>
<p>Lester Jennings Hendershot was an American inventor who, in the late 1920s, built a device he called the "Hendershot Fuelless Motor" or "Hendershot Generator." The device was described as an electromagnetic generator that drew energy from the Earth's magnetic field — converting the ambient magnetic energy into usable electrical power.</p>
<p>Hendershot was not a trained physicist or engineer. He was a practical inventor — a tinkerer who built things based on intuition, experimentation, and what worked. His lack of academic credentials was used to discredit him, but it also meant that he was not constrained by the academic orthodoxy that declares free energy impossible.</p>
<h3 id="the-1928-demonstrations">The 1928 Demonstrations</h3>
<p>In February 1928, Hendershot demonstrated his device at Selfridge Field, a military airfield near Detroit, Michigan. The demonstration was witnessed by:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Major Thomas Lanphier</strong> — a distinguished Army Air Corps officer and father of the pilot who later shot down Admiral Yamamoto's plane in World War II</li>
<li><strong>Several Army officers</strong> and technical personnel</li>
<li><strong>Newspaper reporters</strong> from the Detroit Free Press and other publications</li>
</ul>
<p>The witnesses reported that the device:</p>
<ul>
<li>Generated electrical current sufficient to power lights, radio receivers, and a small sewing machine motor</li>
<li>Operated without any visible external power source — no batteries, no fuel, no electrical connection</li>
<li>Contained coils, magnets, and capacitors arranged in a specific configuration</li>
<li>Functioned for extended periods during testing</li>
</ul>
<p>Major Lanphier was sufficiently impressed to advocate for further military investigation of the device. The story received significant newspaper coverage, with headlines reporting on the "fuelless motor" and its potential to revolutionize energy production.</p>
<h3 id="the-suppression">The Suppression</h3>
<p>What happened next follows the pattern of every free energy suppression:</p>
<p><strong>Discrediting</strong>: After the initial positive coverage, a counter-narrative emerged. Hendershot was accused of fraud — allegedly hiding batteries inside the device. This accusation was never proven, but it was amplified by the media and used to discredit the entire demonstration.</p>
<p><strong>Physical attack</strong>: Hendershot reportedly received an electric shock while working on his device — an incident that some researchers believe was not accidental but a warning from parties who wanted the work stopped.</p>
<p><strong>Psychiatric commitment</strong>: Hendershot was at one point committed to a psychiatric institution — the standard method of neutralizing troublesome inventors. The diagnosis provided a convenient explanation for his "delusional" claims about free energy.</p>
<p><strong>Device confiscation</strong>: The devices Hendershot built either disappeared or were confiscated. No functioning Hendershot generator survives — or if one does, it is in the possession of parties who have no interest in making it public.</p>
<p><strong>Death</strong>: Hendershot died in 1961 under circumstances that have raised questions among researchers. The official cause was listed as natural causes, but the pattern of inventor deaths associated with free energy technology suggests a darker possibility.</p>
