15:T42da,<h1 id="trump-executive-order-bans-gain-of-function-research">Trump Executive Order Bans Gain-of-Function Research</h1>
<h2 id="the-ancient-pattern">The Ancient Pattern</h2>
<blockquote>
<p>"And God saw that the wickedness of man was great in the earth, and that every imagination of the thoughts of his heart was only evil continually." — Genesis 6:5</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Before the Flood, Scripture records that the earth was filled with violence and that the imagination of man's heart was "only evil continually." The Hebrew word for "imagination" — <em>yetser</em> — refers to the formative purpose behind creation, the intent that precedes the act. The wickedness that provoked God's judgment was not mere misbehavior — it was purposeful, systematic, and creative. Man was using his God-given capacity for invention to construct instruments of destruction.</p>
<p>As the Confliction track <a href="https://confliction.zzzdaily.com/tracks/en/73-The_Fallen_Angels.mp4">The Fallen Angels</a> reveals, the Book of Enoch records that the Watchers — fallen angels who descended to earth — taught humanity forbidden knowledge: the making of weapons, the mixing of substances, and the manipulation of the natural order. Gain-of-function research is the modern expression of this ancient transmission — the deliberate enhancement of pathogens to make them more transmissible, more virulent, and more lethal. It is the weaponization of creation itself.</p>
<p>President Trump has signed an executive order banning this research. But the question Scripture poses is whether man's determination to transgress the boundaries God established can be contained by executive decree — or whether the judgment that follows is already in motion.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="what-is-gain-of-function-research">What Is Gain-of-Function Research?</h2>
<p>Gain-of-function (GOF) research refers to scientific experiments that deliberately alter an organism — typically a virus — to give it new or enhanced capabilities. These capabilities include increased transmissibility between species (particularly from animals to humans), enhanced virulence (the ability to cause more severe disease), resistance to existing treatments or vaccines, and the ability to evade immune system defenses.</p>
<p>The stated justification for GOF research is "pandemic preparedness." Proponents argue that by engineering dangerous pathogens in the laboratory, scientists can study them in advance and develop countermeasures before a natural pandemic occurs. The logic is preemptive: create the threat in a controlled environment so you can prepare for it before it emerges in nature.</p>
<p>Critics — including many senior virologists, biosecurity experts, and national security analysts — have argued that GOF research creates the very threats it claims to prevent. Every pathogen engineered in a laboratory represents a potential pandemic waiting to escape. And laboratory accidents are not hypothetical — they are documented and recurrent.</p>
<p>Karen Kingston has documented in The Kingston Report (karenkingston.substack.com) that the distinction between "defensive" biodefense research and "offensive" bioweapons development is largely semantic. "When you engineer a virus to be more transmissible and more lethal, you have created a bioweapon," Kingston has stated. "The fact that you call it 'pandemic preparedness' does not change what it is. The product is the same. The danger is the same. The only difference is the label."</p>
<h3 id="the-wuhan-connection">The Wuhan Connection</h3>
<p>The most consequential gain-of-function research program in modern history was conducted at the Wuhan Institute of Virology (WIV) in Wuhan, China — the city where COVID-19 first emerged in late 2019.</p>
<p>The WIV's coronavirus research program was funded in part by the U.S. National Institutes of Health through grants channeled via EcoHealth Alliance, a New York-based nonprofit headed by Peter Daszak. Between 2014 and 2019, EcoHealth Alliance received approximately $3.75 million in NIH funding, a portion of which was sub-awarded to the WIV for coronavirus research that included gain-of-function experiments on bat coronaviruses.</p>
<p>Dr. Anthony Fauci, as director of NIAID, oversaw the NIH division that funded EcoHealth Alliance. When questioned under oath before Congress about whether NIH funded gain-of-function research at the WIV, Fauci initially denied it. He later acknowledged the funding but disputed the characterization of the research as gain-of-function — a semantic distinction that rested on a narrow definition of GOF that excluded the type of experiments actually conducted.</p>
<p>The ZZZ Daily track <a href="https://zzzdaily.zzzdaily.com/tracks/en/167-DARPA_Dark_Winter.mp4">DARPA Dark Winter</a> documents the military origins of pandemic preparedness programs and their connection to biodefense research that blurs the line between protection and weaponization.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="the-executive-order">The Executive Order</h2>
<p>President Trump's executive order represents the most sweeping federal action against gain-of-function research in U.S. history. The order:</p>
<p><strong>Prohibits all federally funded gain-of-function research</strong> — defined as research that enhances the transmissibility, virulence, or immune evasion capabilities of pathogens with pandemic potential. This prohibition applies to all federal agencies, including NIH, NIAID, BARDA, DARPA, and the Department of Defense.</p>
<p><strong>Prohibits the use of federal funds for GOF research conducted by foreign institutions</strong> — closing the loophole that allowed the NIH to fund GOF research at the Wuhan Institute of Virology through intermediary organizations like EcoHealth Alliance.</p>
<p><strong>Requires a comprehensive audit</strong> of all federally funded research programs that may include GOF components, with results to be reported to Congress within 180 days.</p>
<p><strong>Establishes criminal penalties</strong> for federal officials who approve, fund, or facilitate GOF research in violation of the order — a significant escalation from prior policy, which relied on voluntary compliance and institutional self-reporting.</p>
<p><strong>Revokes the 2017 P3CO Framework</strong> (Potential Pandemic Pathogen Care and Oversight), which was the previous policy governing enhanced pathogen research. The P3CO framework was widely criticized as inadequate, as it relied on a review board with no enforcement authority and allowed exceptions for research deemed to have "acceptable" risk-benefit ratios.</p>
<h3 id="the-history-of-failed-moratoriums">The History of Failed Moratoriums</h3>
<p>This is not the first attempt to restrict gain-of-function research. In October 2014, the Obama administration imposed a moratorium on federal funding for GOF research involving influenza, SARS, and MERS viruses. The moratorium was prompted by a series of laboratory incidents — including the accidental shipment of live anthrax from a DoD laboratory and the discovery of forgotten smallpox samples at an NIH facility.</p>
<p>However, the moratorium included broad exceptions, and some GOF research continued under "grandfather" provisions that allowed ongoing experiments to proceed. In December 2017, the Trump administration's first term lifted the moratorium and replaced it with the P3CO Framework — the same framework now revoked by this executive order.</p>
<p>Kingston has documented that during the 2014-2017 moratorium, GOF research continued through multiple channels: through the exception provisions, through reclassification of research to avoid the GOF definition, and through the offshore funding of research at foreign laboratories — including the Wuhan Institute of Virology.</p>
<p>"The moratorium was a public relations exercise, not a regulatory action," Kingston has written. "The research never stopped. It was relabeled, reclassified, and relocated. The same scientists, the same funding streams, the same institutions continued the same work under different names. The 2014 moratorium proved that voluntary restrictions on gain-of-function research do not work."</p>

<h2 id="the-bioweapons-dimension">The Bioweapons Dimension</h2>
<p>The Biological Weapons Convention (BWC), signed in 1972 and ratified by the United States, prohibits the development, production, and stockpiling of biological weapons. However, the BWC includes an exception for "prophylactic, protective, or other peaceful purposes" — an exception that effectively permits any biological research that can be characterized as defensive.</p>
<p>This exception has been exploited for decades. The United States maintains an extensive biodefense research infrastructure — spanning Fort Detrick (U.S. Army Medical Research Institute of Infectious Diseases), the CDC, DARPA, BARDA, and dozens of university and private-sector laboratories — that conducts research on dangerous pathogens under the aegis of "defensive preparedness."</p>
<p>The line between defensive and offensive biological research is, in practice, nonexistent. Engineering a more transmissible virus to study it is functionally identical to engineering a more transmissible virus to weaponize it. The pathogen does not know the intent of its creator. It does not behave differently because the laboratory that created it filed the research under "pandemic preparedness" rather than "biological weapons development."</p>
<p>Kingston has connected this institutional reality to the broader pattern of government-pharmaceutical collusion: "The biodefense establishment and the pharmaceutical industry are two sides of the same coin. The biodefense labs create the threats. The pharmaceutical companies sell the countermeasures. Both are funded by the same government, staffed by the same rotating personnel, and protected by the same legal frameworks. This is not public health. This is an industry — an industry that profits from the creation and management of biological threats."</p>
