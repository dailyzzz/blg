<h2 id="the-constitutional-convention-a-coup">The Constitutional Convention: A Coup</h2>
<h3 id="what-really-happened-in-philadelphia">What Really Happened in Philadelphia</h3>
<p>The Constitutional Convention of 1787 was convened ostensibly to revise the Articles of Confederation — the existing governing framework of the United States. The delegates were authorized to propose amendments to the Articles, not to replace them entirely. What happened instead was a complete overthrow of the existing government — conducted in secret, behind locked doors, with sentries posted to prevent any unauthorized observation.</p>
<p>The Convention's proceedings were kept secret for decades. James Madison's notes, the most complete record of the debates, were not published until 1840 — more than fifty years after the Convention and four years after Madison's death. The secrecy was not incidental; it was essential. The delegates knew that they were exceeding their authority, and they knew that public awareness of their proceedings would have generated opposition that might have prevented the Constitution's ratification.</p>
<p>The Convention was dominated by Freemasons and men connected to Masonic-Illuminist networks. George Washington, the Convention's president, was a Freemason. <h3 id="the-anti-federalist-warning">The Anti-Federalist Warning</h3>
<p>The opponents of the Constitution — the Anti-Federalists — recognized the danger that the proposed document represented. Patrick Henry, who had refused to attend the Convention because he "smelt a rat," warned that the Constitution would create a consolidated government that would destroy the sovereignty of the states and the liberty of the people. George Mason, who attended the Convention but refused to sign the document, objected to the absence of a bill of rights and the broad powers granted to the federal government.</p>
<p>The Anti-Federalists' warnings proved prophetic. The "supremacy clause" (Article VI, Clause 2), which establishes the Constitution and federal law as "the supreme Law of the Land," ensured that federal power would always prevail over state resistance.</p>
