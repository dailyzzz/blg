<h2 id="the-blood-business">The Blood Business</h2>
<h3 id="the-numbers">The Numbers</h3>
<p>The American Red Cross collects approximately <strong>6.8 million units</strong> of blood per year in the United States. The blood is donated for free by volunteers. It is then:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Tested</strong> for infectious diseases</li>
<li><strong>Processed</strong> into components (red cells, platelets, plasma, cryoprecipitate)</li>
<li><strong>Stored</strong> in temperature-controlled facilities</li>
<li><strong>Sold</strong> to hospitals at prices ranging from <strong>$150 to $300+ per unit</strong> for red blood cells</li>
</ul>
<p>A single donation can be separated into components that generate <strong>$300-$500 in revenue</strong>. Multiply that by 6.8 million donations, and the Red Cross blood operation alone generates <strong>over $2 billion</strong> in annual revenue.</p>
<p>The donor receives nothing. The Red Cross — a "nonprofit" organization — generates billions. The hospitals mark up the blood products further before charging patients. A single unit of blood can cost a patient <strong>$1,000 or more</strong> on their hospital bill.</p>
<h3 id="the-red-cross-not-what-you-think">The Red Cross: Not What You Think</h3>
<p>The American Red Cross is perceived as a charitable organization staffed by volunteers doing good works. The reality is more complex:</p>
<ul>

<li>Senior executives earn <strong>six-figure salaries</strong></li>

<li>During disaster relief operations, donations intended for victims have been diverted to <strong>general operating expenses</strong></li>
<li>Blood operations represent the Red Cross's <strong>largest revenue source</strong></li>
</ul>
<p>The Red Cross was established with a charter from the US Congress, giving it a quasi-governmental status. It operates under the protection of the Geneva Conventions and enjoys tax-exempt status. This combination of governmental authority and private operation creates an accountability gap — the Red Cross is neither fully public (and thus subject to public oversight) nor fully private (and thus subject to market competition).</p>
<h3 id="the-plasma-industry">The Plasma Industry</h3>
<p>The plasma industry is even more revealing. Plasma — the liquid component of blood — is used to manufacture pharmaceutical products including immunoglobulins, clotting factors, and albumin. Unlike whole blood, plasma can be legally purchased from donors.</p>
<p>The plasma industry is dominated by a handful of corporations:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Grifols</strong> (Spain)</li>
<li><strong>CSL Behring</strong> (Australia)</li>
<li><strong>Takeda</strong> (Japan, formerly Shire/Baxter)</li>
<li><strong>Octapharma</strong> (Switzerland)</li>
<li><strong>BPL</strong> (UK)</li>
</ul>
<p>These companies operate thousands of plasma collection centers across the United States, primarily in <strong>low-income neighborhoods</strong>. Donors are paid <strong>$30-75 per donation</strong> and can donate up to <strong>twice per week</strong>. The plasma collected from these donors is processed into products that sell for <strong>thousands of dollars per dose</strong>.</p>
<p>The United States is the world's largest plasma exporter, supplying approximately <strong>70% of the global plasma supply</strong>. The country has become, in effect, a blood farm for the global pharmaceutical industry — with the poorest Americans serving as the livestock.</p>
<h2 id="the-hidden-dangers">The Hidden Dangers</h2>
<h3 id="contamination-history">Contamination History</h3>
<p>The blood supply has a dark history of contamination:</p>
<p><strong>The HIV Blood Scandal</strong>: In the early 1980s, the blood supply was contaminated with HIV, infecting thousands of hemophiliacs and transfusion recipients. The blood industry was slow to implement screening tests, and some companies continued to sell contaminated blood products even after the risk was known. <p><strong>Hepatitis C</strong>: Before screening tests were available, the blood supply was widely contaminated with Hepatitis C. An estimated 300,000 Americans were infected with Hepatitis C through blood transfusions between 1970 and 1990.</p>
<p><strong>Prion Diseases</strong>: The blood supply cannot be effectively tested for prion diseases (such as Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease). <h3 id="the-storage-problem">The Storage Problem</h3>
<p>Research has revealed that stored blood deteriorates significantly over time:</p>
<ul>
<li>Red blood cells lose their flexibility and ability to deliver oxygen</li>
<li>Stored blood releases free hemoglobin, which can damage organs</li>
<li>Stored blood has elevated potassium levels that can cause cardiac problems</li>
<li>Studies have shown that patients who receive older stored blood have <strong>worse outcomes</strong> than those who receive fresh blood or no transfusion at all</li>

<p>Despite this evidence, blood banks routinely store blood for up to <strong>42 days</strong> and distribute it to hospitals without regard to storage age. The system is optimized for inventory management, not patient outcomes.</p>
