<h2 id="the-ancient-pattern">The Ancient Pattern</h2>
<blockquote>
<p>"For we wrestle not against flesh and blood, but against principalities, against powers, against the rulers of the darkness of this world, against spiritual wickedness in high places." — Ephesians 6:12</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The plagues recorded in Scripture were instruments of divine judgment, wielded by the hand of God against the enemies of His people. From the ten plagues of Egypt to the pestilence that struck Israel after David's census, disease in the biblical record serves a sovereign purpose. But what happens when men arrogate to themselves the power of plague — when laboratories replace the hand of God with the hand of the military-industrial complex, and pathogens are engineered not by divine decree but by government contract?</p>
<p>The creation of biological weapons is, at its root, an act of profound spiritual rebellion. It is man seizing the power of pestilence — one of the four horsemen of Revelation — and attempting to ride it for geopolitical advantage. The engineers of gain-of-function research are not merely scientists; they are modern-day sorcerers, manipulating the building blocks of life in ways that Scripture explicitly warns against. The boundaries between species, between the natural and the unnatural, were established by God in Genesis. To breach those boundaries deliberately — to take a virus that infects bats and engineer it to infect humans — is to repeat the transgression that brought judgment upon the ancient world.</p>
<p>As the Confliction album track <a href="https://confliction.zzzdaily.com/tracks/en/05-The_First_Darkness.mp4">The First Darkness</a> explores, there is a darkness that precedes every great calamity — a darkness born not of nature but of human ambition unchecked by moral restraint. The first darkness was the rebellion in Eden. The modern darkness is the rebellion in the laboratory, where men play God with pathogens and call it science.</p>

<h2 id="fort-detrick-americas-biological-weapons-headquarters">Fort Detrick: America's Biological Weapons Headquarters</h2>
<p>The story of modern bioweapons begins at Fort Detrick, a United States Army installation in Frederick, Maryland, that has served as the epicenter of America's biological warfare program since 1943. Originally established as Camp Detrick during World War II, the facility was created to develop offensive biological weapons in response to intelligence that Japan and Germany were pursuing their own bioweapons programs.</p>
<p>During the 1940s and 1950s, Fort Detrick scientists weaponized anthrax, brucella, tularemia, Venezuelan equine encephalitis, and dozens of other pathogens. The program was vast: at its peak, Fort Detrick employed thousands of scientists and technicians working across multiple laboratories dedicated to the production, storage, and delivery of biological agents capable of causing mass casualties.</p>
<p>In 1969, President Richard Nixon signed an executive order officially terminating the United States offensive biological weapons program. The existing stockpiles were reportedly destroyed between 1971 and 1972, and Fort Detrick was ostensibly converted to a defensive research facility under the newly created United States Army Medical Research Institute of Infectious Diseases (USAMRIID). The Biological Weapons Convention (BWC) of 1972, ratified by the United States in 1975, prohibited the development, production, and stockpiling of biological weapons.</p>
<p>But the line between "offensive" and "defensive" biological research is a distinction without a difference. Defensive research requires working with the same pathogens, the same enhancement techniques, and the same delivery mechanisms as offensive research. The only difference is the stated intent — and intent is invisible to a microscope. Fort Detrick never stopped working with the world's most dangerous pathogens. It merely changed the paperwork.</p>
<p>In August 2019, Fort Detrick's highest-security laboratories were abruptly shut down by the CDC following "biosafety lapses" involving the handling of dangerous pathogens. The specific nature of these lapses was classified. The shutdown occurred just months before the first reported cases of COVID-19 — a coincidence that has generated significant scrutiny from researchers worldwide, including formal diplomatic inquiries from the Chinese government.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="gain-of-function-the-science-of-making-pathogens-more-dangerous">Gain-of-Function: The Science of Making Pathogens More Dangerous</h2>
<p>Gain-of-function research is the practice of deliberately enhancing the transmissibility, virulence, or host range of pathogens. In plain language, it means taking a virus that is dangerous and making it more dangerous — engineering it to spread faster, kill more efficiently, or jump from animals to humans.</p>
<p>The stated justification for this research is pandemic preparedness. Proponents argue that by understanding how a pathogen could become more dangerous, scientists can develop countermeasures — vaccines, therapeutics, surveillance systems — before a natural pandemic occurs. This is the argument advanced by virologists like Dr. Ralph Baric of the University of North Carolina and Dr. Peter Daszak of EcoHealth Alliance, both of whom received extensive federal funding for gain-of-function research on coronaviruses.</p>
<p>The counterargument is devastating in its simplicity: if you are engineering pathogens that do not yet exist in nature, you are not preparing for a natural pandemic — you are creating the conditions for an unnatural one. You are manufacturing the very threat you claim to be defending against. And if that engineered pathogen escapes the laboratory — through accident, negligence, or intent — you have not prevented a pandemic. You have caused one.</p>
<p>This is not a hypothetical concern. Laboratory leaks are not rare events. A 2014 investigation by USA Today identified over 100 biosafety incidents at U.S. laboratories in the preceding decade, including accidental exposure to anthrax, avian influenza, and smallpox. The 1977 H1N1 influenza outbreak is widely believed to have originated from a laboratory escape. The SARS virus escaped from Chinese laboratories on at least four documented occasions between 2003 and 2004.</p>
<p>The scientific community itself has raised alarms. In 2014, following several high-profile biosafety incidents, the Obama administration imposed a moratorium on federal funding for gain-of-function research involving influenza, SARS, and MERS viruses. But this moratorium was riddled with exceptions, and key research projects — including those conducted by Ralph Baric in collaboration with the Wuhan Institute of Virology — continued under exemptions granted by the NIH.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="the-wuhan-chapel-hill-pipeline-how-american-money-built-chinas-biolab">The Wuhan-Chapel Hill Pipeline: How American Money Built China's Biolab</h2>
<p>The connection between American gain-of-function research and the Wuhan Institute of Virology (WIV) is not a conspiracy theory. It is a documented funding trail that runs through federal grant databases, published scientific papers, and the testimony of the researchers themselves.</p>
<p>Dr. Ralph Baric, a coronavirus researcher at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, is one of the world's foremost experts in engineering coronaviruses to increase their transmissibility and pathogenicity. In a 2015 paper published in <em>Nature Medicine</em>, Baric and Dr. Zhengli Shi of the Wuhan Institute of Virology described their creation of a chimeric coronavirus — a synthetic virus combining the spike protein of a bat coronavirus (SHC014) with the backbone of a SARS-like virus adapted for mice. This engineered virus was shown to be capable of infecting human airway cells.</p>
<p>The paper acknowledged funding from the National Institutes of Health (NIH), specifically from the National Institute of Allergy and Infectious Diseases (NIAID) — the division led by Dr. Anthony Fauci. It also acknowledged support from EcoHealth Alliance, the nonprofit organization led by Dr. Peter Daszak that served as the primary conduit for funneling American taxpayer dollars to the Wuhan Institute of Virology.</p>
<p>Between 2014 and 2019, EcoHealth Alliance received approximately $3.7 million in NIH grant funding under grant number R01AI110964, a significant portion of which was subcontracted to the Wuhan Institute of Virology. The stated purpose was to study bat coronaviruses in China. But the research conducted under this grant included gain-of-function experiments on coronaviruses — precisely the type of research that the 2014 moratorium was supposed to halt.</p>
<p>When confronted about this funding in congressional hearings, Dr. Fauci initially denied that the NIH funded gain-of-function research at the Wuhan Institute of Virology. He subsequently modified his position, claiming that the research conducted did not meet the technical definition of gain-of-function. NIH Principal Deputy Director Lawrence Tabak later acknowledged in a letter to Congress that EcoHealth Alliance had in fact conducted research at WIV that resulted in a bat coronavirus becoming more infectious — meeting the common-sense definition of gain-of-function that Fauci had denied.</p>

<h2 id="darpa-the-military-hand-behind-the-science">DARPA: The Military Hand Behind the Science</h2>
<p>The Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA) occupies a unique and troubling position in the bioweapons narrative. As the research arm of the Department of Defense, DARPA funds cutting-edge scientific research with potential military applications — including extensive biological research programs.</p>
<p>DARPA's PREDICT program, launched in 2009, was designed to identify viruses in wildlife that could potentially spill over into human populations. The program funded researchers including Peter Daszak and EcoHealth Alliance to collect bat viruses in remote locations, including caves in Yunnan Province, China — the same region where the closest known relative of SARS-CoV-2 was later identified.</p>
<p>In 2018, EcoHealth Alliance submitted a proposal to DARPA's PREEMPT program requesting $14 million to conduct gain-of-function research on bat coronaviruses. The proposal, which was leaked to the public in 2021 by Marine Major Joseph Murphy, included plans to insert furin cleavage sites into bat coronaviruses — a feature that appears in SARS-CoV-2 but is not found in other closely related bat coronaviruses. DARPA rejected the proposal, citing safety concerns. But the question of whether the research described in the rejected proposal was subsequently conducted at the Wuhan Institute of Virology using other funding sources remains one of the most consequential unanswered questions of the pandemic.</p>
<p>As documented in our article on <a href="/darpa-dark-winter-military-origins/?lang=en">DARPA Dark Winter and Military Origins</a>, DARPA's involvement in biological research extends far beyond pandemic preparedness into the domain of biological defense — and offense. The agency's Biological Technologies Office funds research in synthetic biology, gene editing, and novel vaccine platforms that blur the line between medical countermeasure and military capability.</p>

<h2 id="the-dual-use-dilemma-every-defense-is-an-offense">The Dual-Use Dilemma: Every Defense Is an Offense</h2>
<p>The concept of "dual-use research of concern" (DURC) is the polite academic term for the fundamental problem at the heart of bioweapons and gain-of-function research: every tool designed to defend against a biological threat can be repurposed to create one.</p>
<p>A vaccine platform designed to rapidly respond to emerging viruses can also be used to rapidly create them. A genetic sequencing capability designed to identify novel pathogens can also be used to engineer them. A gain-of-function experiment designed to predict how a virus might evolve naturally can instead demonstrate exactly how to make it more dangerous intentionally.</p>
<p>This dual-use dilemma is not theoretical. The Soviet Union maintained a massive covert bioweapons program called Biopreparat throughout the Cold War, employing tens of thousands of scientists in facilities nominally designated as civilian research institutes. Soviet defector Ken Alibek (formerly Kanatjan Alibekov), who served as the first deputy director of Biopreparat, revealed in his 1999 book <em>Biohazard</em> that the Soviet program had weaponized anthrax, smallpox, plague, tularemia, and dozens of other pathogens — all while officially complying with the Biological Weapons Convention.</p>
<p>Japan's Unit 731, which conducted horrific biological warfare experiments on prisoners during World War II, was similarly housed within an ostensibly legitimate research organization. After the war, the United States granted immunity to Unit 731's leaders in exchange for their biological weapons research data — data that was subsequently incorporated into America's own biological weapons program at Fort Detrick.</p>
<p>The pattern is consistent across nations and decades: biological weapons programs hide behind the facade of legitimate scientific research. The laboratories, the personnel, the pathogens, and the techniques are identical. Only the stated intent differs — and stated intent is the flimsiest of safeguards against the weaponization of biology.</p>

<h2 id="ecohealth-alliance-the-nonprofit-shield">EcoHealth Alliance: The Nonprofit Shield</h2>
<p>EcoHealth Alliance, led by British zoologist Peter Daszak, occupies a critical position in the bioweapons-to-gain-of-function pipeline. As a nonprofit organization, EcoHealth served as a bureaucratic intermediary between U.S. government agencies (NIH, DARPA, USAID) and foreign laboratories, including the Wuhan Institute of Virology.</p>
<p>This structure provided several advantages to the funding agencies. First, it created a layer of plausible deniability: if controversial research was conducted at a foreign laboratory, U.S. agencies could claim they did not directly fund or supervise it. Second, it circumvented restrictions on gain-of-function research by outsourcing the most sensitive experiments to laboratories in countries with less stringent biosafety oversight. Third, it allowed military-adjacent research to be conducted under the auspices of a civilian nonprofit, obscuring the national security dimensions of the work.</p>
<p>Daszak's dual role as both an NIH-funded researcher and a member of the WHO team investigating the origins of COVID-19 represented a conflict of interest so brazen it bordered on parody. The man who had funded and collaborated on coronavirus research at the Wuhan Institute of Virology was appointed to investigate whether that same laboratory might have been the source of the pandemic. Unsurprisingly, the WHO investigation concluded that a laboratory origin was "extremely unlikely" — a conclusion that the WHO's own director-general, Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus, subsequently walked back.</p>
<p>As detailed in our investigation of <a href="/event-201-pandemic-blueprint/?lang=en">Event 201: The Pandemic Blueprint</a>, the network connecting EcoHealth Alliance, the Johns Hopkins Center for Health Security, the Gates Foundation, and the World Economic Forum reveals a system in which the same organizations that fund pandemic-capable research also design the response frameworks that govern pandemic policy.</p>

<h2 id="the-covid-19-origin-question-laboratory-or-nature">The COVID-19 Origin Question: Laboratory or Nature?</h2>
<p>The question of whether SARS-CoV-2 emerged naturally from an animal reservoir or was released — accidentally or intentionally — from the Wuhan Institute of Virology is arguably the most consequential scientific question of the twenty-first century. The evidence, as it has accumulated, increasingly points toward a laboratory origin.</p>
<p>The circumstantial evidence is substantial. The Wuhan Institute of Virology is located approximately 300 meters from the Huanan Seafood Market, which was initially identified as the epicenter of the outbreak. The WIV housed the world's largest collection of bat coronaviruses. The WIV was conducting gain-of-function research on bat coronaviruses, funded by American taxpayer dollars through EcoHealth Alliance. The WIV had experienced documented biosafety concerns, including a 2018 cable from U.S. Embassy officials in Beijing warning of inadequate safety protocols at the laboratory.</p>
<p>The molecular evidence is equally suggestive. SARS-CoV-2 contains a furin cleavage site — a genetic feature that enhances the virus's ability to infect human cells — that is not found in any closely related bat coronavirus. This furin cleavage site is precisely the type of feature that gain-of-function researchers would insert to study enhanced transmissibility. The EcoHealth Alliance DARPA PREEMPT proposal, leaked in 2021, specifically described plans to insert furin cleavage sites into bat coronaviruses.</p>
<p>The genomic backbone of SARS-CoV-2 also shows unusual features. Unlike SARS-CoV-1, which left a clear trail of intermediate animal hosts, no intermediate host for SARS-CoV-2 has been identified despite years of intensive searching. The virus appeared to be pre-adapted for human infection from the earliest known cases, displaying none of the genetic diversification expected during a natural zoonotic spillover event.</p>
<p>The institutional response to the lab-origin hypothesis was itself revealing. Scientists who privately acknowledged the plausibility of a laboratory origin publicly dismissed it as a "conspiracy theory" — a coordinated messaging effort documented in emails obtained through Freedom of Information Act requests. The <em>Lancet</em> published a statement organized by Peter Daszak condemning the lab-leak hypothesis as misinformation, without disclosing Daszak's financial and professional ties to the WIV.</p>

<h2 id="the-biodefense-to-bioweapon-pipeline">The Biodefense-to-Bioweapon Pipeline</h2>
<p>The United States has spent over $100 billion on biodefense since the 2001 anthrax attacks — attacks that were ultimately traced not to a foreign adversary but to a U.S. government scientist working at Fort Detrick. This massive expenditure has created an enormous biodefense industrial complex, with hundreds of laboratories across the country and around the world conducting research on the most dangerous pathogens known to humanity.</p>
<p>The proliferation of these laboratories exponentially increases the risk of accidental release. A 2009 report by the National Research Council found that the expansion of high-containment biological laboratories in the United States had outpaced the development of adequate oversight mechanisms. By 2015, more than 1,500 facilities in the United States were registered to work with select agents — the most dangerous pathogens — up from fewer than 100 in 2001.</p>
<p>Each of these facilities represents a potential point of origin for the next pandemic. And each of them is staffed by scientists who, by the nature of their work, possess the knowledge and the access required to create biological weapons. The system designed to protect against bioterrorism has instead created the infrastructure for it.</p>
<p>As our article on the <a href="/military-pharmaceutical-partnership/?lang=en">Military-Pharmaceutical Partnership</a> documents, the pharmaceutical industry is deeply embedded in this biodefense apparatus. The same companies that develop vaccines and therapeutics also serve as government contractors for biodefense research, creating a financial incentive structure in which pandemic preparedness and pandemic profiteering become indistinguishable.</p>
