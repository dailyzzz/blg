15:T40b5,<h1 id="cvs-immediately-bans-covid-shots-at-some-pharmacies">CVS Immediately Bans COVID Shots at Some Pharmacies</h1>
<h2 id="the-ancient-pattern">The Ancient Pattern</h2>
<blockquote>
<p>"Can the Ethiopian change his skin, or the leopard his spots? then may ye also do good, that are accustomed to do evil." — Jeremiah 13:23</p>
</blockquote>
<p>When Pharaoh's heart was hardened, he did not release the Israelites because it was right. He released them because the plagues made the cost of holding them unbearable. His compliance was not repentance — it was calculation. And the moment the pressure subsided, he pursued them to the Red Sea.</p>
<p>As the Confliction track <a href="https://confliction.zzzdaily.com/tracks/en/127-The_Overcoming.mp4">The Overcoming</a> reveals, the battle between good and evil is not won through the voluntary compliance of the wicked but through the overwhelming power of truth that makes the continuation of the lie untenable. CVS did not ban COVID shots because it discovered a conscience. It banned them because the ground beneath the COVID-19 injection program has shifted — legally, politically, and commercially — and the cost of continuing has begun to exceed the cost of stopping.</p>
<p>The question is not why CVS stopped. The question is why it took so long — and how many millions of injections were administered while the company knew, or should have known, that the products it was dispensing had not been subjected to adequate safety review.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="the-pharmacy-as-distribution-channel">The Pharmacy as Distribution Channel</h2>
<p>During the COVID-19 pandemic, pharmacies became the primary distribution channel for COVID-19 injections in the United States. CVS Health, the nation's largest pharmacy chain with over 9,900 locations, administered tens of millions of COVID-19 doses through its retail pharmacies and MinuteClinic locations.</p>
<p>The Federal Retail Pharmacy Program for COVID-19 Vaccination — a partnership between the federal government and pharmacy chains — was announced in November 2020, before any vaccine had received Emergency Use Authorization. The program enrolled CVS, Walgreens, Walmart, Rite Aid, and other major pharmacy chains as distribution nodes in the national vaccination campaign.</p>
<p>Under this program, pharmacies received COVID-19 vaccine doses directly from the federal government at no cost. The pharmacies were reimbursed for administration fees — approximately $40 per dose — through Medicare, Medicaid, and private insurance, as well as through the HRSA COVID-19 Uninsured Program for patients without insurance. At scale, these administration fees generated billions in revenue for pharmacy chains.</p>
<p>Karen Kingston has documented in The Kingston Report (karenkingston.substack.com) that the pharmacy distribution model served a specific strategic purpose: it bypassed the traditional physician-patient relationship. "When you receive a vaccine at your doctor's office, your physician reviews your medical history, assesses your risk factors, discusses the risks and benefits, and makes an individualized recommendation," Kingston has written. "When you receive a vaccine at a pharmacy, a pharmacist or pharmacy technician administers it according to a protocol — no medical history review, no individualized risk assessment, no physician oversight. The pharmacy model was designed to maximize throughput, not safety."</p>
<h3 id="the-prep-act-shield-at-the-pharmacy-counter">The PREP Act Shield at the Pharmacy Counter</h3>
<p>Pharmacists and pharmacy technicians who administered COVID-19 injections were covered under the PREP Act liability shield. This meant that even if a pharmacist administered a dose to a patient with known contraindications, even if the pharmacist failed to screen for allergies or medical conditions, even if the pharmacist administered the wrong dose or used an expired vial — the pharmacist, the pharmacy, and the manufacturer were all shielded from liability.</p>
<p>This liability shield removed the only mechanism that normally incentivizes caution in medical practice: the fear of consequences for negligence. With liability eliminated, the incentive structure was entirely one-directional — more doses administered meant more revenue, with zero legal risk.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="the-ban-what-happened">The Ban: What Happened</h2>
<p>CVS Health announced the immediate cessation of COVID-19 vaccination services at select pharmacy locations. The announcement cited multiple factors:</p>
<p><strong>Declining demand:</strong> Public uptake of COVID-19 boosters had plummeted from pandemic-era highs. The most recent booster campaign achieved an uptake rate below 20% of the eligible population — a dramatic decline from the initial vaccination campaign that reached over 70% of adults.</p>
<p><strong>Regulatory uncertainty:</strong> With the FDA's termination of Emergency Use Authorizations and HHS leadership openly questioning the safety of mRNA technology, the regulatory landscape for COVID-19 injections has become unstable. Pharmacies that continue to administer these products face potential future liability exposure if the PREP Act shield is modified or vacated.</p>
<p><strong>Inventory management:</strong> COVID-19 vaccine doses have a limited shelf life and require specialized cold chain storage. With declining demand, pharmacies have been discarding significant quantities of expired doses — representing both waste and reputational risk.</p>
<p><strong>Staff concerns:</strong> Pharmacy workers — including pharmacists and technicians — have increasingly expressed concern about administering products whose safety profile is under active scrutiny by federal health authorities. Several pharmacy employee organizations have called for the right to decline administration without professional consequences.</p>
<h3 id="what-cvs-did-not-say">What CVS Did Not Say</h3>
<p>CVS did not acknowledge the safety concerns that have driven the decline in public demand. The company did not reference the VAERS data documenting hundreds of thousands of adverse event reports. It did not reference the myocarditis signal in young males. It did not reference the excess mortality data. It did not reference the growing body of peer-reviewed research documenting adverse effects of mRNA COVID-19 injections.</p>
<p>The company framed its decision as a response to market conditions — demand has declined, and the business case for maintaining COVID-19 vaccination services at all locations no longer exists. This framing allows CVS to retreat from the vaccination program without admitting that the products it administered to millions of Americans may have caused harm.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="the-revenue-machine">The Revenue Machine</h2>
<p>To understand CVS's decision, one must understand the scale of the revenue the company generated from COVID-19 vaccinations.</p>
<p>CVS Health reported over $11 billion in COVID-19-related revenue across 2021-2023, encompassing vaccination administration fees, COVID-19 testing services, and related pharmacy sales. The company administered over 59 million COVID-19 vaccine and booster doses during this period.</p>
<p>At approximately $40 per dose in administration fees — multiplied across 59 million doses — the vaccination program generated approximately $2.36 billion in administration fee revenue alone. This does not account for additional revenue from COVID-19 testing, related pharmacy purchases, and the customer acquisition value of the millions of new patients who entered CVS locations for vaccination services.</p>
<p>Kingston has observed: "CVS did not administer 59 million doses because it cared about public health. It administered 59 million doses because each dose generated $40 in revenue under a liability-free legal framework. The moment the revenue opportunity declines and the liability landscape shifts, the 'public health' mission evaporates. This tells you everything you need to know about what motivated the program."</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="the-pharmacists-dilemma">The Pharmacist's Dilemma</h2>
<p>Pharmacists occupy a unique position in the healthcare system. They are licensed professionals with doctoral-level education in pharmacology, drug interactions, and patient safety. They take an oath to prioritize patient welfare. Yet during the COVID-19 pandemic, pharmacists were placed in an impossible position.</p>
<p>The protocols governing COVID-19 vaccination at pharmacies were set not by the pharmacist's independent professional judgment but by federal and corporate directives. Pharmacists were instructed to administer the injections to every eligible patient who requested them. Screening protocols were minimal — a brief questionnaire about allergies and current illness, with no review of the patient's complete medical history.</p>
<p>Pharmacists who expressed concerns about the products — who questioned their safety, who reported adverse events, who recommended caution for specific patient populations — faced professional consequences. State pharmacy boards, guided by CDC and FDA positions, treated any deviation from the recommended vaccination protocols as potential grounds for disciplinary action.</p>
<p>Kingston has documented cases in which pharmacists who refused to administer COVID-19 injections — based on their professional judgment that the products posed unacceptable risks for specific patients — were terminated, reported to licensing boards, and subjected to professional investigation. "The pharmacist's oath to 'do no harm' was functionally suspended during COVID-19," Kingston has stated. "Any pharmacist who acted on that oath — who declined to administer a product they believed was harmful — was punished. The system demanded compliance, not professional judgment."</p>

The motivation has been, is, and will remain financial. The leopard has not changed its spots. It has merely repositioned itself for the next commercial opportunity.</p>
