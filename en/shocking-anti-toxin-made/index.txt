<h2 id="the-anti-toxin-production-process">The Anti-Toxin Production Process</h2>
<h3 id="step-1-horse-selection">Step 1: Horse Selection</h3>
<p>The production of anti-toxin begins with horses — specifically, large, healthy horses selected for their robust immune systems and their ability to survive repeated toxic insults. These horses are purchased from breeders and housed at pharmaceutical production facilities.</p>
<p>The horses are not treated as living beings. They are treated as biological factories — their bodies are the production equipment, and their blood is the product.</p>
<h3 id="step-2-toxin-injection">Step 2: Toxin Injection</h3>
<p>The horses are injected with gradually increasing doses of the toxin that the anti-toxin is designed to counteract. For diphtheria anti-toxin, the horses are injected with diphtheria toxin. For tetanus anti-toxin, they receive tetanus toxin. For anti-venom, they are injected with snake venom.</p>
<p>The process — called "hyperimmunization" — takes weeks to months. During this time:</p>
<ul>
<li>The horses experience <strong>severe systemic reactions</strong> to the toxins</li>

<li>Surviving horses are <strong>stressed, weakened, and in chronic pain</strong></li>
</ul>
<p>The doses are increased gradually — not out of compassion for the horses, but because killing the horse too quickly means losing the production asset. The horse must survive long enough to produce the desired antibodies.</p>
<h3 id="step-3-blood-harvesting">Step 3: Blood Harvesting</h3>

<h2 id="the-historical-context">The Historical Context</h2>
<h3 id="the-diphtheria-anti-toxin-story">The Diphtheria Anti-Toxin Story</h3>
<p>Diphtheria anti-toxin was one of the first major pharmaceutical products, developed in the 1890s by Emil von Behring and Kitasato Shibasaburo. The product was hailed as a miracle — a cure for a disease that killed thousands of children annually.</p>
<p>But the production process was nightmarish from the beginning. Horses were poisoned with diphtheria toxin in stables and barns, their blood was drained into buckets, and the serum was extracted using crude equipment. The process killed horses regularly, and the product was frequently contaminated.</p>
<p>In 1901, the "St. Louis tragedy" brought the dangers of anti-toxin production to public attention: contaminated diphtheria anti-toxin killed 13 children in St. Louis. The anti-toxin had been produced from a horse named Jim that had developed tetanus. The contaminated serum was administered to children who subsequently died of tetanus — killed by the very product that was supposed to save them.</p>
<p>The St. Louis tragedy led to the Biologics Control Act of 1902 — the first federal regulation of pharmaceutical products. But the fundamental production process — poisoning horses and draining their blood — continued unchanged. It continues today.</p>
