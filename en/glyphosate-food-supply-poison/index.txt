15:T3d7f,<h1 id="glyphosate-in-the-food-supply-roundup-on-your-plate">Glyphosate in the Food Supply: Roundup on Your Plate</h1>
<h2 id="the-ancient-pattern">The Ancient Pattern</h2>
<blockquote>
<p>"Cursed is the ground because of you; through painful toil you will eat food from it all the days of your life." — Genesis 3:17</p>
</blockquote>
<p>When Adam and Eve fell, the ground itself was cursed. What had once produced food freely now required labor, struggle, and suffering. But even cursed ground was still God's ground — it still produced real food, nourished by real soil, governed by real biological processes. What Monsanto has done with glyphosate is something different entirely: the deliberate poisoning of the ground itself, the systematic destruction of the soil biology that makes food nutritious, and the saturation of the food supply with a chemical that attacks the human body at the cellular level. This is not a curse on the ground. This is war against the ground — and against every living thing that depends on it.</p>
<p>As the Confliction album track <a href="https://confliction.zzzdaily.com/tracks/en/43-Hunger_and_Thirst.mp4">Hunger and Thirst</a> laments, what God provided as sustenance has been corrupted into poison. The provision remains, but it has been contaminated by forces that profit from the corruption and deny its consequences.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="what-is-glyphosate">What Is Glyphosate?</h2>
<p>Glyphosate is the active ingredient in Roundup, the world's most widely used herbicide. It was first patented by Monsanto in 1974 as a weed killer. Its mechanism of action is deceptively simple: glyphosate inhibits the shikimate pathway, an enzymatic process essential for plant growth. Since this pathway exists in plants but not in mammals, Monsanto marketed Roundup as harmless to humans and animals.</p>
<p>This claim was the foundation of a four-decade global marketing campaign that would make Roundup the most commercially successful agricultural chemical in history. By 2014, over 826 million kilograms of glyphosate were being applied annually worldwide — enough to spray nearly half a pound on every cultivated acre of land on Earth.</p>
<p>But the claim that glyphosate is harmless to humans because humans lack the shikimate pathway contained a critical omission: the trillions of bacteria in the human gut — the microbiome — do possess the shikimate pathway. Glyphosate does not kill humans directly. It kills the bacteria that keep humans alive.</p>
<p>The human microbiome is not merely a digestive aid. It is a foundational system that regulates immune function, produces neurotransmitters (including approximately 90% of the body's serotonin), synthesizes vitamins, maintains the intestinal barrier, and modulates gene expression. When glyphosate disrupts the gut microbiome, it does not produce a single symptom. It produces a cascade of systemic dysfunction that manifests as autoimmune disease, neurological impairment, hormonal disruption, and cancer.</p>
<h2 id="the-monsanto-papers">The Monsanto Papers</h2>
<p>In 2017, as part of litigation against Monsanto (now owned by Bayer), internal company documents were released through legal discovery. These documents — known as the Monsanto Papers — revealed what the company knew about glyphosate's health effects and what it did to suppress that knowledge.</p>
<p>The documents revealed that:</p>
<p><strong>Monsanto ghostwrote scientific studies.</strong> Internal emails showed that Monsanto employees drafted studies that were then published under the names of academic scientists, giving industry-funded research the appearance of independent validation. One email from a Monsanto toxicologist stated: "We would be keeping the costs down by us doing the writing."</p>
<p><strong>Monsanto attacked independent researchers.</strong> Scientists who published findings linking glyphosate to health problems were targeted with coordinated campaigns to discredit their work, defund their laboratories, and destroy their careers. Professor Gilles-Eric Seralini, whose 2012 study showed tumors in rats fed GMO corn sprayed with Roundup, was subjected to a systematic campaign that resulted in the retraction of his paper — a retraction later reversed when the paper was republished in a different journal after independent review confirmed its findings.</p>
<p><strong>Monsanto influenced regulatory agencies.</strong> Documents showed that a senior EPA official, Jess Rowland, worked to suppress a review of glyphosate by the Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry (ATSDR), telling Monsanto: "If I can kill this I should get a medal." Rowland retired shortly after these communications were exposed.</p>
<p><strong>Monsanto knew glyphosate caused cancer.</strong> Internal documents revealed that Monsanto's own toxicologists had concerns about glyphosate's carcinogenic potential as early as the 1980s but chose to suppress and reinterpret the data rather than acknowledge the risk.</p>
<p>In 2015, the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC), a division of the World Health Organization, classified glyphosate as "probably carcinogenic to humans" based on a comprehensive review of the scientific evidence. Monsanto immediately launched a campaign to discredit the IARC finding, spending millions on lobbyists, PR firms, and academic allies to defend its product.</p>
<p>By 2024, Bayer (which acquired Monsanto in 2018 for $63 billion) had paid over $11 billion to settle more than 100,000 lawsuits from individuals who claimed Roundup caused their non-Hodgkin lymphoma. Bayer settled while officially denying that Roundup causes cancer — a legal maneuver that allowed the company to avoid a definitive judicial ruling while acknowledging the practical reality that juries consistently found against them.</p>
<h2 id="glyphosate-as-desiccant-sprayed-directly-on-your-food">Glyphosate as Desiccant: Sprayed Directly on Your Food</h2>
<p>Most people assume glyphosate is sprayed on weeds around crops. This is only part of the story. Since the late 1990s, glyphosate has been increasingly used as a pre-harvest desiccant — sprayed directly on food crops shortly before harvest to accelerate drying and make harvesting more uniform.</p>
<p>This practice means that glyphosate is applied to wheat, oats, barley, lentils, beans, chickpeas, sugar cane, and numerous other crops at the point of maximum absorption — just days before they are harvested, processed, and turned into the food on your plate.</p>
<p>Testing by the Canadian Food Inspection Agency found glyphosate residues in approximately 30% of food products tested, with the highest levels found in grain-based products. The Environmental Working Group found glyphosate in 43 of 45 conventional oat-based food products tested, including popular children's cereals and granola bars. A study published in the journal Environmental Health found detectable levels of glyphosate in the urine of 93% of participants tested, with children having the highest levels.</p>
<p>You are not accidentally exposed to glyphosate. You are systematically fed glyphosate, and the system that feeds it to you calls it safe.</p>
<h2 id="the-gut-destruction-pathway">The Gut Destruction Pathway</h2>
<p>Dr. Stephanie Seneff of MIT has published extensively on the mechanisms by which glyphosate damages human health. Her research, compiled in the book "Toxic Legacy," identifies multiple pathways of harm:</p>
<p><strong>Microbiome destruction.</strong> Glyphosate preferentially kills beneficial gut bacteria (Lactobacillus, Bifidobacterium) while leaving pathogenic bacteria (Clostridium, Salmonella) relatively unharmed. This dysbiosis is linked to inflammatory bowel disease, autoimmune conditions, depression, and obesity.</p>
<p><strong>Amino acid disruption.</strong> The shikimate pathway that glyphosate inhibits in plants and bacteria produces aromatic amino acids — tryptophan, tyrosine, and phenylalanine — that are precursors to serotonin, dopamine, melanin, and thyroid hormones. By disrupting microbial production of these amino acids, glyphosate contributes to depression, neurological dysfunction, thyroid disease, and hormonal imbalance.</p>
<p><strong>Chelation of minerals.</strong> Glyphosate was originally patented as a metal chelator — a chemical that binds to minerals and makes them unavailable. In soil, it binds manganese, zinc, iron, and cobalt, reducing the mineral content of crops. In the human body, it binds the same essential minerals, contributing to nutritional deficiency even when dietary intake appears adequate.</p>
<p><strong>Endocrine disruption.</strong> Studies published in Toxicological Sciences and Environmental Health Perspectives have shown that glyphosate disrupts estrogen receptors at concentrations far below those found in the food supply. This endocrine disruption is linked to reproductive disorders, early puberty, and hormone-dependent cancers.</p>
<p><strong>Glyphosate and celiac disease.</strong> Research published in the journal Interdisciplinary Toxicology found a striking correlation between the rise of celiac disease and the increasing use of glyphosate on wheat as a desiccant. The paper hypothesized that glyphosate damages the gut lining, disrupts digestive enzyme function, and impairs the body's ability to process gluten — suggesting that the "gluten epidemic" may be, in significant part, a glyphosate epidemic.</p>
<h2 id="the-gmo-connection">The GMO Connection</h2>
<p>Glyphosate's market dominance is inseparable from genetically modified organisms. As detailed in our article on <a href="/gmo-genetic-modification-food/?lang=en">GMOs: Genetic Modification of the Food Supply</a>, Monsanto created "Roundup Ready" crops — soybeans, corn, cotton, canola, sugar beets, and alfalfa genetically engineered to survive being sprayed with Roundup.</p>
<p>This created a closed commercial ecosystem: farmers bought Monsanto's patented seeds and Monsanto's patented herbicide together. The more GMO crops were planted, the more Roundup was sold. The more Roundup was sold, the more dependent farmers became on GMO seeds. By 2014, over 90% of soybeans and 89% of corn planted in the United States were Roundup Ready varieties.</p>
<p>The environmental consequences have been catastrophic. Weed species have evolved resistance to glyphosate, creating "superweeds" that require ever-increasing applications of herbicide — or the addition of even more toxic chemicals like 2,4-D (a component of Agent Orange) to the mix. The soil biology that sustains agriculture is being systematically destroyed. The pollinators that food production depends on — bees, butterflies, beneficial insects — are collapsing under the weight of chemical exposure, as documented in our article on <a href="/bee-colony-collapse-food-chain/?lang=en">Bee Colony Collapse</a>.</p>
<h2 id="regulatory-capture-and-the-revolving-door">Regulatory Capture and the Revolving Door</h2>
<p>The regulatory agencies tasked with protecting the public from toxic chemicals have been systematically captured by the companies they regulate. The revolving door between Monsanto and the EPA, FDA, and USDA is not a metaphor — it is a documented personnel pipeline:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Michael Taylor</strong> served as FDA Deputy Commissioner for Policy while simultaneously holding a position as Monsanto's Vice President for Public Policy. Under his influence, the FDA adopted a policy of "substantial equivalence" that treated GMO foods as identical to conventional foods, exempting them from safety testing.</li>
<li><strong>Clarence Thomas</strong>, the Supreme Court Justice who wrote the majority opinion in the landmark case that allowed the patenting of genetically modified organisms, was formerly a Monsanto attorney.</li>
<li><strong>Margaret Miller</strong>, who worked for Monsanto developing bovine growth hormone, was appointed to the FDA where she approved her own research.</li>
</ul>
<p>This is not oversight that occasionally fails. This is a system designed to ensure that the industry writes its own rules, reviews its own safety data, and approves its own products — with the full authority of the United States government behind it.</p>
<p>As our article on <a href="/organic-farming-under-attack/?lang=en">Organic Farming Under Attack</a> documents, the same regulatory apparatus that protects glyphosate actively attacks the alternatives. Organic farmers face regulatory burdens, certification costs, and legal threats that conventional chemical agriculture is exempt from. The system is rigged to protect poison and penalize purity.</p>
