<h2 id="the-silver-question">The Silver Question</h2>
<h3 id="bimetallism-vs-the-gold-standard">Bimetallism vs. the Gold Standard</h3>
<p>To understand the Panic of 1893, you must understand the monetary battle that defined late 19th-century America: the struggle between bimetallism (using both gold and silver as monetary standards) and the gold standard (using gold alone).</p>
<p>Bimetallism favored farmers, small businesses, and the working class. Silver was more abundant than gold, and a bimetallic standard meant more money in circulation, lower interest rates, and easier access to credit. The gold standard, by contrast, favored bankers and creditors. Less money in circulation meant higher interest rates, tighter credit, and greater power for those who controlled the limited supply of gold.</p>
<p>The banking elite — the Rothschilds, the Morgans, the Rockefellers, all intimately connected to the Jesuit-Vatican power structure — wanted the gold standard because it gave them control over the money supply. With gold as the sole basis of currency, whoever controlled the gold controlled the economy. And the gold was concentrated in the vaults of a handful of banks, all controlled by the same network of families and secret societies.</p>
<h3 id="the-sherman-silver-purchase-act">The Sherman Silver Purchase Act</h3>
<p>In 1890, Congress passed the Sherman Silver Purchase Act, which required the federal government to purchase 4.5 million ounces of silver per month at market prices, paying with Treasury notes redeemable in gold. This was a compromise between the silver interests (primarily Western mining states and agricultural regions) and the gold interests (primarily Eastern banks and industrial interests).</p>
<p>The Act was designed to fail. By requiring that silver purchases be redeemable in gold, it created a mechanism for draining the Treasury's gold reserves. Every silver purchase note that was redeemed in gold reduced the government's gold supply. And the banking elite, who controlled vast holdings of Treasury notes, could — and did — present those notes for gold redemption at a rate designed to deplete the reserves.</p>
<p>This was not an accident. It was a trap — a financial booby trap designed by the same minds that engineered the Columbian Exposition. The silver purchases would inflate the money supply temporarily (creating a brief boom), while the gold redemption mechanism would drain the Treasury (creating the conditions for a catastrophic bust). The timing was precise: the boom coincided with the planning and construction of the World's Fair, and the bust arrived just as the fair opened its gates.</p>
<h2 id="the-panic-unfolds">The Panic Unfolds</h2>
<h3 id="the-run-on-gold">The Run on Gold</h3>
<p>By early 1893, the Treasury's gold reserves had fallen below $100 million — the psychological threshold that was considered the minimum for maintaining confidence in the dollar. On February 20, 1893, the Philadelphia and Reading Railroad went bankrupt, triggering a stock market crash. Banks began calling in loans, businesses failed by the thousands, and unemployment soared.</p>
<p>The Panic of 1893 was the worst economic crisis in American history to that point. Over 15,000 businesses failed. 500 banks collapsed. Unemployment reached 25% in some industrial cities. Farmers who had borrowed during the boom years found themselves unable to repay their loans and lost their land to foreclosure — land that was then purchased at pennies on the dollar by the same banks that had engineered the crisis.</p>
<h3 id="the-exposition-as-distraction">The Exposition as Distraction</h3>

<p>The exposition was also an economic extraction device. The money flowed from the pockets of ordinary Americans into the coffers of the Masonic-connected businesses that controlled the fair's concessions, transportation, and hospitality infrastructure.</p>
<p>While ordinary Americans suffered through the worst depression they had ever experienced, the men who controlled the exposition and its associated businesses profited enormously.
