<h2 id="nanotechnology-in-the-food-supply">Nanotechnology in the Food Supply</h2>
<h3 id="what-already-exists">What Already Exists</h3>
<p>Nanotechnology is already present in the food supply. This is not speculation — it is documented:</p>
<p><strong>Nano-titanium dioxide (TiO2)</strong>: Used as a whitening agent in hundreds of food products, including candy, chewing gum, pastries, and sauces. Studies have shown that nano-TiO2 particles can cross the blood-brain barrier, accumulate in organs, and alter gut microbiome composition.</p>
<p><strong>Nano-silver</strong>: Used as an antimicrobial agent in food packaging and processing equipment. Silver nanoparticles can migrate from packaging into food and accumulate in the body.</p>
<p><strong>Nano-silicon dioxide (SiO2)</strong>: Used as an anti-caking agent in powdered foods, spices, and coffee creamer. Nanoparticles of SiO2 have been found in the bloodstream of people consuming products containing them.</p>
<p><strong>Nano-encapsulation</strong>: Food companies use nanotechnology to encapsulate flavors, vitamins, and other additives — creating particles that survive digestion and deliver their payload to specific locations in the body.</p>
<p>These are legitimate, commercially deployed food technologies. They demonstrate that the food industry has the capability and the willingness to incorporate nanotechnology into the food supply.</p>
