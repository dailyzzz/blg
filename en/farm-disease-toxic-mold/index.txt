<h2 id="the-hidden-epidemic">The Hidden Epidemic</h2>
<h3 id="what-is-toxic-mold">What Is Toxic Mold?</h3>
<p>Mold is a fungus. It is everywhere — in soil, in air, on surfaces. Most mold species are harmless. But certain species — particularly Stachybotrys chartarum (black mold), Aspergillus, and Penicillium — produce mycotoxins: chemical compounds that are toxic to humans and animals.</p>
<p>Mycotoxins cause a range of health effects: respiratory disease, neurological damage, immune suppression, chronic fatigue, cognitive impairment, liver damage, and cancer. These effects are dose-dependent and cumulative — meaning that low-level chronic exposure, the kind that occurs in contaminated buildings and on contaminated farms, causes progressive health deterioration that is often misdiagnosed as other conditions.</p>
<p>The medical establishment has been slow to recognize mycotoxin illness. Many doctors are not trained to identify it. Insurance companies do not routinely cover testing or treatment. And the industries responsible for mold exposure — construction, agriculture, real estate — have powerful financial incentives to minimize the problem.</p>
<h3 id="the-farm-crisis">The Farm Crisis</h3>
<p>Farms are particularly vulnerable to toxic mold. Grain storage facilities, barns, silos, and farmhouses provide the three conditions mold needs: moisture, organic material, and warmth. Farmers who spend their days working in and around contaminated structures receive chronic, high-level exposure to mycotoxins.</p>
<p>The health consequences are devastating. Farmer's lung — a chronic respiratory condition caused by inhaling mold spores — has been documented for decades. But the broader spectrum of mycotoxin illness — neurological symptoms, chronic fatigue, immune dysfunction, cognitive decline — is less well-recognized, largely because the agricultural industry has lobbied against research that would establish the connection.</p>
<p>Agent131711 documents on Substack (agent131711.substack.com) how the agricultural industry has suppressed research on mycotoxin illness, using the same regulatory capture strategies described in earlier articles in this series. The USDA, which oversees agricultural safety, is staffed by former industry executives who return to industry after their government service. The revolving door ensures that research priorities protect industry, not farmers.</p>
<h3 id="mycotoxins-in-the-food-supply">Mycotoxins in the Food Supply</h3>
<p>The mold crisis extends beyond farms into the food supply. Grains, nuts, coffee, dried fruits, and spices are all susceptible to mycotoxin contamination. Aflatoxin — a mycotoxin produced by Aspergillus species — is one of the most potent carcinogens known. It is commonly found in peanuts, corn, and tree nuts.</p>
<p>The FDA sets limits on aflatoxin levels in food but does not require comprehensive testing. The result is that mycotoxin-contaminated food enters the supply chain regularly. Consumers have no way to know whether the peanut butter, corn chips, or coffee they consume contains mycotoxins — because the system does not require testing and the industry does not volunteer it.</p>
