<h2 id="false-flag-1-the-sinking-of-the-lusitania">False Flag #1: The Sinking of the Lusitania</h2>
<h3 id="the-setup">The Setup</h3>
<p>On May 7, 1915, the British ocean liner RMS Lusitania was torpedoed by a German U-boat off the coast of Ireland. The ship sank in 18 minutes, killing 1,198 people, including 128 Americans. The sinking was immediately used as propaganda to push the United States toward entering the war.</p>
<p>But the official story conceals critical facts:</p>
<p><strong>The Lusitania was carrying munitions.</strong> Despite being classified as a passenger vessel, the Lusitania's cargo included 4.2 million rounds of rifle ammunition, 1,248 cases of shrapnel shells, and 18 cases of non-explosive fuses. This was a violation of the rules governing neutral shipping and made the Lusitania a legitimate military target under international law. The British Admiralty knew this. The American government knew this. The passengers did not.</p>
<p><strong>The German government warned American travelers.</strong> The German Embassy in Washington placed advertisements in 50 American newspapers warning that vessels flying the British flag in the war zone were liable to destruction. The Imperial German Embassy's notice ran alongside Cunard's own advertisement for the Lusitania's voyage. American travelers were explicitly warned — and the U.S. government did nothing to prevent them from boarding.</p>
<p><strong>The British Admiralty withdrew the Lusitania's naval escort.</strong> The cruiser HMS Juno, which had been assigned to escort the Lusitania through the U-boat danger zone, was recalled to port just before the liner entered the area. Winston Churchill, then First Lord of the Admiralty, was responsible for naval dispositions. The withdrawal of the escort left the Lusitania defenseless — as if by design.</p>
