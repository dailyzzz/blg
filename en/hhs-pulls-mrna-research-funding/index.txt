15:T4a97,<h1 id="hhs-pulls-500-million-in-mrna-research-funding">HHS Pulls $500 Million in mRNA Research Funding</h1>
<h2 id="the-ancient-pattern">The Ancient Pattern</h2>
<blockquote>
<p>"For the love of money is the root of all evil: which while some coveted after, they have erred from the faith, and pierced themselves through with many sorrows." — 1 Timothy 6:10</p>
</blockquote>
<p>In the days of Babel, men sought to build a tower that would reach heaven — not to glorify God, but to make a name for themselves and to defy the boundaries He had established between the created and the Creator. The project was funded by collective ambition and constructed with the latest technology of the age. It was not a house of worship. It was an engine of rebellion — a monument to man's conviction that he could transcend his nature, rewrite the order of creation, and become as gods.</p>
<p>God scattered them and confused their language. The tower was never completed.</p>
<p>As the Confliction track <a href="https://confliction.zzzdaily.com/tracks/en/47-The_Transgression.mp4">The Transgression</a> reveals, the sin of transgression is not mere disobedience — it is the attempt to cross a boundary that God has established. To alter the human genome, to rewrite the code of life itself, to inject synthetic instructions into the cells of billions of human beings — this is not medicine. It is transgression. And the funding that sustains it is the mortar of a modern Babel.</p>
<p>HHS has pulled $500 million from the tower. The question is whether the builders will stop.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="the-mrna-empire-how-we-got-here">The mRNA Empire: How We Got Here</h2>
<p>The mRNA technology platform did not emerge from the COVID-19 pandemic. It was in development for decades, funded primarily by the U.S. Department of Defense through the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA) and by the National Institutes of Health (NIH) through the National Institute of Allergy and Infectious Diseases (NIAID), directed for nearly four decades by Dr. Anthony Fauci.</p>
<p>DARPA's Pandemic Prevention Platform (P3), launched in 2017, aimed to develop the capability to produce nucleic acid-based countermeasures — including mRNA therapies — within 60 days of identifying a pathogen. Moderna's mRNA platform was developed with direct DARPA funding under this program. The company's very name — <strong>Mo</strong>dified <strong>RNA</strong> — describes its technological foundation.</p>
<p>The NIH co-developed the spike protein design used in the Moderna COVID-19 injection. This co-development meant the U.S. government held a direct financial interest in the product — it stood to receive royalty payments from its commercialization. NIH scientists, including those working under Fauci, received personal royalty payments from pharmaceutical companies for technologies they helped develop while employed as public servants.</p>
<p>Karen Kingston has documented in The Kingston Report (karenkingston.substack.com) the financial architecture underlying the mRNA platform. According to Kingston's research, the COVID-19 mRNA injections were not developed under standard pharmaceutical regulatory pathways but under Other Transaction Authority (OTA) contracts — military procurement agreements that exempt the products from standard FDA pharmaceutical regulations. This means the products were never required to meet the safety and efficacy standards of conventional drugs. They were classified as "countermeasures" — military assets deployed under a declared emergency.</p>
<p>"The mRNA platform was funded by the military, developed under military contracts, deployed under military authority, and shielded from liability by military-grade legal protections," Kingston has stated. "The fact that it was administered in pharmacies and doctor's offices does not change what it is. It is a defense technology, not a medical product."</p>
<h3 id="the-500-million-pipeline">The $500 Million Pipeline</h3>
<p>The $500 million in mRNA research funding that HHS has now pulled represents the pipeline — the next generation of mRNA products being developed for influenza, RSV, cancer, autoimmune diseases, rare genetic disorders, and a broad category of "pandemic preparedness" applications. This funding supported research at universities, biotech companies, and federal laboratories across the country.</p>
<p>The pipeline included:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Next-generation COVID-19 boosters</strong> designed to target evolving variants</li>
<li><strong>mRNA influenza vaccines</strong> intended to replace traditional egg-based flu vaccines</li>
<li><strong>Self-amplifying mRNA (sa-mRNA) technology</strong> that instructs cells not only to produce a target protein but to replicate the mRNA itself, theoretically extending the duration and magnitude of the immune response</li>
<li><strong>mRNA cancer vaccines</strong> personalized to individual patients' tumor profiles</li>
<li><strong>Combined mRNA platforms</strong> delivering multiple vaccines in a single injection</li>
</ul>
<p>Each of these applications was built on the same foundational technology deployed during COVID-19 — synthetic mRNA wrapped in lipid nanoparticles, delivered into human cells to instruct those cells to manufacture proteins. The same technology that generated hundreds of thousands of adverse event reports, that distributed throughout the body contrary to initial claims, that concentrated in organs including the ovaries and liver, and that was deployed under legal frameworks specifically designed to prevent accountability.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="the-financial-web">The Financial Web</h2>
<p>The mRNA research funding did not flow in a straight line from HHS to researchers and back. It moved through a web of grants, contracts, sub-awards, and public-private partnerships that connected federal agencies, pharmaceutical companies, universities, and nonprofit organizations in a financial ecosystem designed to obscure accountability and maximize profit.</p>
<p>Karen Kingston has mapped this financial web in detail. Key nodes include:</p>
<p><strong>The Biomedical Advanced Research and Development Authority (BARDA)</strong> — an HHS agency that provided billions in funding for COVID-19 countermeasures and continues to fund mRNA platform development. BARDA operates with unusual secrecy, as many of its contracts are classified or subject to limited disclosure requirements.</p>
<p><strong>The Coalition for Epidemic Preparedness Innovations (CEPI)</strong> — founded in 2017 at the World Economic Forum's annual meeting in Davos, funded by the Bill &#x26; Melinda Gates Foundation, Wellcome Trust, Norway, Germany, Japan, and the European Commission. CEPI funded mRNA vaccine development for pandemic preparedness and played a central role in the COVAX global vaccine distribution program.</p>
<p><strong>The NIH's National Center for Advancing Translational Sciences (NCATS)</strong> — which funds the translation of basic research into clinical applications, including mRNA therapeutics.</p>
<p><strong>University research programs</strong> receiving NIH and DARPA grants for mRNA-related research, many of which have licensing agreements with pharmaceutical companies that entitle the university (and individual researchers) to royalty payments from commercialized products.</p>
<p>The withdrawal of $500 million from this web disrupts not merely individual research projects but the financial relationships that bind federal agencies, pharmaceutical companies, and academic institutions into a self-reinforcing cycle of mutual profit.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="self-amplifying-mrna-the-next-frontier">Self-Amplifying mRNA: The Next Frontier</h2>
<p>Among the most concerning technologies in the defunded pipeline is self-amplifying mRNA (sa-mRNA). Unlike conventional mRNA, which delivers a finite set of instructions that degrade over time, sa-mRNA includes an additional genetic sequence — derived from alphaviruses — that enables the mRNA to replicate itself inside human cells.</p>
<p>This means that once injected, the sa-mRNA produces copies of itself, each copy instructing the cell to manufacture more target protein. The process continues until the cell's machinery degrades the self-amplifying instructions — but the timeline for that degradation is not fully understood, and the total quantity of protein produced by a self-amplifying construct is orders of magnitude greater than conventional mRNA.</p>
<p>Japan approved the world's first sa-mRNA vaccine (ARCT-154, developed by Arcturus Therapeutics and manufactured by CSL Seqirus) for COVID-19 in November 2023. The approval was granted with limited clinical trial data and has generated significant concern among scientists and physicians regarding the potential for uncontrolled protein production, immune system overstimulation, and unknown long-term effects.</p>
<p>Kingston has identified sa-mRNA as a critical escalation in the technological trajectory: "Self-amplifying mRNA is not a vaccine. It is a synthetic genetic program that, once inside your cells, runs autonomously. You cannot stop it. You cannot remove it. You have no control over how much protein it produces or for how long. This is not medicine. This is a self-replicating genetic technology deployed inside the human body."</p>
<p>The defunding of sa-mRNA research represents one of the most significant implications of the HHS decision.</p>

<h2 id="what-the-defunding-means--and-what-it-does-not">What the Defunding Means — and What It Does Not</h2>
<p>The HHS decision to pull $500 million in mRNA research funding is significant for several reasons:</p>
<p><strong>It disrupts the pipeline.</strong> Without federal funding, many mRNA research programs will stall or halt entirely. University labs dependent on NIH grants will lose their funding streams. Early-stage biotech companies without revenue will face existential financial pressure. The timeline for next-generation mRNA products will be extended.</p>
<p><strong>It sends a political signal.</strong> The defunding communicates that the current administration views the mRNA platform with skepticism rather than enthusiasm. This signal affects the behavior of regulators, researchers, and investors throughout the biomedical ecosystem.</p>
<p><strong>It creates space for accountability.</strong> As long as the mRNA platform was expanding and generating revenue, there was no institutional incentive to examine its safety record. The defunding creates a pause — and in that pause, questions can be asked, data can be analyzed, and injured individuals can be heard.</p>
<p>However, the defunding does not:</p>
<p><strong>Affect existing products.</strong> COVID-19 mRNA injections already on the market (under full FDA approval) are not recalled or withdrawn by this action. Pfizer and Moderna retain their existing approvals and can continue manufacturing and distributing their current products.</p>
<p><strong>Address liability.</strong> The PREP Act liability shield remains in force. Manufacturers remain immune from lawsuits related to COVID-19 countermeasures administered during the declared emergency.</p>
<p><strong>Eliminate private funding.</strong> Pharmaceutical companies, venture capital firms, and international organizations (including CEPI, the Gates Foundation, and the Wellcome Trust) continue to fund mRNA research independently of federal appropriations. The technology will continue to be developed — the question is whether it will be developed with or without federal oversight and safety requirements.</p>
<p><strong>Reverse the damage.</strong> The hundreds of millions of doses already administered cannot be recalled from the bodies of those who received them. Whatever effects the mRNA injections produce — in the near term or over decades — will manifest regardless of future funding decisions.</p>
