15:T2309,<h1 id="most-favored-nation-pricing-the-cornerstone-of-trumprx-explained">Most Favored Nation Pricing: The Cornerstone of TrumpRx Explained</h1>
<h2 id="the-ancient-pattern">The Ancient Pattern</h2>
<blockquote>
<p>"The rich ruleth over the poor, and the borrower is servant to the lender." — Proverbs 22:7</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Most Favored Nation (MFN) pricing — the policy that the United States should pay no more for pharmaceutical products than the lowest price negotiated by any other developed nation — sounds like simple fairness. Americans have subsidized global pharmaceutical profits for decades, paying dramatically more than citizens of other nations for identical products. MFN pricing corrects this inequity. But in the pharmaceutical arena, simple fairness always conceals complex consequences.</p>
<p>The Jesuits operated the most sophisticated financial network in the pre-modern world, understanding that pricing mechanisms are power mechanisms. Napoleon's "military organization" pursuing "absolute power" used economic relationships to create dependencies that served political objectives. Today, pharmaceutical pricing is a power mechanism: it determines who can access medicine, which countries subsidize research, and how deeply pharmaceutical companies penetrate healthcare systems. MFN pricing restructures these power relationships — but whether the restructuring serves patients or the pharmaceutical agenda depends on the details that the headlines never explain.</p>
<p>Howard Pittman's testimony about the second heaven — where demonic principalities coordinate global strategies — is relevant to international pricing negotiations because these negotiations determine how pharmaceutical power is distributed across nations. The principalities of Ephesians 6:12 operate through economic structures as readily as through political ones. Listen to <a href="https://confliction.zzzdaily.com/tracks/en/67-The_Enemy_Within.mp4">The Enemy Within</a> — the enemy operates within the pricing structures that determine who lives and who dies.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="how-mfn-pricing-works">How MFN Pricing Works</h2>
<h3 id="the-basic-mechanism">The Basic Mechanism</h3>
<p>Most Favored Nation pricing ties American pharmaceutical prices to the lowest prices negotiated by comparable nations. If Germany negotiates a price of $100 for a drug that costs Americans $400, MFN pricing would reduce the American price to $100 (or close to it). The principle is that Americans should receive the most favorable pricing available to any comparable nation.</p>
<p>This mechanism addresses a genuine injustice. The United States has functioned as the pharmaceutical industry's profit center, with Americans paying prices that subsidize lower prices in other countries. MFN pricing ends this subsidy, potentially saving American consumers and the government hundreds of billions of dollars over time.</p>
<h3 id="the-implementation-challenges">The Implementation Challenges</h3>
<p>Implementing MFN pricing is more complex than the concept suggests. Pharmaceutical companies can respond to MFN requirements by raising prices in other countries (eliminating the reference point), creating different product formulations for different markets (undermining price comparisons), shifting revenue to services, licensing fees, or other non-product channels, and lobbying against implementation through political channels.</p>
<p>The pharmaceutical industry's $230+ billion in investment generates an enormous lobbying capacity. Every member of Congress, every regulator, and every policy advisor is subject to pharmaceutical industry influence. MFN pricing will be implemented only to the extent that the pharmaceutical industry permits — which means it will be implemented in a form that serves the industry's interests while appearing to serve the public's interests.</p>
<h2 id="the-hidden-dynamics">The Hidden Dynamics</h2>
<h3 id="price-reduction-market-expansion">Price Reduction, Market Expansion</h3>
<p>The pharmaceutical industry's standard response to price constraints is market expansion. If prices per unit decrease, the industry compensates by increasing the number of units sold — expanding indications, broadening recommendations, and mandating products for populations that previously did not receive them.</p>
<p>MFN pricing could paradoxically increase total pharmaceutical spending even as it decreases per-unit prices. If lower prices lead to broader insurance coverage, expanded vaccination schedules, and new mandated pharmaceutical interventions, the total volume of pharmaceutical products consumed could increase sufficiently to offset — or exceed — the revenue lost through price reduction.</p>
<p>This is the pharmaceutical industry's preferred outcome: lower prices (political victory), higher volume (revenue maintenance), and deeper system integration (strategic advance). The consumer celebrates lower prices while consuming more pharmaceutical products, creating deeper dependency and greater exposure.</p>
<p>Karen Kingston has documented how pharmaceutical pricing strategies consistently serve long-term industry objectives rather than patient interests. MFN pricing, like previous pricing reforms, will be shaped by industry influence to produce outcomes that appear to benefit patients while advancing the pharmaceutical agenda.</p>
<h2 id="the-spiritual-dimension-of-healthcare-economics">The Spiritual Dimension of Healthcare Economics</h2>
<h3 id="when-money-determines-access-to-healing">When Money Determines Access to Healing</h3>
<p>The biblical model of healing is free — Jesus healed without charge, and He instructed His disciples to "freely ye have received, freely give" (Matthew 10:8). The pharmaceutical model of healing is commercial — every treatment has a price, every dose has a cost, and access to healing is determined by the ability to pay (or the willingness of insurance or government to pay on one's behalf).</p>
<p>MFN pricing operates within the commercial model, seeking to reduce the price of pharmaceutical healing without questioning whether pharmaceutical healing should be the primary model. The spiritual question is not "how much should drugs cost?" but "why have we replaced divine healing with pharmaceutical dependency?"</p>
<p>Pittman warned that "You call me Lord but you never made me Lord." A healthcare system built entirely on pharmaceutical products rather than on faith, prayer, nutrition, and divine provision has made Pfizer its lord, not God. MFN pricing negotiates the terms of that lordship without questioning the lordship itself.</p>
