<h2 id="the-official-story">The Official Story</h2>
<h3 id="what-you-were-taught">What You Were Taught</h3>
<p>The standard Christian narrative of the crucifixion runs as follows: Jesus of Nazareth was arrested in the Garden of Gethsemane, tried before the Sanhedrin and Pontius Pilate, sentenced to death by crucifixion, nailed to a cross at Golgotha, died after approximately six hours, and was buried in a tomb provided by Joseph of Arimathea. Three days later, the tomb was found empty, and Jesus appeared resurrected to his disciples.</p>
<p>This narrative is taken primarily from the four canonical Gospels — Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John. These four accounts agree on the broad outlines but differ significantly on the details. These differences are important, and the institution has spent centuries explaining them away rather than honestly examining them.</p>
<h3 id="the-differences-that-matter">The Differences That Matter</h3>
<p><strong>Who was at the tomb?</strong> Matthew says Mary Magdalene and "the other Mary." Mark says Mary Magdalene, Mary the mother of James, and Salome. Luke says the women who had come from Galilee. John says only Mary Magdalene.</p>
<p><strong>What did they find?</strong> Matthew says an angel sitting on the rolled-away stone. Mark says a young man in white sitting inside. Luke says two men in shining garments. John says the stone removed and the tomb empty, with two angels appearing later.</p>
<p><strong>What happened next?</strong> Matthew says Jesus met the women on their way back. Mark says they told no one because they were afraid (the original ending of Mark stops at 16:8 — the rest was added later). Luke says the disciples did not believe the women. John has an extended narrative involving Mary Magdalene alone.</p>
<p>These are not minor discrepancies. They describe fundamentally different events. If four witnesses to a modern crime gave accounts this divergent, no court would accept any of them as reliable. The institution explains these differences as "complementary perspectives." But this explanation requires special pleading — an assumption that the texts are true, applied before the evidence is examined.</p>
<h2 id="the-problems-with-the-narrative">The Problems with the Narrative</h2>
<h3 id="the-missing-contemporary-sources">The Missing Contemporary Sources</h3>
<p>The crucifixion of Jesus, if it happened as described, would have been accompanied by extraordinary events: darkness covering the land for three hours, an earthquake, the veil of the temple torn in two, and according to Matthew 27:52-53, the graves of saints opening and dead people walking into Jerusalem.</p>
<p>These events would have been noticed. Darkness over the land for three hours would have been recorded by Roman historians, astronomers, and administrators. An earthquake would appear in geological and administrative records. Dead people walking through Jerusalem would have been the most extraordinary event in human history.</p>
<p>Yet no contemporary non-Christian source records any of these events. Josephus, the Jewish historian who documented the period in exhaustive detail, makes no mention of three hours of darkness, an earthquake, or a zombie invasion of Jerusalem. Roman records are silent. Not a single non-Christian source from the first century confirms the extraordinary events that Matthew describes accompanying the crucifixion.</p>
<p>Agent131711's research on Substack (agent131711.substack.com) examines the silence of contemporary sources in detail, noting that the argument from silence is not proof of absence but is certainly grounds for inquiry. When an event of this magnitude leaves no trace in the historical record outside the texts produced by the movement that benefits from the story, honest examination demands that we ask why.</p>
<h3 id="the-josephus-problem">The Josephus Problem</h3>
<p>The most commonly cited non-Christian reference to Jesus is the <em>Testimonium Flavianum</em> in Josephus's <em>Antiquities of the Jews</em> (Book 18, Chapter 3). This passage describes Jesus as "a wise man, if it be lawful to call him a man," says he was "the Christ," and mentions his crucifixion and resurrection.</p>
<p>However, the scholarly consensus — not fringe opinion, but mainstream academic scholarship — is that this passage was either entirely fabricated or substantially altered by later Christian scribes. The reasons are compelling:</p>
<ol>
<li>The passage interrupts the surrounding text, which discusses completely unrelated events.</li>
<li>No Christian writer before Eusebius (4th century) quotes or references this passage, despite its obvious apologetic value.</li>
<li>The language is inconsistent with Josephus's style and beliefs — as a Jewish historian, he would not have called Jesus "the Christ" or declared the resurrection.</li>
<li>Eusebius, who first cited the passage, was known for his willingness to use deception in the service of faith (he wrote: "it is not possible to be saved without telling lies").</li>
</ol>
<p>If the primary non-Christian historical reference to Jesus was fabricated or altered, this does not prove the crucifixion did not happen. But it demonstrates that the institution was willing to manufacture evidence — willing to lie, forge, and deceive in service of the narrative. This is precisely the behavior that <em>Catholic Confidential</em> describes: infiltration "cloaked with cunning," practiced by men who engage in "every species of vice."</p>
<h3 id="the-roman-execution-problem">The Roman Execution Problem</h3>
<p>Roman crucifixion was not a private affair. It was a public spectacle designed to terrorize the population into obedience. Crucifixion victims were left on their crosses for days, sometimes weeks, their bodies consumed by birds and dogs. They were not buried in private tombs. They were dumped in mass graves or left to rot as a public warning.</p>
<p>The Gospel narrative of Jesus receiving a dignified burial in a rich man's private tomb contradicts everything known about Roman crucifixion practice. Pontius Pilate, described by the contemporary philosopher Philo as "inflexible, merciless, and obstinate," would not have released the body of an executed criminal to a private individual. This simply did not happen in the Roman system.</p>
<p>The institution explains this anomaly by appealing to divine providence — God arranged events to fulfill prophecy. But this explanation is circular: the text must be true because God ensured it, and we know God ensured it because the text says so.</p>
<h2 id="the-jesuit-role">The Jesuit Role</h2>
<h3 id="controlling-the-narrative">Controlling the Narrative</h3>
<p>The Jesuits did not create the crucifixion narrative — it predates them by fifteen centuries. But they have served as its most aggressive defenders and its most sophisticated propagators. The Jesuit educational system, which trains theologians, historians, and apologists, produces generation after generation of scholars whose purpose is to defend the narrative, not examine it.</p>
<p>The Jesuit approach to the crucifixion is the same as their approach to everything: present the desired conclusion as established fact, suppress alternative views, and punish questioners. The Index of Forbidden Books, which the Jesuits helped administer, banned any text that challenged the orthodox narrative. The Inquisition, which Jesuits administered with enthusiasm, tortured and killed those who publicly questioned church doctrine.</p>
<p>These methods did not prove the crucifixion happened. They proved that the institution was willing to use violence to prevent examination of the question. Organizations that possess truth welcome examination. Organizations that possess lies fear it.</p>
<h3 id="the-passion-play-tradition">The Passion Play Tradition</h3>
<p>The Jesuits popularized the Passion Play — dramatic reenactments of the crucifixion performed during Holy Week. These performances served a crucial function: they made the narrative vivid, emotional, and real in the minds of audiences who could not read and had no access to alternative accounts.</p>
<p>The Passion Play tradition transformed a historical claim into an emotional experience. Once people had wept over the suffering of Jesus, seen the nails driven in, witnessed the blood flow — even in staged form — the narrative became psychologically unquestionable. To doubt it felt like cruelty, like denying the suffering of someone you had witnessed being tortured.</p>
<p>This is masterful psychological manipulation. The Jesuits understood that emotional investment prevents rational examination. By making the crucifixion a lived emotional experience rather than a historical claim to be evaluated, they made it effectively un-questionable.</p>
<h2 id="the-deeper-questions">The Deeper Questions</h2>
<h3 id="what-if-it-happened-differently">What If It Happened Differently?</h3>
<p>Questioning the crucifixion narrative does not require claiming it never happened at all. It may have happened — but differently from how the institution describes it. Early Christian groups held diverse views:</p>
<p>The Docetists believed Jesus was a spiritual being who only appeared to have a physical body. His suffering and death were illusions.</p>
<p>The Gnostics held various views, some believing that Simon of Cyrene (who carried the cross) was actually crucified in Jesus' place, while Jesus watched from a distance, laughing.</p>
<p>The Islamic tradition (Quran 4:157) states: "They did not kill him, nor did they crucify him, but it was made to appear so to them."</p>
<p>These alternative views were suppressed by the institution through violence — not through evidence. The councils that established orthodox doctrine did so by majority vote, political pressure, and imperial enforcement, not by demonstrating that their version was historically accurate.</p>
<h3 id="what-if-it-is-true">What If It Is True?</h3>
<p>The possibility that the crucifixion happened exactly as described must also be considered. The spiritual realm is real — Howard Pittman testified to this directly. If the spiritual realm is real, then supernatural events are possible, including the events described in the Gospels.</p>
<p>But even if the crucifixion happened as described, the institution's interpretation of it — the theology of substitutionary atonement, the necessity of the Catholic Mass, the authority of the Pope — does not automatically follow. The event and the institution's interpretation of the event are separate things. The Jesuits have conflated them deliberately, so that questioning the institution feels like questioning Christ himself.</p>
<p>This conflation is the institution's greatest weapon. "Touch not mine anointed" (1 Chronicles 16:22) is used to silence anyone who questions clergy. But the verse refers to God's anointed — not to the Jesuit-trained administrators of a human institution. The distinction matters, and the institution depends on you not making it.</p>
<h2 id="the-path-of-honest-inquiry">The Path of Honest Inquiry</h2>
<h3 id="what-honest-examination-requires">What Honest Examination Requires</h3>
<p>Examining the crucifixion honestly requires separating what you know from what you were told. What you know is limited to the texts themselves and the archaeological evidence. What you were told includes centuries of institutional interpretation, emotional conditioning, and social pressure.</p>
<p>Honest examination requires reading the Gospel accounts side by side and acknowledging the contradictions rather than harmonizing them. It requires considering the absence of contemporary corroboration. It requires acknowledging that the institution that transmitted the narrative had motives beyond truth-telling.</p>
<p>Most importantly, it requires courage. The courage to face the possibility that you have been wrong. The courage to accept uncertainty. The courage to follow the evidence wherever it leads, even if it leads away from comfort and community.</p>
<p>Howard Pittman's 97.5 percent were people who lacked this courage. They accepted the institution's narrative without examination. They made the institution Lord rather than making Christ Lord. And they were rejected — not for questioning, but for failing to question.</p>
