15:T67d5,<h1 id="gmo-the-genetic-modification-of-your-food-supply">GMO: The Genetic Modification of Your Food Supply</h1>
<h2 id="the-ancient-pattern">The Ancient Pattern</h2>
<blockquote>
<p>"Then God said, 'Let the earth bring forth grass, the herb that yields seed, and the fruit tree that yields fruit according to its kind, whose seed is in itself, on the earth'; and it was so." — Genesis 1:11</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The phrase "according to its kind" appears ten times in Genesis 1. It is not a casual detail — it is a foundational principle of creation. God designed each living thing to reproduce according to its own kind, with its seed in itself. The seed was sovereign. The seed was free. The seed belonged to the plant, to the farmer, and ultimately to God.</p>
<p>What the genetic modification industry has done is seize the seed, rewrite its code, patent its biology, and transform the most fundamental element of the food supply into corporate intellectual property. This is not agriculture. This is biological conquest.</p>
<p>The Confliction album track <a href="https://confliction.zzzdaily.com/tracks/en/47-The_Transgression.mp4">The Transgression</a> speaks to the crossing of boundaries God established at creation — boundaries between kinds, between the natural and the engineered, between what belongs to the Creator and what man has no right to claim.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="what-is-genetic-modification">What Is Genetic Modification?</h2>
<p>Genetic modification is the process of artificially inserting genetic material from one organism into another to produce traits that do not occur naturally. Unlike traditional breeding, which works within the reproductive boundaries of a species, genetic modification crosses those boundaries entirely.</p>
<p>Genes from bacteria are inserted into corn. Genes from viruses are inserted into soybeans. Genes from fish have been inserted into tomatoes. The technology does not respect the boundaries of "kind" — it erases them.</p>
<p>The first commercially grown GMO crop was the Flavr Savr tomato, approved by the FDA in 1994. It was a commercial failure. But the technology that followed — particularly Monsanto's Roundup Ready and Bt crop platforms — transformed global agriculture within a single generation.</p>
<p>By 2023, over 190 million hectares of GMO crops were planted worldwide. In the United States, over 90% of soybeans, corn, cotton, canola, and sugar beets are genetically modified.</p>
<p>The two dominant GMO technologies are herbicide tolerance and insect resistance. Roundup Ready crops are engineered to survive being sprayed with Monsanto's glyphosate herbicide (Roundup), allowing farmers to spray entire fields with herbicide without killing the crop.</p>
<p>Bt crops are engineered to produce their own insecticide — a toxin derived from the bacterium Bacillus thuringiensis — in every cell of the plant, including the parts humans and animals eat.</p>
<p>Both technologies were marketed as improvements: fewer weeds, fewer insects, higher yields, less chemical use. Every one of these claims has proven to be misleading or false over the long term.</p>
<p>Weed resistance has exploded. Insect resistance is emerging. Yield increases are marginal compared to conventional breeding. And total chemical use has increased, not decreased, as farmers spray more herbicide to combat resistant weeds.</p>
<p>A 2016 report by the National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine — hardly a radical anti-GMO institution — found no evidence that GMO crops had increased the rate of yield improvement beyond what conventional breeding had already achieved. The report acknowledged that herbicide-resistant weeds were proliferating and that the environmental benefits of GMO crops were, at best, mixed.</p>
<p>The promises that sold GMOs to the world — higher yields, less chemicals, better nutrition — have not materialized. What has materialized is corporate control of the seed supply, chemical saturation of the food system, and the systematic destruction of agricultural biodiversity.</p>
<h2 id="monsanto-the-corporation-that-owned-the-food">Monsanto: The Corporation That Owned the Food</h2>
<p>To understand GMOs, you must understand Monsanto. Founded in 1901 in St. Louis, Missouri, Monsanto's history reads like a catalog of chemical warfare against life itself.</p>
<p>Before entering the agricultural sector, Monsanto was a leading manufacturer of polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs), which were banned in 1979 as persistent environmental toxins. Monsanto produced Agent Orange, the defoliant used in the Vietnam War, which caused cancer, birth defects, and environmental devastation that persists to this day.</p>
<p>Monsanto produced bovine growth hormone (rBGH), which was injected into dairy cows to increase milk production despite evidence of health effects in both cows and humans.</p>
<p>When Monsanto entered the seed business in the 1990s, it did so with the same aggressive, monopolistic strategy that characterized its chemical operations. The company purchased seed companies across the globe, acquiring the genetics of crop varieties that farmers had developed over centuries.</p>
<p>It then patented genetically modified versions of these crops and used its intellectual property rights to control how farmers could use their own seeds.</p>
<p>Under Monsanto's patent regime, farmers who planted Roundup Ready seeds were required to sign technology agreements prohibiting them from saving seeds for replanting — a practice that had been the foundation of agriculture since the beginning of civilization.</p>
<p>Farmers who violated these agreements — or who were found to have Monsanto's patented genetics in their fields due to cross-pollination — were sued. Between 1997 and 2010, Monsanto filed 147 lawsuits against American farmers and settled 700 additional cases out of court.</p>
<p>Monsanto maintained an enforcement division that included private investigators who surveilled farmers suspected of seed saving. Farmers reported being visited by Monsanto agents who demanded access to their fields and records. The atmosphere in rural farming communities was described by the Center for Food Safety as one of "intimidation and fear."</p>
<p>The case of Percy Schmeiser, a Canadian canola farmer, became a global symbol of corporate overreach. Schmeiser's fields were contaminated with Roundup Ready canola through wind-blown pollen from neighboring farms. Monsanto sued him for patent infringement.</p>
<p>The Supreme Court of Canada ruled in Monsanto's favor, establishing the legal precedent that a corporation can own the genetics of a living organism even when that organism contaminates a farmer's field without his consent.</p>
<p>The Schmeiser case established a legal principle with terrifying implications: genetic contamination flows in one direction only. When Monsanto's pollen drifts onto a farmer's field, the farmer is guilty of patent infringement. But when the farmer's organic crop is contaminated by GMO pollen — destroying its organic certification and its market value — Monsanto bears no liability.</p>
<p>The asymmetry is absolute. The corporation's contamination creates corporate rights. The farmer's contamination creates corporate liability. The law protects the patent holder, not the farmer. The law protects the polluter, not the polluted.</p>
<p>In the United States, the Organic Seed Growers and Trade Association filed a preemptive lawsuit in 2011 seeking a court declaration that farmers who were inadvertently contaminated by Monsanto's GMO traits could not be sued for patent infringement. The case was dismissed, and the dismissal was upheld on appeal. The court acknowledged Monsanto's "binding assurance" not to sue farmers with trace contamination — but refused to provide any legal protection beyond the corporation's voluntary promise.</p>
<h2 id="glyphosate-the-chemical-partner">Glyphosate: The Chemical Partner</h2>
<p>GMO crops do not exist in isolation. They exist as part of a chemical system. Roundup Ready crops are designed to be drenched in glyphosate — and they are. Since the introduction of Roundup Ready crops in 1996, global glyphosate use has increased approximately 1,500%.</p>
<p>In the United States alone, over 300 million pounds of glyphosate are applied annually.</p>
<p>Glyphosate is not merely an herbicide. It was originally patented as a mineral chelator (binding essential minerals and making them unavailable to plants), and later patented as an antibiotic.</p>
<p>When applied to soil, it kills beneficial microorganisms, chelates essential minerals, and disrupts the biological processes that make soil fertile. When consumed by humans, it disrupts the gut microbiome, chelates minerals in the digestive tract, and has been classified by the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) as "probably carcinogenic to humans."</p>
<p>The saturation of the food supply with glyphosate is now total. Testing has found glyphosate in bread, cereal, oatmeal, honey, beer, wine, infant formula, breast milk, and human urine.</p>
<p>A 2022 study published in Environmental Health found detectable levels of glyphosate in over 80% of urine samples from Americans tested. You do not choose to consume glyphosate. It is in the food supply, in the water, and in your body whether you consent or not.</p>
<p>The use of glyphosate as a pre-harvest desiccant — sprayed directly on wheat, oats, barley, and other crops days before harvest to accelerate drying — means that the highest glyphosate residues are found in the grain-based foods that the USDA's dietary guidelines tell Americans to eat 6-11 servings of per day.</p>
<p>The system is internally consistent: recommend the foods that contain the most poison.</p>
<h2 id="the-safety-lie">The Safety Lie</h2>
<p>The FDA approved GMO foods in 1992 under a policy of "substantial equivalence" — the assertion that genetically modified foods are substantially equivalent to their conventional counterparts and therefore require no special safety testing, labeling, or regulation.</p>
<p>This policy was drafted under the direction of Michael Taylor, who served simultaneously as FDA Deputy Commissioner and Monsanto's Vice President for Public Policy. The conflict of interest was not hidden — it was celebrated as "public-private partnership."</p>
<p>The substantial equivalence doctrine is not a scientific finding. It is a political decision that exempts GMO foods from the safety testing required of food additives, drugs, and every other novel substance introduced into the human body.</p>
<p>No long-term human feeding studies were required before GMO crops were approved. No independent safety assessments were conducted. The safety data relied upon by the FDA came almost entirely from the companies seeking approval — primarily Monsanto.</p>
<p>Internal FDA documents, released through a lawsuit filed by the Alliance for Bio-Integrity, revealed that the agency's own scientists had warned that GMO foods could produce unexpected toxins, allergens, and nutritional changes. These warnings were overruled by political appointees, and the substantial equivalence policy was adopted without the scientific consensus the FDA publicly claimed.</p>
<p>When independent researchers attempted to conduct safety studies, they were met with systematic obstruction.</p>
<p>Dr. Arpad Pusztai, a researcher at the Rowett Institute in Scotland, conducted feeding studies showing that rats fed genetically modified potatoes developed pre-cancerous cell growth, smaller brains, and damaged immune systems. When he reported his findings on British television in 1998, he was fired from his position within 48 hours, his research team was disbanded, and his data was seized.</p>
<p>Professor Gilles-Eric Seralini's two-year feeding study on rats, published in 2012, found increased tumor rates and organ damage in rats fed Roundup Ready corn. The study was attacked by a coordinated campaign that included a former Monsanto employee serving as editor of the journal that retracted the paper.</p>
<p>The retraction was later reversed when the study was republished after independent review — but the damage to Seralini's reputation, and the chilling effect on other researchers, had already been achieved.</p>
<h2 id="the-labeling-battle">The Labeling Battle</h2>
<p>For two decades, the biotechnology industry fought to prevent mandatory labeling of GMO foods. The industry spent over $700 million between 2012 and 2016 alone to defeat state-level GMO labeling initiatives in California, Washington, Oregon, and Colorado.</p>
<p>The argument was revealing: if GMO foods are safe and substantially equivalent, why spend $700 million to prevent consumers from knowing they contain them?</p>
<p>In 2016, Congress passed the National Bioengineered Food Disclosure Standard, a federal labeling law that preempted stronger state labeling requirements. The law was widely criticized for using the obscure term "bioengineered" instead of "GMO," for allowing companies to disclose GMO content through QR codes rather than on-package labels (disadvantaging consumers without smartphones), and for exempting highly processed ingredients (like high-fructose corn syrup derived from GMO corn) from disclosure.</p>
<p>The labeling law was designed to appear responsive to consumer demand while providing the minimum possible transparency. It was a victory for the biotech industry, not for consumers.</p>
<p>Over 60 countries worldwide have implemented mandatory GMO labeling requirements. The United States — the world's largest producer of GMO crops — has the weakest labeling regime among developed nations. The correlation between industry lobbying power and regulatory leniency is not coincidental.</p>
<p>Consumers who wish to avoid GMOs must navigate a labeling system designed to obscure rather than inform. The "Non-GMO Project Verified" label — a private certification — has become the primary means by which American consumers identify non-GMO products. That a private organization must fill the gap left by government failure tells you everything you need to know about whose interests the government serves.</p>
<h2 id="seed-patents-and-food-sovereignty">Seed Patents and Food Sovereignty</h2>
<p>The patenting of seeds is perhaps the most consequential and least understood dimension of the GMO revolution. When Monsanto patents a genetically modified seed, it does not merely own a product. It owns a life form — and every reproduction of that life form.</p>
<p>The seed cannot reproduce freely, as God designed it to do. It reproduces under license, with royalty payments, subject to corporate terms and conditions.</p>
<p>This is not a minor legal technicality. It is the privatization of the biological commons — the transformation of the seed, which has been the shared heritage of humanity since the dawn of agriculture, into corporate intellectual property.</p>
<p>When a corporation owns the seed, it owns the food. When it owns the food, it owns the people who depend on that food for survival.</p>
<p>The implications for global food sovereignty are devastating. In India, the introduction of Monsanto's Bt cotton seeds in 2002 created a cycle of debt and dependency that contributed to a wave of farmer suicides.</p>
<p>Farmers who purchased the expensive patented seeds and the chemical inputs they required found themselves trapped in debt when crops failed or prices dropped. Between 1995 and 2018, over 300,000 Indian farmers committed suicide, a crisis widely linked to the economic pressures of industrial agriculture and seed dependency.</p>
<p>As our article on <a href="/seed-vault-control-food-future/?lang=en">The Seed Vault: Who Controls the Future of Food?</a> documents, the corporations and billionaires promoting GMOs are simultaneously collecting and storing the world's natural seed genetics in facilities like the Svalbard Global Seed Vault — preserving the natural originals while replacing them with patented modifications in the world's fields.</p>
<h2 id="the-environmental-fallout">The Environmental Fallout</h2>
<p>The environmental consequences of GMO agriculture extend far beyond the fields where modified crops are planted. Cross-pollination spreads GMO genetics to wild plant populations and organic farms.</p>
<p>Herbicide-resistant weeds — "superweeds" — have evolved in response to glyphosate saturation, with over 50 weed species now documented as glyphosate-resistant worldwide. These superweeds require ever more toxic herbicides to control, including 2,4-D (a component of Agent Orange) and dicamba, which drifts from treated fields and damages neighboring crops.</p>
<p>The dicamba drift crisis has caused millions of dollars in damage to non-GMO farms across the Midwest and South. Farmers who did not plant dicamba-tolerant seeds found their crops destroyed by herbicide drifting from neighboring fields — a bitter irony in which the GMO system punishes those who refuse to participate in it.</p>
<p>The impact on pollinators has been devastating, as documented in our article on <a href="/bee-colony-collapse-food-chain/?lang=en">Bee Colony Collapse</a>. Bt toxin produced by GMO crops kills beneficial insects along with target pests. Glyphosate destroys the wildflower habitats that pollinators depend on.</p>
<p>Soil health — the foundation of all agriculture — is being systematically degraded by the GMO chemical system. Glyphosate kills soil fungi, bacteria, and earthworms. Synthetic fertilizers, applied to compensate for the biological sterility created by chemical agriculture, further degrade soil structure and water-holding capacity.</p>
<p>As our article on <a href="/organic-farming-under-attack/?lang=en">Organic Farming Under Attack</a> documents, the alternative to this destruction — organic, regenerative agriculture — is under systematic regulatory assault by the same interests that promote GMOs.</p>
<h2 id="gene-editing-the-next-generation-of-control">Gene Editing: The Next Generation of Control</h2>
<p>The GMO industry has rebranded. Recognizing the public resistance to "genetically modified organisms," the biotechnology sector has shifted its focus to gene editing technologies — particularly CRISPR-Cas9 — which it markets as fundamentally different from traditional GMOs.</p>
<p>The argument is that gene editing modifies existing genes rather than inserting foreign DNA. The crop is still the "same species," the industry claims, and therefore should not be regulated as a GMO.</p>
<p>This distinction is largely semantic. Gene editing still involves deliberate human manipulation of genetic code at a level of precision that has no precedent in nature. Off-target mutations — unintended changes to the genome — have been documented in gene-edited organisms. The long-term ecological consequences of releasing gene-edited organisms into the environment are as unknown as they were for first-generation GMOs.</p>
<p>The regulatory implications are profound. In 2018, the USDA declared that it would not regulate gene-edited crops that could have been developed through conventional breeding — effectively exempting the majority of gene-edited organisms from any oversight whatsoever.</p>
<p>The European Court of Justice took the opposite position in 2018, ruling that gene-edited organisms should be regulated under the EU's existing GMO framework. But the decision has been contested by industry, and the European Commission has proposed new regulations that would significantly loosen restrictions on gene-edited crops.</p>
<p>The pattern is familiar: a novel technology with unknown risks is introduced with minimal regulation, marketed as an improvement over its predecessor, and presented to the public as safe based on industry-funded assessments.</p>
<p>The difference is that gene-edited organisms may be even harder to detect than traditional GMOs, because they do not necessarily contain foreign DNA. This means that labeling, testing, and regulation become practically impossible. The consumer's right to know what is in their food disappears — not because the information is withheld, but because the modification itself is invisible to existing detection methods.</p>
<p>Gene editing represents the next generation of biological control — more precise, more powerful, and more difficult to resist than the GMO technology it is designed to replace.</p>
<h2 id="the-revolving-door">The Revolving Door</h2>
<p>The regulatory capture that enabled GMO approval is not a historical artifact. It is an ongoing structural feature of the relationship between the biotechnology industry and the agencies that regulate it.</p>
<p>Michael Taylor moved from the FDA to Monsanto and back to the FDA. Margaret Miller, a former Monsanto researcher, became Deputy Director of the FDA's Office of New Animal Drugs. Linda Fisher moved from the EPA to Monsanto's vice presidency and back to the EPA.</p>
<p>Islam Siddiqui, a former lobbyist for CropLife America (the pesticide and biotechnology industry trade group), was appointed as the USDA's Chief Agricultural Negotiator, where he promoted GMO adoption in international trade agreements.</p>
<p>The revolving door ensures that the people making regulatory decisions about GMO safety are the same people who profited from GMO sales — or who will profit from them after leaving government service.</p>
<p>This is not conspiracy theory. It is documented career history, available in public records, and it explains why the regulatory framework for GMOs in the United States has remained fundamentally unchanged since 1992 — despite thirty years of accumulating evidence that the original safety assumptions were inadequate.</p>

The seed that God made — perfect in its kind, carrying life within itself, free to reproduce and multiply — has been rewritten, patented, and placed under corporate control.</p>

<p>The seed was given freely. The seed belongs to all. And those who have stolen it, modified it, patented it, and sold it back to the people it was given to will discover that you cannot improve upon the work of God without inheriting the consequences of the attempt.</p>
