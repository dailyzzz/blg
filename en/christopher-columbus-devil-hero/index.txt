<p>Columbus's own journals and the accounts of his contemporaries paint a picture of monstrous cruelty that makes his continued celebration as a hero an act of collective insanity — or collective mind control.</p>
<p>Upon arriving in the Caribbean, Columbus immediately noted the Taino people's gentleness, generosity, and lack of weapons. In his journal, he wrote: "They would make fine servants... <p>Within two years of Columbus's arrival, the encomienda system was established — a system of forced labor that was slavery in all but name. Taino men were worked to death in gold mines. <h3 id="the-genocide-in-numbers">The Genocide in Numbers</h3>
<p>When Columbus arrived in 1492, the Taino population of Hispaniola was estimated at between 250,000 and several million (estimates vary widely). By 1514, the Spanish census recorded only 32,000 Taino. By 1542, barely 200 remained. This is not colonization; it is extermination — carried out under the banner of Christ and the authority of the Pope.</p>
<p>Columbus personally oversaw a tribute system that required every Taino over the age of 14 to deliver a hawk's bell full of gold dust every three months. The entire operation was a machine of extraction and death, sanctioned by papal bulls that granted Spain dominion over any lands not already held by a Christian ruler.</p>
<p>The Papal Bull <em>Inter Caetera</em>, issued by Pope Alexander VI in 1493, granted Spain all lands west of a line drawn through the Atlantic.
