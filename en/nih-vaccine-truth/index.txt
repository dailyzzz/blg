<p>SV40 is a known oncogenic virus — it causes cancer in laboratory animals. Research has found SV40 DNA in human cancers, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Brain tumors (mesotheliomas)</li>
<li>Bone cancers (osteosarcomas)</li>
<li>Lymphomas</li>
<li>Lung cancers</li>
</ul>
<p>The NIH's response to this contamination was not to alert the public, not to recall the contaminated vaccines, and not to investigate the potential cancer link. Instead, they:</p>
<ul>
<li>Quietly switched to SV40-free vaccine production</li>
<li>Suppressed research linking SV40 to human cancers</li>
<li>Funded studies designed to find no connection between SV40 and cancer</li>
<li>Allowed the contaminated vaccine to be used until existing stocks were exhausted</li>
</ul>
<p>Ninety-eight million Americans were injected with a cancer-causing virus, and the NIH covered it up. This is not speculation — it is documented in congressional testimony, internal NIH communications, and peer-reviewed research that the mainstream medical establishment has spent decades trying to discredit.</p>
<h3 id="the-simpsonwood-conference">The Simpsonwood Conference</h3>
<p>In June 2000, the CDC convened a secret meeting at the Simpsonwood Conference Center in Norcross, Georgia. The meeting brought together 52 attendees from the CDC, FDA, NIH, vaccine manufacturers, and the WHO to discuss a study by CDC epidemiologist Thomas Verstraeten.</p>
<p>Verstraeten's study, using the Vaccine Safety Datalink (a database of millions of children's medical records), had found a statistically significant association between thimerosal-containing vaccines and neurological disorders, including autism.</p>
<p>The Simpsonwood transcripts — obtained through the Freedom of Information Act — reveal that the attendees were more concerned with protecting the vaccine program than with protecting children:</p>
<ul>
<li>Participants discussed how to <strong>manipulate the data</strong> to eliminate the association</li>
<li>They discussed the <strong>legal implications</strong> of the findings</li>
<li>They discussed how to <strong>prevent the data from being accessed</strong> by outside researchers</li>
<li>One participant stated he would not want to give thimerosal-containing vaccines to his own grandchild</li>
</ul>
<p>After the meeting, Verstraeten's study was reworked — using different statistical methods and different data sets — until the association between thimerosal and autism disappeared. The final published version showed no association. The original data showing the association was not made public.</p>

<ul>
<li>Met in a conference room</li>

<li>Threw them in a <strong>garbage can</strong></li>
