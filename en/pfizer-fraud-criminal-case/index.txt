15:T526e,<h1 id="the-pfizer-criminal-case-a-history-of-fraud-and-deception">The Pfizer Criminal Case: A History of Fraud and Deception</h1>
<h2 id="the-ancient-pattern">The Ancient Pattern</h2>
<blockquote>
<p>"Bread of deceit is sweet to a man; but afterwards his mouth shall be filled with gravel." — Proverbs 20:17</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The bread of deceit always tastes sweet at first. It promises health, safety, and peace of mind. It comes wrapped in the language of science, endorsed by authorities, and marketed with the full force of a multi-billion-dollar advertising apparatus. But afterward — after the pills are swallowed, the injections administered, and the profits banked — the mouth is filled with gravel. With adverse events. With injuries that were known but concealed. With data that was manipulated. With trust that was betrayed.</p>
<p>Pfizer Inc. is the largest pharmaceutical company in the world by revenue. It is also the company with the largest criminal fine in the history of American corporate law. In 2009, Pfizer agreed to pay $2.3 billion to settle criminal and civil charges related to the fraudulent marketing of multiple drugs. This was not a first offense. It was not an isolated incident. It was the latest in a decades-long pattern of corporate fraud that spans continents, drugs, and regulatory frameworks.</p>
<p>The Confliction album track <a href="https://confliction.zzzdaily.com/tracks/en/51-The_Covenant_Blood.mp4">The Covenant Blood</a> speaks to the sanctity of covenant — the unbreakable bond of truth between parties. A pharmaceutical company entrusted with the health of billions operates under an implicit covenant: that it will be truthful about its products, transparent about risks, and honest in its research. Pfizer has broken this covenant repeatedly, systematically, and profitably. The blood of the covenant demands truth. The blood of Pfizer's corporate history reveals deception.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="the-2009-criminal-settlement-23-billion-in-fraud">The 2009 Criminal Settlement: $2.3 Billion in Fraud</h2>
<p>In September 2009, Pfizer agreed to pay $2.3 billion — the largest healthcare fraud settlement in history at that time — to resolve criminal and civil allegations brought by the U.S. Department of Justice. The settlement encompassed criminal fines, civil penalties, and forfeiture provisions related to the illegal marketing of multiple drugs.</p>
<p>The criminal case centered on Pfizer's promotion of Bextra (valdecoxib), a painkiller that the company marketed for uses not approved by the FDA — a practice known as "off-label marketing." Pfizer promoted Bextra for surgical pain at dosages the FDA had specifically declined to approve due to safety concerns. The company used a sophisticated marketing operation to persuade physicians to prescribe the drug for unapproved uses, including deploying sales representatives, medical liaisons, and paid consultants to promote off-label use.</p>
<p>Pfizer's subsidiary, Pharmacia &#x26; Upjohn Company Inc., pleaded guilty to a felony violation of the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act for misbranding Bextra "with the intent to defraud or mislead." The guilty plea was accompanied by a criminal fine of $1.195 billion — the largest criminal fine ever imposed on any corporation for any crime in U.S. history.</p>
<p>The civil component of the settlement addressed allegations that Pfizer had also illegally promoted three other drugs — Geodon (an antipsychotic), Zyvox (an antibiotic), and Lyrica (a nerve pain medication) — for unapproved uses. The civil penalties totaled an additional $1 billion. And Pfizer entered into a Corporate Integrity Agreement with the Department of Health and Human Services, promising to reform its marketing practices.</p>
<p>But here is the critical detail that most reporting omitted: the $2.3 billion settlement was Pfizer's fourth such settlement in a decade. The company had previously settled fraud allegations in 2002, 2004, and 2007. Each time, Pfizer paid the fine, signed an integrity agreement, and continued the same behavior. The fines were treated not as punishments but as the cost of doing business — a calculated expense weighed against the billions in revenue generated by the fraudulent practices.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="a-pattern-of-fraud-the-complete-record">A Pattern of Fraud: The Complete Record</h2>
<p>The 2009 settlement was not an aberration. It was the most visible manifestation of a corporate culture in which fraud is systematic, institutional, and profitable. The complete record includes:</p>
<p><strong>Trovan (trovafloxacin) — 1996:</strong> During a meningitis epidemic in Kano, Nigeria, Pfizer conducted an experimental trial of its antibiotic Trovan on approximately 200 children. According to lawsuits filed by the Nigerian government and the families of affected children, Pfizer failed to obtain proper informed consent, did not adequately explain the experimental nature of the treatment, and did not provide appropriate medical follow-up. Eleven children died during the trial, and dozens suffered serious injuries including organ failure, brain damage, and paralysis. Pfizer settled with the Nigerian government in 2009 for $75 million — a fraction of the damages alleged.</p>
<p><strong>Warner-Lambert/Neurontin (gabapentin) — 2004:</strong> Pfizer acquired Warner-Lambert in 2000, inheriting the company's massive off-label marketing scheme for Neurontin, a seizure medication. Warner-Lambert had promoted Neurontin for a wide range of unapproved conditions — including bipolar disorder, pain, migraine, and attention deficit disorder — generating billions in off-label sales. The company paid physicians to promote the drug at medical conferences, published ghostwritten articles in medical journals, and suppressed unfavorable research. In 2004, Warner-Lambert (now a Pfizer subsidiary) pleaded guilty to two felony counts and paid $430 million in criminal fines and civil penalties.</p>
<p><strong>Protonix (pantoprazole) — 2013:</strong> Pfizer subsidiary Wyeth (acquired by Pfizer in 2009) agreed to pay $491 million to settle allegations of illegal off-label promotion of Protonix, a proton pump inhibitor. The government alleged that Wyeth had promoted Protonix for unapproved uses and paid kickbacks to healthcare professionals to prescribe the drug.</p>
<p><strong>Celebrex and Bextra — Cardiovascular Risk Concealment:</strong> Pfizer faced allegations that it concealed cardiovascular safety risks associated with its COX-2 inhibitor drugs, Celebrex and Bextra. Internal documents revealed that Pfizer had data suggesting increased cardiovascular risk but continued to market the drugs aggressively. Bextra was eventually withdrawn from the market in 2005.</p>
<p><strong>Chantix (varenicline) — Psychiatric Side Effects:</strong> Pfizer's smoking cessation drug Chantix was linked to serious psychiatric side effects, including suicidal ideation, psychosis, and aggressive behavior. The FDA issued multiple safety warnings, and Pfizer faced thousands of lawsuits from patients who experienced severe psychiatric events. In 2021, Pfizer voluntarily recalled all lots of Chantix due to the presence of nitrosamines — carcinogenic compounds — at levels above acceptable limits.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="whistleblowers-the-people-who-told-the-truth">Whistleblowers: The People Who Told the Truth</h2>
<p>The fraud at Pfizer was not uncovered by regulators. It was uncovered by whistleblowers — employees who witnessed illegal activity and chose to report it, often at enormous personal cost.</p>
<p><strong>John Kopchinski</strong>, a former Pfizer sales representative, was the primary whistleblower in the 2009 Bextra case. Kopchinski reported that Pfizer had instructed its sales force to promote Bextra for uses not approved by the FDA, using scripted talking points designed to steer physicians toward off-label prescribing. When Kopchinski raised concerns internally, he was told to follow orders. He filed a qui tam lawsuit under the False Claims Act and ultimately received $51.5 million as his share of the government's recovery.</p>
<p><strong>Six other whistleblowers</strong> participated in the 2009 case, collectively receiving over $100 million in relator shares. Their testimony revealed a corporate culture in which illegal marketing was not a rogue activity by individual employees but a systematic strategy directed by senior management.</p>
<p><strong>Brook Jackson</strong>, a clinical trial site manager at Ventavia Research Group — a contractor conducting Pfizer's COVID-19 vaccine Phase III trial — filed a complaint with the FDA in September 2020 alleging serious irregularities in the trial conduct. Jackson reported falsified data, broken blinding protocols, inadequately trained vaccinators, slow follow-up on adverse events, and a failure to maintain proper laboratory standards. Jackson was fired by Ventavia on the same day she contacted the FDA. She subsequently filed a False Claims Act lawsuit against Pfizer.</p>
<p>Jackson's allegations are particularly significant because they concern the clinical trial that formed the basis for the Emergency Use Authorization of Pfizer's COVID-19 vaccine — the product administered to billions of people worldwide. If the trial data was compromised at even one of the 153 trial sites, the integrity of the entire dataset is called into question.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="data-manipulation-the-science-of-deception">Data Manipulation: The Science of Deception</h2>
<p>Pfizer's fraud has not been limited to illegal marketing. The company has a documented history of manipulating clinical trial data to make its products appear safer and more effective than they are.</p>
<p>The practice of selective publication — conducting multiple studies and publishing only those with favorable results while suppressing unfavorable studies — has been documented across the pharmaceutical industry, and Pfizer is no exception. A 2008 analysis published in the <em>New England Journal of Medicine</em> by Turner et al. examined FDA reviews of antidepressant trials and found that 94% of published trials showed positive results, while the FDA's own analysis — which included unpublished data — showed that only 51% of all trials were positive. The industry systematically published the good news and buried the bad.</p>
<p>Ghostwriting — the practice of hiring medical writers to produce journal articles that are then attributed to prominent academic physicians — has been documented in Pfizer's marketing of multiple products. Internal documents revealed during litigation showed that Pfizer contracted with medical communication companies to draft articles promoting its drugs, then recruited academic physicians to serve as named authors. The physicians often had minimal involvement in the research or writing but lent their institutional prestige to what were essentially corporate marketing documents disguised as independent scientific publications.</p>
<p>Endpoint manipulation — changing the criteria by which a clinical trial measures success during or after the trial — is another documented practice. By redefining what counts as a positive outcome, companies can transform a failed trial into a successful one. This practice is difficult to detect without access to the original trial protocol, which is why the growing demand for full clinical trial transparency is so important — and so fiercely resisted by the pharmaceutical industry.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="the-covid-19-vaccine-trust-and-verification">The COVID-19 Vaccine: Trust and Verification</h2>
<p>Against this documented history of fraud, the public was asked in 2020 to trust Pfizer with the most consequential pharmaceutical product in modern history — a novel mRNA vaccine to be administered to billions of people worldwide, developed in less than a year, authorized under Emergency Use Authorization, and mandated by governments and employers as a condition of participation in society.</p>
<p>The question is not whether Pfizer is capable of producing a safe and effective product. The question is whether, given its documented history of criminal fraud, data manipulation, safety concealment, and whistleblower retaliation, the public has any rational basis for trusting Pfizer's representations about the safety and efficacy of any product — particularly one developed under unprecedented time pressure, with enormous financial incentives, and under legal immunity from liability.</p>
<p>Pfizer's COVID-19 vaccine generated $36.8 billion in revenue in 2021 and $37.8 billion in 2022 — making it the single most profitable pharmaceutical product in history. The company's stock price soared. Its executives received enormous bonuses. And the entire windfall was predicated on a single claim: that the vaccine was safe and effective.</p>
<p>If that claim proves to be overstated — if the safety data was less robust than represented, if the efficacy waned more rapidly than disclosed, if the adverse event profile was more severe than acknowledged — then the COVID-19 vaccine campaign becomes not merely a public health intervention but the largest pharmaceutical fraud in human history, perpetrated by a company with a documented history of perpetrating exactly this kind of fraud.</p>
<p>The Pfizer documents — the 329,000+ pages of clinical trial data that Pfizer and the FDA attempted to keep sealed for 75 years, before a federal judge ordered their release — are now being analyzed by independent researchers. As documented in our article on <a href="/informed-consent-destroyed/?lang=en">Informed Consent Destroyed</a>, the information contained in these documents raises serious questions about the adequacy of the informed consent provided to vaccine recipients.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="the-liability-shield-no-consequences-for-harm">The Liability Shield: No Consequences for Harm</h2>
<p>Perhaps the most troubling aspect of the Pfizer story is the complete absence of liability for vaccine injuries. Under the PREP Act (Public Readiness and Emergency Preparedness Act) and the National Childhood Vaccine Injury Act (NCVIA), vaccine manufacturers are shielded from lawsuits related to injuries caused by their products.</p>
<p>This liability shield means that even if Pfizer's COVID-19 vaccine causes serious injuries — and the VAERS database, the V-safe system, and numerous international pharmacovigilance databases contain hundreds of thousands of adverse event reports — the company faces no financial liability. Injured individuals cannot sue Pfizer. They can only file claims with the Countermeasures Injury Compensation Program (CICP), which has historically denied the vast majority of claims and provides no mechanism for judicial review of its decisions.</p>
<p>A company with the largest criminal fine in corporate history has been given the most profitable product in pharmaceutical history with zero liability for harm. This is not a market incentive for safety. It is a market incentive for fraud — the same fraud that Pfizer has been convicted of committing repeatedly, across multiple drugs, over multiple decades.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="the-75-year-seal-what-they-did-not-want-you-to-see">The 75-Year Seal: What They Did Not Want You to See</h2>
<p>When a group of scientists and physicians filed a Freedom of Information Act request seeking Pfizer's clinical trial data from the FDA, the agency initially proposed releasing the documents at a rate of 500 pages per month — a schedule that would have taken approximately 75 years to complete. The implication was extraordinary: the FDA was prepared to keep the data underlying the most widely administered pharmaceutical product in history hidden from public view until everyone involved was dead.</p>
<p>Federal Judge Mark Pittman of the Northern District of Texas rejected this timeline, ordering the FDA to produce the documents at a rate of 55,000 pages per month. The resulting document releases have provided independent researchers with an unprecedented view of the clinical trial process — and the findings have been deeply concerning.</p>
<p>The documents revealed that Pfizer identified over 1,200 categories of adverse events during the first three months of the vaccine's deployment. They revealed that the company was aware of waning efficacy earlier than publicly disclosed. They revealed manufacturing quality concerns, including the presence of truncated mRNA species and process-related impurities. And they revealed that the FDA's review process was far less rigorous than the public was led to believe.</p>
<p>As our investigation into <a href="/mrna-shedding-transmission/?lang=en">mRNA Shedding and Transmission</a> documents, Pfizer's own trial protocol anticipated the possibility that the vaccine could be transmitted from vaccinated to unvaccinated individuals — a possibility that was never communicated to the public during the vaccination campaign.</p>
