15:T69b2,<h1 id="organic-farming-under-attack-the-war-on-natural-food">Organic Farming Under Attack: The War on Natural Food</h1>
<h2 id="the-ancient-pattern">The Ancient Pattern</h2>
<blockquote>
<p>"The Lord God took the man and put him in the Garden of Eden to work it and take care of it." — Genesis 2:15</p>
</blockquote>
<p>God's first assignment to humanity was agricultural. Before there were nations, laws, or commerce, there was a garden — and a man tasked with tending it. The work of cultivation was not a punishment but a calling. The land would produce food, and humanity would steward the process with care.</p>
<p>This was the original economy: the soil, the seed, the sun, the rain, and the labor of human hands, all working in harmony under the Creator's design.</p>
<p>What is happening to organic farming today is the systematic destruction of that calling — the replacement of the garden with the factory, the farmer with the corporation, and stewardship with extraction.</p>
<p>The Confliction album track <a href="https://confliction.zzzdaily.com/tracks/en/51-The_Covenant_Blood.mp4">The Covenant Blood</a> speaks to the sacred bond between God and His people — a bond that extends to the land itself. In the Old Testament, the land was part of the covenant. It was to be tended, rested in sabbath years, and never permanently sold. The destruction of organic farming is the breaking of that covenant relationship between humanity and the ground, replaced by a corporate contract that serves profit, not provision.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="the-regulatory-war-on-small-farms">The Regulatory War on Small Farms</h2>
<p>Across the Western world, small and organic farmers face a regulatory environment designed to crush them. The regulations are not written for them — they are written for industrial operations, then applied uniformly in a way that makes compliance impossible for anyone without a corporate legal team and a million-dollar processing facility.</p>
<p>The effect is not accidental. It is the deliberate consolidation of food production into fewer and fewer corporate hands.</p>
<p>In the United States, the Food Safety Modernization Act (FSMA), signed into law in 2011, imposed a sweeping set of regulations on food producers. While large-scale operations could absorb compliance costs, small farmers faced requirements for extensive documentation, hazard analysis plans, third-party audits, and facility upgrades that threatened to bankrupt them.</p>
<p>The Tester-Hagan Amendment provided partial exemptions for farms with less than $500,000 in annual sales, but the regulatory burden remained crushing for operations that lacked dedicated compliance staff.</p>
<p>The European Union's regulatory framework is even more hostile to small producers. EU food safety regulations require standardized processing facilities, approved packaging, and laboratory testing that are manageable for multinational food companies but prohibitively expensive for a farmer selling cheese at a village market.</p>
<p>Traditional food products that had been made safely for centuries — artisanal cheeses, cured meats, fermented vegetables — were suddenly classified as non-compliant because they did not meet industrial processing standards designed for factory production.</p>
<p>In the United Kingdom, the 2021 Environment Act introduced new environmental land management schemes that shifted subsidies away from food production toward "environmental outcomes." Farmers who had been growing food for decades found their subsidies redirected toward rewilding, carbon sequestration, and biodiversity targets — worthy goals in theory, but implemented in a way that made food production economically unviable for small operations.</p>
<p>The consolidation of farming is accelerating. In the United States, the number of farms has declined from 6.8 million in 1935 to approximately 2 million today. The farms that remain are increasingly large: operations with over $1 million in annual sales now account for the majority of agricultural output, while small farms producing less than $50,000 annually make up the majority by number but only a fraction by revenue.</p>
<p>The system rewards scale and punishes smallness. Every regulation, every compliance requirement, every inspection fee pushes the economics further in favor of the industrial operation that can spread these costs across millions of units of production — and further against the small farmer who absorbs the full cost on a few hundred units.</p>
<h2 id="the-fdas-armed-raids">The FDA's Armed Raids</h2>
<p>Perhaps nothing illustrates the war on natural food more dramatically than the FDA's armed raids on small farms and food cooperatives.</p>
<p>In 2010, the FDA conducted a year-long sting operation against Rawesome Foods, a private food cooperative in Venice, California. Armed federal agents raided the cooperative, confiscated raw milk, cheese, and other products, and arrested the operator, James Stewart. His crime: selling raw milk and organic food to willing buyers in a private club arrangement.</p>
<p>In 2011, the FDA raided Amish farmer Dan Allgyer's farm in Kinzers, Pennsylvania, after conducting an undercover investigation that included sending agents to purchase raw milk under false pretenses.</p>
<p>Allgyer was selling raw milk to a buying club in Maryland — a group of families who had specifically sought out his product because they believed raw milk was healthier than the pasteurized, homogenized product available in grocery stores. The FDA obtained a permanent injunction shutting down his interstate sales.</p>
<p>In Wisconsin, farmer Vernon Hershberger was charged with four criminal counts for selling raw milk and other farm products directly to members of a private buying club. The charges included operating a retail food establishment without a license and violating a holding order placed on his food by state agriculture officials.</p>
<p>Hershberger faced up to 30 months in prison and $10,000 in fines for the crime of feeding willing families with food from his own farm.</p>
<p>In Michigan, Mark Baker of Baker's Green Acres was ordered by the Department of Natural Resources to destroy his heritage breed pigs — Mangalitsa and Russian boar crosses — because the state classified them as "invasive species" based on physical characteristics (curly tails, certain color patterns) that the pigs happened to share with feral hogs.</p>
<p>Baker was told to either kill all his pigs or face felony charges carrying a penalty of $700,000 in fines and up to four years in prison. He fought the order in court and eventually prevailed, but only after years of legal battles and tens of thousands of dollars in legal fees.</p>
<p>These are not isolated incidents. They are part of a pattern of selective enforcement that targets small, independent food producers while leaving industrial operations — whose products cause thousands of foodborne illness outbreaks annually — largely unscrutinized.</p>
<p>The statistics are revealing. According to the CDC, the majority of foodborne illness outbreaks in the United States are linked to industrial-scale food production — contaminated lettuce from factory farms, salmonella in mass-produced eggs, E. coli in ground beef from processing plants that handle millions of pounds per day. The scale of industrial production creates the conditions for mass contamination.</p>
<p>Yet enforcement resources flow disproportionately toward small producers. The FDA does not raid Tyson chicken processing plants with armed agents. It does not conduct undercover sting operations against Monsanto. It sends its enforcement resources against Amish farmers and food cooperatives — because the real threat to the industrial food system is not contamination. It is the existence of alternatives.</p>
<p>The message sent by these raids is not about food safety. It is about obedience. Every farmer who sees a neighbor raided by federal agents for selling raw milk understands the implicit threat: comply with the industrial system or face the power of the state.</p>
<h2 id="codex-alimentarius-the-global-food-control-framework">Codex Alimentarius: The Global Food Control Framework</h2>
<p>The Codex Alimentarius Commission, established in 1963 by the World Health Organization and the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations, sets international food standards that are increasingly adopted as binding trade requirements.</p>
<p>While framed as a public health initiative, Codex Alimentarius standards consistently favor industrial food production over traditional and organic methods.</p>
<p>Codex standards on pesticide residues set "acceptable" levels of chemical contamination in food — levels that legitimize the presence of agrochemicals that organic farmers work to eliminate entirely.</p>
<p>Codex standards on nutritional supplements have been used to restrict access to high-dose vitamins and minerals, reclassifying natural health products as drugs that require pharmaceutical-grade regulation.</p>
<p>Codex standards on food labeling have resisted mandatory GMO labeling, protecting the interests of Monsanto and other biotech companies at the expense of consumer information.</p>
<p>The harmonization of global food standards through Codex creates a regulatory floor that organic and traditional producers cannot compete beneath. When a country adopts Codex standards, its domestic food regulations must conform — and conform means industrialize.</p>
<p>Small producers who cannot meet industrial standards are squeezed out. Traditional food cultures that have sustained communities for generations are classified as non-compliant. The diversity and resilience of local food systems are replaced with the uniformity and fragility of global corporate supply chains.</p>
<p>Dr. Rima Laibow, a physician who has studied Codex Alimentarius extensively, has described it as "the single greatest threat to health freedom in the world." The framework does not ban natural food outright. It regulates it into impossibility — setting standards that only industrial producers can meet, then using trade agreements to enforce those standards worldwide.</p>
<p>The World Trade Organization (WTO) enforces Codex standards through its Sanitary and Phytosanitary (SPS) Agreement. Countries that adopt food safety standards stricter than Codex can face trade disputes and sanctions. Countries that adopt standards less strict must justify them scientifically — but "scientific justification" is defined by the same institutional science that the chemical industry funds and controls.</p>
<p>The net effect is a global regulatory ratchet that moves in only one direction: toward industrial standardization and away from traditional, local, and organic food production. The farmer in Ghana who has been making traditional fermented foods for generations finds that her products do not meet Codex processing standards. The cheesemaker in France whose family recipe predates the French Revolution finds that his cellar does not meet EU-mandated temperature and humidity specifications.</p>
<p>The regulations are not protecting consumers. They are eliminating competitors.</p>
<h2 id="the-organic-label-compromise">The Organic Label Compromise</h2>
<p>Even the organic certification system — the primary regulatory framework that distinguishes natural food from chemical agriculture — has been compromised from within.</p>
<p>The USDA National Organic Program (NOP), which administers organic certification in the United States, has progressively weakened organic standards to accommodate industrial interests.</p>
<p>In 2005, a rider attached to an agricultural appropriations bill weakened the organic standard to allow 38 synthetic substances in organic food processing. The Organic Trade Association — the industry group representing corporate organic brands — supported the changes, while independent organic farmers opposed them.</p>
<p>The pattern revealed a fundamental tension within the organic movement: as organic food became a multi-billion dollar industry, the corporate players who entered the market used their lobbying power to reshape the standards in their favor.</p>
<p>Large-scale "organic" operations — massive monoculture farms and confined animal feeding operations (CAFOs) that technically meet organic certification requirements while violating the spirit of organic agriculture — have come to dominate the market.</p>
<p>A 2017 Washington Post investigation revealed that a major organic egg producer, Herbruck's Poultry Ranch, was housing millions of hens in industrial facilities that bore no resemblance to the pastoral image consumers associated with "organic" eggs. The hens had minimal outdoor access, lived in densely packed conditions, and were organic in label only.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, genuine small-scale organic farmers — the producers who actually practice the rotational grazing, soil building, and chemical-free cultivation that organic farming was meant to represent — are increasingly unable to compete with the economies of scale that corporate organic operations enjoy.</p>
<p>The organic label that was created to protect real organic farming has been captured by the same industrial interests it was meant to distinguish from.</p>
<p>Organic fraud is a growing crisis. In 2017, a Washington Post investigation revealed that millions of pounds of soybeans and corn shipped from Turkey and imported as "organic" were in fact conventionally grown — contaminated with glyphosate and other prohibited pesticides. The shipments were worth hundreds of millions of dollars and had entered the USDA organic supply chain through a system with inadequate verification and enforcement.</p>
<p>The fraud extends to organic dairy. A 2022 investigation found that large-scale "organic" dairy operations were importing conventional grain, feeding it to cattle, and selling the milk as organic. Testing of milk from these operations revealed residues of pesticides prohibited under organic standards.</p>
<p>The economic incentive for fraud is enormous. Organic products command premium prices — often 20-100% above conventional equivalents. When the certification system lacks robust enforcement, the premium becomes a magnet for fraud. The consumer pays extra for organic. The fraudulent producer pockets the premium while delivering a conventional product. And the genuine organic farmer, who incurs the real costs of organic production, loses market share to competitors who cheat.</p>
<p>The system that was supposed to distinguish real food from chemical food has been infiltrated by the very interests it was designed to protect against.</p>
<h2 id="the-seed-control-connection">The Seed Control Connection</h2>
<p>The war on organic farming is inseparable from the corporate control of seeds, as documented in our article on <a href="/seed-vault-control-food-future/?lang=en">The Seed Vault: Who Controls the Future of Food?</a>.</p>
<p>Organic farming depends on access to non-GMO, non-patented seeds — seeds that farmers can save, share, and replant without corporate permission.</p>
<p>The consolidation of the global seed industry into a handful of corporations (Bayer-Monsanto, Corteva, Syngenta, and BASF now control over 60% of the global seed market) directly threatens the seed sovereignty that organic farming requires.</p>
<p>Seed laws in many countries restrict or prohibit the sale of non-certified seed varieties — effectively criminalizing the traditional practice of seed saving and exchange that has been the foundation of agriculture for ten thousand years.</p>
<p>In the European Union, seed marketing regulations require registration, distinctness, uniformity, and stability (DUS) testing that favors commercial hybrid and GMO varieties over the diverse, locally adapted heirloom varieties that organic farmers depend on.</p>
<p>The connection between GMO agriculture and the regulatory assault on organic farming is documented in our article on <a href="/gmo-genetic-modification-food/?lang=en">GMO: The Genetic Modification of Your Food Supply</a>.</p>
<p>The same companies that sell patented GMO seeds lobby for regulations that disadvantage the farmers who refuse to use them. The same agencies that approve GMO crops enforce standards that penalize organic alternatives.</p>
<p>The system is designed to funnel all food production through a corporate pipeline — and organic farming is the obstruction that must be removed.</p>
<h2 id="the-right-to-food-freedom">The Right to Food Freedom</h2>
<p>Despite the regulatory onslaught, a growing movement of farmers, consumers, and activists is fighting to preserve the right to produce and consume natural food.</p>
<p>The raw milk movement has expanded across the United States, with numerous states passing laws to legalize direct farm sales. Community Supported Agriculture (CSA) programs connect consumers directly with local farmers, bypassing the corporate supply chain. Seed libraries and seed swaps preserve heirloom varieties outside the corporate seed system.</p>
<p>In Maine, voters passed a constitutional amendment in 2021 recognizing the "right to food" — the first state in the nation to enshrine food sovereignty in its constitution. The amendment states that "all individuals have a natural, inherent, and unalienable right to food, including the right to save and exchange seeds and the right to grow, raise, harvest, produce, and consume the food of their own choosing."</p>
<p>It is a remarkable document — remarkable because it should not have been necessary. The right to grow and eat food should not require a constitutional amendment. That it does reveals how far the regulatory state has gone in restricting the most basic human freedom.</p>
<p>Similar food sovereignty ordinances have been passed in dozens of towns across Maine, Vermont, and other states. These local laws assert the right of communities to regulate their own food systems, independent of state and federal restrictions that were designed for industrial agriculture.</p>
<p>The battle for food freedom is not merely a political issue. It is a spiritual one. The right to grow food, save seeds, and feed one's family without corporate or government permission is a right that derives from the created order itself — from God's command to tend the garden and His provision of seed-bearing plants and animals for food.</p>
<p>The regulatory war on organic farming is, at its deepest level, a war against the freedom God gave to humanity in the beginning.</p>
<h2 id="the-nitrogen-crackdown-and-farm-seizures">The Nitrogen Crackdown and Farm Seizures</h2>
<p>The most aggressive phase of the war on farming is now unfolding under the banner of environmental regulation — specifically, nitrogen reduction mandates that threaten to shut down thousands of farms across the developed world.</p>
<p>In the Netherlands, the government's nitrogen reduction policy — which aims to cut nitrogen emissions by 50% by 2030 — has targeted approximately 3,000 farms for closure. Dutch farmers, many of whom have worked the same land for generations, have been offered buyouts or told their farms will be compulsorily acquired.</p>
<p>The result was the largest farmer protest movement in European history. Dutch farmers blockaded highways with tractors, dumped manure at government buildings, and formed the BoerBurgerBeweging (Farmer-Citizen Movement) political party, which won the most seats in the 2023 provincial elections.</p>
<p>But the policy has not been abandoned. It has been repackaged, delayed, and redistributed — but the objective remains: reduce the number of farms, consolidate agricultural production, and transition the food supply toward corporate-controlled alternatives.</p>
<p>Ireland has proposed culling 200,000 dairy cows to meet climate targets. Canada has implemented fertilizer reduction targets. New Zealand attempted to tax agricultural methane emissions before the policy was reversed under public pressure.</p>
<p>The pattern across all these nations is identical: use environmental regulation to make traditional farming economically unviable, purchase or seize the land from farmers who can no longer operate, and redirect the food supply toward industrial and synthetic alternatives that can be manufactured, patented, and centrally controlled.</p>
<p>The farmers being displaced are not the problem. They are the obstacle — the last decentralized food producers standing between consumers and a fully corporatized food supply.</p>
<h2 id="the-economic-squeeze">The Economic Squeeze</h2>
<p>Beyond direct regulation, organic and small-scale farmers face an economic environment deliberately structured to disadvantage them.</p>
<p>Agricultural subsidies overwhelmingly favor large-scale commodity production. In the United States, the top 10% of farm subsidy recipients receive approximately 78% of all subsidy payments. These payments flow primarily to operations growing corn, soybeans, wheat, cotton, and rice — the commodity crops that feed the industrial food system.</p>
<p>Organic farmers receive minimal subsidy support. Farmers transitioning from conventional to organic must endure a three-year certification period during which they cannot sell their products as organic but must bear the costs of organic production methods — no synthetic pesticides, no synthetic fertilizers, and reduced yields during the soil recovery period.</p>
<p>This transition period is a financial death zone for many farmers. They cannot afford to produce organic food at conventional prices for three years while their soil rebuilds. The certification system that is supposed to support organic farming instead functions as a barrier that prevents farmers from entering it.</p>
<p>Insurance programs are similarly biased. Crop insurance policies are calibrated to conventional production methods and yields. Organic farmers face higher premiums and lower coverage, despite evidence that diversified organic systems are more resilient to weather extremes than monoculture conventional systems.</p>
<p>The entire economic infrastructure of agriculture — subsidies, insurance, credit, commodity markets, processing facilities, and retail distribution — is designed for industrial scale and corporate participation. The small organic farmer operates not just outside this system but against it.</p>
<p>Every organic farm that survives does so in spite of the economic framework, not because of it. And that is by design.</p>
<h2 id="biblical-reflection">Biblical Reflection</h2>
<blockquote>
<p>"He causes the grass to grow for the cattle, and vegetation for the service of man, that he may bring forth food from the earth." — Psalm 104:14</p>
</blockquote>
<p>God designed the earth to produce food. He did not design it to produce food subject to corporate licensing, government approval, and regulatory compliance. The vegetation grows for the service of man — not for the profit of Monsanto or the jurisdiction of the FDA.</p>
<p>When a farmer cannot sell milk from his own cow to his own neighbor without facing criminal prosecution, something has gone fundamentally wrong — not with the farmer, but with the system that prosecutes him.</p>
<p>The prophets spoke of a time when every man would sit under his own vine and fig tree, with none to make him afraid. That vision — of agricultural self-sufficiency, local abundance, and freedom from oppression — is the exact opposite of what the corporate food system is building.</p>
<p>The system wants no vines, no fig trees, no independence, and maximum fear. It wants consumers, not farmers. It wants dependency, not sovereignty. It wants control, not freedom.</p>
<p>The organic farmer who saves seeds, builds soil, refuses chemicals, and feeds families directly from the land is practicing an act of resistance — not merely political resistance, but spiritual resistance against a system that has declared war on the created order.</p>
<p>Every garden planted, every seed saved, every bushel sold at a farmer's market is a declaration that God's design is sufficient and man's replacement is not.</p>
<p>The garden was the beginning. The farm is its continuation. And the farmer who tends the soil according to the Creator's principles — building rather than depleting, nurturing rather than extracting, trusting rather than controlling — is doing the work that was given in Eden.</p>
<p>That work is under attack not because it fails, but because it succeeds. Organic farming works. Regenerative agriculture works. Real food nourishes. And a system built on chemical dependency, corporate control, and processed counterfeits cannot tolerate the existence of an alternative that proves it unnecessary.</p>
